# Draft vs Can vs Bottle

The same IPA can taste different depending on how it reached you. Draft, can, and bottle
each protect — or fail to protect — hop aroma in their own way. Knowing the trade-offs
helps you choose well at the shop and the bar. This note connects [[Carbonation and Packaging]]
with the drinker's experience and pairs with [[IPA Freshness and Shelf Life]].

## What an IPA Needs From Packaging

A hop-forward beer needs its packaging to do three things: **block light** (to prevent
[[Light-Struck Beer and Skunking|skunking]]), **block oxygen** (to slow
[[Hop Fade and Oxidation]]), and **get to the drinker fast**. How each format performs:

| Format | Light protection | Oxygen barrier | Freshness potential | Typical experience |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Draft** | Total (in keg) | Excellent until tapped | Very high if turnover is fast | Fresh, lively, venue-dependent |
| **Can** | Total | Excellent seal | Very high | Reliable, portable, no skunking |
| **Brown bottle** | Partial | Good | Moderate | Traditional; vulnerable to light |
| **Green/clear bottle** | Poor | Good | Lower in practice | High skunking risk — avoid for IPA |

## Draft

Draft beer straight from a well-kept keg can be the **freshest** way to drink an IPA — no
light exposure and minimal oxygen until the keg is tapped. The catch is that quality is
**venue-dependent**: dirty lines, wrong gas pressure, or a slow-moving keg can ruin an
otherwise excellent beer.

> [!warning] Draft is only as good as the line
> Foul or sour notes on a draft pour often mean dirty draft lines, not a bad beer. A
> well-run [[IPA in Bars and Taprooms|taproom]] cleans lines regularly.

## Can

The modern craft default — and for good reason. Cans are an **airtight, fully opaque**
seal that completely blocks light and oxygen. They are light, chill fast, and travel well.
The old "metallic taste" complaint is obsolete: the beer never touches bare aluminum, and
the perceived metal note usually comes from drinking *from* the can.

> [!tip] Always pour the can into a glass
> Drinking from the can starves you of aroma — the whole point of an IPA. Pour into proper
> [[Glassware for IPAs|glassware]] every time.

## Bottle

Bottles are traditional and can perform well in **brown glass**, which filters much of the
damaging light spectrum. **Green and clear bottles offer little protection** and are a
real skunking risk for hoppy beer. Bottles also tend to have slightly more headspace
oxygen than cans.

> [!note] Glass color is a signal
> If you must buy an IPA in a bottle, choose brown glass and check that it has been stored
> out of direct light. See [[Light-Struck Beer and Skunking]].

## Choosing in Practice

For take-home, **cans win** on protection and convenience. At a bar, **fresh draft from a
reputable venue** is hard to beat. Whatever the format, freshness — see
[[IPA Freshness and Shelf Life]] — and a proper [[Serving Temperature|temperature]] matter
more than the package itself.

> [!example] A simple rule
> Buy cans, drink fresh draft out, pour everything into a glass, and avoid green-bottle
> IPAs. That covers ninety percent of good packaging decisions.
