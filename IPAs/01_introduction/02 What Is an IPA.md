# What Is an IPA?

**IPA** stands for **India Pale Ale** — a hop-forward style of [[Origins of Pale Ale|pale ale]] characterized by pronounced bitterness, hop flavor, and hop aroma, typically
balanced over a relatively light, dry malt base. It is the best-selling craft beer
style in the United States and the defining beer of the modern craft movement.

## The Defining Characteristics

An IPA is recognizable by four traits, all of which trace back to [[Hops]]:

1. **Bitterness** — measured in [[IBU and Perceived Bitterness|IBU]], from a
   restrained ~40 in a [[Session IPA]] to 100+ in a [[Double IPA]].
2. **Hop aroma & flavor** — citrus, pine, tropical fruit, resin, floral, and
   dank notes, driven by [[Hop Oils and Terpenes]] and [[Thiols and Hop Burst|thiols]].
3. **A supporting malt base** — usually pale and dry, designed to let hops lead.
   See [[Base Malts]].
4. **Moderate-to-high strength** — most sit between 5.5% and 7.5% ABV, though the
   style spans [[Session IPA|3.5%]] to [[Triple IPA|11%+]].

## What an IPA Is *Not*

- Not simply "a strong beer" — strength is incidental; **hops** are the point.
- Not always bitter — a [[New England IPA]] can be aromatic yet mild in bitterness.
- Not a single recipe — the IPA is a *family* of styles. See the [[IPA Family Tree]].

## Where the Name Comes From

The "India" in India Pale Ale reflects the style's (heavily mythologized) association
with British beer exported to colonial India in the 1800s. The popular story — that
extra hops and alcohol were added to survive the sea voyage — is largely a
**legend**. The real history is more interesting and is told in
[[The October Beer Myth]] and [[Hodgson and the East India Trade]].

## A Style in Constant Motion

No beer style has evolved faster. The IPA has, in roughly two centuries, gone from a
[[English IPA|British pale ale]] to a [[West Coast IPA|bitter American showcase]] to a
[[New England IPA|soft and hazy juice bomb]] — and continues to spin off novelties
like the [[Brut IPA]], [[Cold IPA]], and [[Milkshake IPA]]. Understanding the IPA
means understanding a moving target.
