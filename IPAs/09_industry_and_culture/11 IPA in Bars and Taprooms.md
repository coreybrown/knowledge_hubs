# IPA in Bars and Taprooms

For most modern breweries, the **taproom** is not a side project; it is the business. And for most drinkers, a fresh draft IPA at a bar or brewery is the style at its best. The on-premise channel is where the IPA's commerce and culture meet.

## The Taproom Model

A taproom is a brewery's own on-site bar, selling its beer directly to the public. It has become central to craft economics:

> [!example] Why breweries love taprooms
> Selling a pint in your own taproom captures the **full retail margin**: no [[Beer Distribution and the Three-Tier System|distributor]] or third-party retailer taking a cut. It is, per ounce, by far the most profitable way to sell beer, as detailed in [[The Business of Brewing an IPA]].

Taprooms also deliver benefits money cannot easily buy: a direct relationship with customers, immediate feedback, brand experience, and total control over how fresh the beer is when it reaches the glass.

## The Freshness Advantage

The IPA is perishable. In a taproom, an IPA can be served **days from packaging or straight from the bright tank**, capturing the [[Hop Aroma Compounds|hop aroma]] before [[Hop Fade and Oxidation|oxidation]] dulls it. No distribution channel can match that. This is the strongest practical argument for drinking IPA on-premise, reinforcing the points in [[IPA Freshness and Shelf Life]].

## Draft Lines and Service

On-premise IPA quality depends on details invisible to the drinker:

| Factor | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Line cleaning | Dirty draft lines breed [[Off-Flavors in IPA\|off-flavors]] |
| Line temperature | Warm lines cause foaming and flat pours |
| Tap rotation | Slow-moving handles let IPA go stale |
| [[Glassware for IPAs\|Glassware]] | Clean, correct glass protects [[Beer Flavor Wheel\|aroma]] and head |
| Pour pressure | Wrong CO2 balance ruins [[Carbonation and Packaging\|carbonation]] |

> [!warning] A great IPA can be ruined at the tap
> Even a perfectly brewed IPA arrives lifeless through dirty lines or a warm faucet. On-premise quality is a *service* problem as much as a brewing one. That is why the [[Draft vs Can vs Bottle|draft vs can]] question has no universal answer.

## On-Premise Culture

The taproom reshaped how people *socialize* around beer. The modern craft taproom (communal tables, no TVs, often family- and dog-friendly, frequently hosting food trucks rather than a kitchen) is a distinct social space. It is where [[The Hype Beer Phenomenon|hype releases]] are launched, where [[IPA Festivals and Competitions|festival]] culture lives, and where regional [[Regional Drinking Cultures|drinking cultures]] take shape.

## Tap Handles as Battleground

In traditional bars, **tap handle space is finite and contested**. With IPA the dominant craft style, breweries compete fiercely for those handles. Securing a tap line at a popular bar is a marketing victory, and losing one to a competitor is a real commercial blow, tied directly to [[IPA Branding and Label Art|brand strength]].
