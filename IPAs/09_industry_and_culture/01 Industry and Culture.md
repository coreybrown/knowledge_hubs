# Industry and Culture

The IPA is not only a recipe and a flavor profile. It is an **economic engine** and a **cultural phenomenon**. This domain maps the world *around* the beer: the breweries that build their identity on it, the people who shaped it, the supply chains that feed it, the laws that move it, and the fan culture that elevated it from a beverage to an obsession.

> [!note] What this domain covers
> If [[IPA Styles]] and the [[Brewing Guide]] explain *what an IPA is and how it's made*, the Industry & Culture domain explains *why it dominates the market and how it reaches your glass*.

## Why Industry and Culture Matter

A homebrewer can ignore distribution law. A commercial brewer cannot. The IPA's commercial dominance (well over a third of the craft category) means that **business decisions shape what beer is even available to drink**. Hop contracts signed three years out determine which [[Hop Variety Index|varieties]] appear in next year's releases. The [[Beer Distribution and the Three-Tier System|three-tier system]] determines whether a beloved regional [[New England IPA]] can ever cross state lines.

> [!tip] Read alongside the history domain
> The [[The American Craft Beer Revolution]] is the bridge between [[History of IPAs|history]] and industry. Many "industry" stories began as historical turning points.

Culture, meanwhile, is a feedback loop. [[Untappd and Beer Rating Culture|Rating apps]], [[The Hype Beer Phenomenon|hype releases]], and social media have changed *what breweries brew*, pushing the style toward ever-softer, fruitier, more [[Modern IPA Diversification|novelty-driven]] expressions.

## A Maturing Category

After two decades of explosive growth, the craft segment has plateaued. The story of the IPA's industry is now less about expansion and more about **consolidation, differentiation, and survival**, themes explored throughout this domain and forecast in [[The Future of IPA]].
