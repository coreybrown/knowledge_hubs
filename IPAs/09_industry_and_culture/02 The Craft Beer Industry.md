# The Craft Beer Industry

The **craft beer industry** is the commercial ecosystem of small, independent breweries that emerged in the late 20th century as an alternative to mass-market industrial lager. The IPA is its flagship, and understanding the industry's shape explains why.

## Defining "Craft"

In the United States, the Brewers Association defines a craft brewer by three criteria: **small** (under 6 million barrels annually), **independent** (less than 25% owned by a non-craft producer), and traditional in its brewing. The definition is contested at the edges, but it frames a distinct market segment born from the [[The American Craft Beer Revolution|craft beer revolution]].

## Size and Structure

At its peak the US craft segment grew to roughly **9,000+ breweries**, more breweries than at any point in American history. The structure is a steep pyramid:

| Tier | Description | Volume share |
|---|---|---|
| Regional craft | Large independents (Sierra Nevada, New Belgium) | Majority of craft volume |
| Microbreweries | Mid-size, multi-state distribution | Moderate |
| Brewpubs & taprooms | Sell mostly on-premise | Small per unit, large in count |

Most breweries are *tiny*: the typical American brewery produces only a few thousand barrels a year and relies on its [[IPA in Bars and Taprooms|taproom]].

## IPA's Dominant Share

> [!example] The IPA's market position
> The IPA is consistently the **single best-selling craft style**, accounting for well over a third of craft volume, which is more than the next several styles combined. Many breweries derive the *majority* of their revenue from IPAs alone.

This dominance is self-reinforcing: distributors want IPAs, retailers stock IPAs, and so new breweries brew IPAs. The style's hold is documented in [[IPA Beer Statistics and Data]].

## Growth and the Plateau

The industry's history has two acts:

1. **Explosive growth (roughly 2005–2017)**: double-digit annual volume gains, a brewery opening nearly every day.
2. **The plateau (2018–present)**: volume growth slowing to low single digits or flat, brewery closures rising to match openings.

> [!warning] A maturing, crowded market
> Craft beer is now a **mature category**. Competition for shelf space and tap handles is fierce, input costs have risen, and the broader "beyond beer" trend (seltzer, cocktails, [[The Future of IPA|non-alcoholic options]]) pressures the segment. Survival increasingly depends on brand strength and local loyalty.

## Consolidation Pressure

Big brewing conglomerates have acquired numerous formerly independent craft brands, blurring the line consumers once trusted. This has spurred "independence" marketing campaigns and tighter scrutiny of ownership, themes that intersect with [[IPA Branding and Label Art|branding]] and [[Landmark IPA Breweries|brewery identity]].
