# Key Figures in IPA History

Styles are made by people. Behind every [[Landmark IPA Breweries|landmark brewery]] is an individual (usually a stubborn, technically obsessed one) who made a decision that bent the IPA's trajectory.

> [!note] People are plain text
> The individuals below are described in prose. Only the breweries, beers, and concepts are wikilinked, per the vault's convention.

## The Revivalists

The IPA could not have a modern history without the people who rescued American brewing from near-total industrialization, the story told in [[The American Craft Beer Revolution]].

| Figure | Contribution |
|---|---|
| Fritz Maytag | Bought and saved Anchor Brewing; created [[Anchor Liberty Ale and the First Modern IPA\|Liberty Ale]], the dry-hopped proto-IPA |
| Jack McAuliffe | Founded New Albion, the first modern American microbrewery |
| Ken Grossman | Founded Sierra Nevada; made [[Cascade]]-driven hoppy beer a national product |

## The West Coast Bitterness Era

The [[Rise of the West Coast IPA]] was driven by brewers who treated hops as the entire point of the beer:

- **Vinnie Cilurzo**: widely credited with brewing the first [[Double IPA]] (at Blind Pig) and later creating Pliny the Elder at Russian River. He effectively *invented a substyle*.
- **Greg Koch and Steve Wagner**: co-founded Stone Brewing and turned aggressive bitterness into a brand attitude.
- **Bert Grant**: an outspoken hop evangelist credited with the first modern American IPA.

> [!example] The Cilurzo benchmark
> Vinnie Cilurzo's work is the connective tissue between [[Origins of the Double IPA]], [[Triple IPA]], and modern hop-forward brewing. Few individuals can claim to have launched a category.

## The New England Innovators

The soft, hazy [[The New England IPA Emergence|NEIPA]] came from a different sensibility:

- **John Kimmich**: founder of The Alchemist; Heady Topper redefined what an IPA could look and feel like and pioneered the can-only, hyper-fresh model.
- **Nate Lanier and Shaun Hill**: East Coast brewers who advanced the haze movement and the [[The Hype Beer Phenomenon|allocation culture]] around it.

## The Communicators and Globalizers

Some figures shaped the IPA through writing and marketing rather than mash tuns:

- **Michael Jackson** (the beer writer): documented and dignified beer styles, influencing how the world understood [[IPA Styles]].
- **James Watt and Martin Dickie**: BrewDog's founders, who used confrontational [[IPA Branding and Label Art|branding]] to drive the [[Globalization of IPA]].

> [!tip] A pattern worth noticing
> The recurring profile is an obsessive who ignored conventional wisdom by adding "too many" hops, refusing to filter, or canning a beer no one else would. Innovation in IPA has consistently come from the edges.
