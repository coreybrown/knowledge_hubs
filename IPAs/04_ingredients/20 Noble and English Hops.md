# Noble and English Hops

Before the citrus explosion of the American [[Hops|hop]] industry, brewing ran on a small set of **traditional European varieties** — the German and Czech "noble" hops and the classic English hops. They are the genetic and stylistic ancestors of every modern hop and remain essential to the [[English IPA]].

## The Noble Hops

"**Noble**" is a traditional designation for four refined Continental aroma hops, prized for low bitterness, high humulene, and a delicate herbal-spicy character.

| Hop | Origin | Alpha % | Character |
|-----|--------|---------|-----------|
| Saaz | Czech Republic (Žatec) | ~3–4.5% | Delicate, spicy, herbal — the classic Pilsner hop |
| Hallertau Mittelfrüh | Germany (Hallertau) | ~3–5.5% | Floral, herbal, mildly spicy |
| Tettnang | Germany (Tettnang) | ~3.5–5.5% | Fine, herbal, slightly fruity |
| Spalt | Germany (Spalt) | ~3–5% | Soft, spicy, refined |

## The English Hops

England's hops are earthier and more rustic — the taste of the original [[English IPA]] and of [[Burton-on-Trent and Burton Pale Ale|Burton pale ale]].

| Hop | Alpha % | Character |
|-----|---------|-----------|
| East Kent Goldings | ~4–6% | Sweet, floral, honey, marmalade — the classic English aroma hop |
| Fuggle | ~3.5–5.5% | Earthy, woody, minty, mild |
| Challenger | ~6–9% | Spicy, cedar — a versatile dual-purpose hop |
| Target | ~9–12% | Higher-alpha, intense, sometimes used for bittering |

## Character and Chemistry

Noble and English hops share a defining trait: a **high humulene-to-myrcene ratio** and very low alpha acid. This gives the gentle, herbal, spicy, earthy aroma that [[Hop Oils and Terpenes|terpene chemistry]] predicts — the opposite of the myrcene-heavy citrus blast of [[Citra]] or [[Galaxy]].

> [!note] The ancestors of everything
> These traditional hops appear in the parentage of nearly every modern variety. [[Cascade]] descends from Fuggle; [[Citra]] and [[Centennial]] carry Hallertau and East Kent Golding in their pedigrees. Modern hops are, genetically, the children of these classics.

## Use in IPA

In a traditional [[English IPA]], English hops do everything — bittering, flavor, and aroma — yielding the style's earthy, marmalade-like restraint. In modern brewing, noble and English hops are mostly used as **accent and balancing hops**, adding herbal complexity beneath flashier American and Southern Hemisphere varieties.

> [!tip] A grounding note
> A small charge of East Kent Goldings or Hallertau in a modern hop bill adds an "old-world" floral-herbal depth that pure tropical recipes lack — a way to give a [[New England IPA]] or [[West Coast IPA]] a subtle backbone.
