# Cascade

**Cascade** is the hop that started it all. Released in 1972, it gave American craft brewing its first distinctive flavor signature — a bright grapefruit citrus utterly unlike anything in European brewing — and made the modern [[What Is an IPA|IPA]] possible.

## Variety Statistics

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Alpha acid | ~4.5–7% |
| Origin | USA (Oregon State University / USDA program) |
| Year released | 1972 |
| Breeding | Seedling of Fuggle and a Russian Serebrianka hop |
| Key oils | Myrcene-forward; balanced humulene and farnesene |
| Flavor descriptors | Grapefruit, floral, citrus, mild spice, pine |
| Typical styles | [[American IPA]], [[West Coast IPA]], [[English IPA]], pale ale, [[Session IPA]] |

## The Craft Pioneer

Cascade's place in history is secured by **Sierra Nevada Pale Ale** (1980) and **Anchor Liberty Ale** (1975), the beers that taught American drinkers what citrusy hops tasted like. See [[Anchor Liberty Ale and the First Modern IPA]] and [[The American Craft Beer Revolution]]. For two decades it was the defining aroma of American craft beer.

> [!note] The original "C-hop"
> Cascade is the founding member of the American "C-hops" — joined by [[Centennial]] and later [[Citra]]. Its grapefruit-and-floral profile is the reference point against which later varieties are measured.

## Character

By modern standards Cascade is **moderate** — lower in alpha acid and gentler in aroma than today's tropical powerhouses. Its character is clean grapefruit, soft floral notes, and a hint of spice. This restraint, once cutting-edge, now reads as classic and balanced.

## Use in IPA

A **dual-purpose** hop, Cascade works across the boil but shines as a late and dry-hop addition. It remains a staple of the traditional [[West Coast IPA]] and is a reliable, widely available, relatively affordable choice — unlike the contracted proprietary hops in [[Hop Contracts and the Hop Supply Chain]].

> [!tip] Not obsolete — foundational
> Brewers chasing the newest thiol bombs sometimes overlook Cascade, but it remains essential for retro West Coast recipes and as a balancing hop that adds brightness without overwhelming.
