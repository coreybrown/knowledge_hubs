# Specialty Malts and Adjuncts

If [[Base Malts]] are the foundation of an [[What Is an IPA|IPA]], **specialty malts and adjuncts** are the seasoning and the texture. Used in small to moderate proportions, they add color, body, mouthfeel, and flavor accents. In the modern [[New England IPA]], they define the style itself.

## Specialty Malts

Specialty malts are [[Malt|malts]] taken further in processing: kilned hotter, or roasted, or stewed wet to develop caramel. They are mostly **non-enzymatic**, so they ride on the base malt's converting power.

| Malt | Adds | Notes |
|------|------|-------|
| Crystal / Caramel | Caramel, toffee, color, body | Sparing use in modern IPA; key to old-school [[English IPA]] and [[Red IPA]] |
| Munich | Rich malty depth, golden color | Adds backbone without heavy sweetness |
| Victory / biscuit | Toasted, bready notes | Light accent malt |
| Chocolate / roasted | Coffee, dark color | Defines the [[Black IPA]] |
| Acidulated | Lowers mash pH | A [[Water Treatment for Brewing|water chemistry]] tool |

> [!warning] The crystal malt debate
> Heavy **crystal malt** was once standard in American IPA, but its toffee sweetness can clash with bright modern hops and is blamed for a "cloying" quality in aged beer. Most contemporary [[West Coast IPA|West Coast]] and hazy recipes use little or none, a major shift from the 1990s.

## Adjuncts

Adjuncts are non-malt (or minimally modified) fermentables and additives. In IPA they are mostly about **texture and drinkability**.

| Adjunct | Role |
|---------|------|
| Flaked / malted **wheat** | Haze, soft body, foam; core to [[New England IPA]] |
| Flaked / malted **oats** | Silky, pillowy mouthfeel; the hallmark of hazy IPA |
| **Dextrose** (corn sugar) | Dries the body and boosts ABV cleanly, as used in [[Double IPA]] |
| **Lactose** | Unfermentable milk sugar; sweetness and body in [[Milkshake IPA]] |
| Rye | Spicy, dry character in a [[Rye IPA]] |

> [!example] How adjuncts built the hazy
> The soft, juicy mouthfeel of a [[New England IPA]] is not from hops. It is **oats and wheat**. Often 20–40% of the grist, these adjuncts create the protein-rich, full body and stable haze the style is famous for. See [[Hop Haze and Colloidal Stability]].

## Using Specialty Ingredients in IPA

> [!tip] Restraint is the modern rule
> The trend in IPA brewing is toward **fewer, more purposeful** specialty ingredients. A West Coast IPA may use just a touch of Munich or none at all; a hazy commits hard to oats and wheat. The question is always: does this serve the hops, or distract from them?
