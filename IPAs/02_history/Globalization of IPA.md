# Globalization of IPA

The IPA crossed the world once already, in the 19th century, on the trade routes of [[IPA in the British Empire|the British Empire]]. Its second global spread — the **craft IPA**, from the 1990s onward — has been faster, broader, and more transformative than the first.

## The American Style Goes Abroad

The hop-forward American IPA, born in [[The American Craft Beer Revolution|the craft revolution]], became the **export template** of a worldwide craft beer movement. As local brewers in country after country discovered American craft beer, the IPA — bold, distinctive, and unmistakably *not* mass-market lager — was the style they most often adopted first.

| Region | How IPA took hold |
|---|---|
| **United Kingdom** | The American IPA returned to its ancestral home, helping spark the UK craft scene; BrewDog became a global symbol |
| **Scandinavia** | "Gypsy"/contract brewers like Mikkeller spread hoppy beer across Europe |
| **Continental Europe** | Italy, Spain, Germany, and others built craft scenes with IPA at the centre |
| **Australia & New Zealand** | Local hops ([[Galaxy]], [[Nelson Sauvin]]) made the region a hop powerhouse |
| **Latin America & Asia** | Fast-growing craft scenes in Brazil, Mexico, Japan, China and beyond |

> [!example] The IPA boomerang
> The most striking story is Britain's: the style its breweries once *exported to the Empire*, then *let decline*, came back as an **American reinvention** and reignited British craft brewing. See [[Decline of IPA in Britain]].

## The Global Hop Trade

Globalization is not just about where IPA is *drunk* — it reshaped where its key ingredient is *grown* and *moved*. The modern IPA depends on an international [[Hop Contracts and the Hop Supply Chain|hop supply chain]]:

- **Growing regions** now span the US Pacific Northwest, Germany, the UK, Australia, New Zealand, and beyond — see [[Hop Growing Regions]].
- A brewer in any country can build an IPA from [[Citra|American]], [[Galaxy|Australian]], and [[Nelson Sauvin|New Zealand]] hops in a single recipe.
- [[Hop Products and Formats|Pellets, extracts, and cryo formats]] made hops stable and shippable worldwide.

> [!note] Terroir goes global
> Just as wine grapes express place, hops express [[Hop Terroir|terroir]]. Globalization let brewers everywhere blend the terroir of multiple continents into one glass — a genuinely new possibility in beer history.

## Regional Adaptation

The IPA did not spread as a fixed recipe; each scene adapted it. Brewers reach for **local hops**, local water, and local taste. The result is a patchwork of regional interpretations rather than one global IPA — and that variety feeds the next chapter, [[Modern IPA Diversification]]. For a tour of the results, see [[Best IPAs by Region - Europe]] and [[Best IPAs by Region - Rest of World]].

## Why It Spread So Far, So Fast

Three factors explain the speed:

1. **A ready-made template.** Newcomer brewers had a proven, exciting style to copy.
2. **The internet.** Recipes, techniques, and [[Untappd and Beer Rating Culture|ratings culture]] crossed borders instantly.
3. **Cultural meaning.** Worldwide, the IPA signalled *craft, independence, and flavor* against industrial lager — the same meaning it carried in America.
