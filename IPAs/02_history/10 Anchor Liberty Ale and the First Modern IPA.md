# Anchor Liberty Ale and the First Modern IPA

If the modern IPA has a single point of origin, most historians place it in **1975**, in San Francisco, with one beer: **Anchor Liberty Ale**. It was not labelled an "IPA" — but it established the template that every American IPA has followed since.

## The Beer

Liberty Ale was brewed by **Anchor Brewing** under [[The American Craft Beer Revolution|Fritz Maytag]] to commemorate the 200th anniversary of Paul Revere's ride (1775 → 1975). It was a pale, assertive ale that did two things almost no contemporary American beer was doing:

| Feature | Why it was revolutionary |
|---|---|
| Brewed entirely with **Cascade hops** | [[Cascade]] was a brand-new American variety with bold citrus-floral character |
| **Dry-hopped** | Whole-cone hops added after fermentation for pure aroma — a revived English technique |
| Pale, dry, all-malt base | Let the hops lead, with no adjunct or heavy malt to muddy them |

> [!note] The Cascade moment
> [[Cascade]], released by the USDA breeding program in 1972, gave American brewers a hop unlike anything English. Liberty Ale was its showcase. The grapefruit-and-pine signature of the American IPA — see [[Hop Oils and Terpenes]] — effectively begins here.

## Why "the First Modern IPA"?

Liberty Ale was sold simply as "ale." The term "IPA" was attached later, retrospectively. But in its **construction**, it is unmistakably the prototype:

- Pale, lean malt base built as a stage for hops.
- Aggressive American hop flavor and aroma.
- [[Dry Hopping]] used deliberately for aroma intensity.

Every defining trait of the [[American IPA]] is present. What changed afterward was *degree* — more hops, more bitterness, higher strength — not *concept*.

> [!example] Prototype, not a fully formed style
> Liberty Ale is the **missing link**: more hop-forward than an English pale ale, less extreme than a 1990s [[West Coast IPA]]. It is to the IPA roughly what an early ancestor is to a modern species — recognisably the same lineage, visibly earlier on the branch.

## The Dry-Hopping Revival

[[Dry Hopping]] — adding hops after the boil for aroma without bitterness — was an old English keeping-ale practice that had largely fallen out of American use. Anchor's deliberate dry hopping of Liberty Ale reintroduced the technique to American craft brewing. It is now utterly central to the IPA, from the [[West Coast IPA]] to the [[Double Dry Hopping|double-dry-hopped]] [[New England IPA]].

## Legacy

Liberty Ale demonstrated three things that the entire craft IPA movement would build on:

1. A single, characterful American hop could **define** a beer.
2. **Aroma** — not just bitterness — was worth chasing.
3. American drinkers would **embrace** an intensely hoppy beer.

Sierra Nevada Pale Ale (1980), another Cascade-driven landmark, soon reinforced the lesson. From there the path runs straight to [[Rise of the West Coast IPA]]. Liberty Ale earns its place among the [[Iconic IPAs That Defined the Style]].
