🔬Science & Sensory

Tasting and Evaluating IPAs

2 min read·441 words
sensorytastingevaluationmethod

To evaluate an IPA is to taste it deliberately and systematically — assessing it against a standard rather than simply enjoying it. This note describes a structured method used by judges, brewers, and serious enthusiasts, built on the vocabulary of the Beer Flavor Wheel.

#Tasting vs. Drinking

ℹA different mode of attention

How to Taste an IPA introduces tasting for pleasure. Evaluation adds rigor: a fixed sequence, a vocabulary, an awareness of bias, and a judgment against style expectations like those in BJCP and Style Guidelines.

#The Evaluation Sequence

Always assess in the same order — appearance, then aroma, then flavor, then mouthfeel, then overall — so nothing is skipped.

StepWhat to assessIPA-specific notes
AppearanceColor, clarity, headBrilliant clarity (West Coast IPA) vs. stable haze (New England IPA)
AromaIntensity, descriptors, faultsCitrus, pine, tropical — see Hop Aroma Compounds
FlavorMalt/hop balance, bitterness, finishWhere [[IBU and Perceived Bitternessperceived bitterness]] is judged
MouthfeelBody, carbonation, astringencyThe "pillowy" axis — The Science of Mouthfeel
OverallBalance, drinkability, faultsDoes it match its style?

#Aroma: Get It Right First

Aroma is the most fragile and most informative dimension. Swirl gently to release volatiles, then take short sniffs. Use both orthonasal smell (through the nose) and, while drinking, retronasal smell (aroma rising from the throat) — the latter carries much of perceived "flavor."

✦Smell before the aroma fades

Thiols and light terpenes dissipate fast in the glass. Evaluate aroma within the first minute or two.

#Controlling for Bias

Rigorous evaluation actively fights perceptual bias:

  • Temperature — serve at the correct Serving Temperature; cold mutes aroma and flattens faults.
  • Glassware — a consistent, aroma-concentrating glass; see Glassware for IPAs.
  • Order effects — palate fatigue builds; rotate samples, rinse, rest.
  • Expectation bias — label, brewery reputation, and hype distort judgment. Serious evaluation is done blind.
  • Freshness — note the packaged-on date; staleness (see Hop Fade and Oxidation) is not the recipe's fault.
◆A structured flight

Tasting four West Coast IPAs blind, in identical glasses, at 8 °C, scoring each on the same sheet — that is evaluation. Building such a lineup is covered in Building an IPA Tasting Flight.

#Scoring Against a Standard

Formal evaluation ends in a judgment: does the beer meet its style? Compare against BJCP and Style Guidelines and the IPA Style Comparison Table. A technically flawless beer can still score poorly if it does not represent its declared style.
