# Brut IPA

The **Brut IPA** is the driest beer in the IPA family: a bone-dry,
ultra-pale, highly carbonated style modeled on the crispness of brut
champagne. Borrowing its name from the wine world's driest designation, it
strips out nearly all residual sugar to leave hop aroma floating over a
finish of almost nothing at all.

> [!example] Quick stats
> | | |
> |---|---|
> | **ABV** | 6.0–7.5% |
> | **IBU** | 20–35 (low) |
> | **Color** | 2–5 SRM (very pale straw) |
> | **Key hops** | Aroma-forward: [[Citra]], [[Mosaic]], floral varieties |
> | **Glassware** | Flute, tulip, or wine glass |

## Origins

The Brut IPA was created in late 2017 by brewer Kim Sturdavant at San
Francisco's Social Kitchen & Brewery. It spread quickly as a 2018 trend before
settling into a niche, a textbook entry in [[Modern IPA Diversification]].

## The Enzyme That Defines It

The Brut IPA's signature dryness comes from **amyloglucosidase**, an enzyme
added during fermentation. Ordinary brewing yeast cannot ferment complex
dextrins; amyloglucosidase breaks those dextrins down into fully fermentable
simple sugars, driving the beer to near-zero residual sugar and a final gravity
that can dip below 1.000. See [[Fermentation]] and [[Recipe Formulation]].

> [!warning] Hop creep's close relative
> The same dextrin-cleaving effect underlies [[Hop Creep and Refermentation|hop creep]].
> A Brut IPA deliberately harnesses what brewers of other styles fight to avoid:
> over-attenuation and extra CO2.

## Sensory Profile

| Element | Character |
|---|---|
| Appearance | Very pale, often brilliantly clear, lively bubbles |
| Aroma | Delicate floral, white wine, light citrus and tropical |
| Bitterness | Low; the style avoids harshness |
| Mouthfeel | Effervescent, thin, spritzy (see [[The Science of Mouthfeel]]) |
| Finish | Bone-dry, leaving almost no trace |

## Why It Faded

The Brut IPA's extreme dryness is also its weakness: with no malt sweetness and
low bitterness, there is little to mask hop fade or any flaw. As hop aroma
oxidizes the beer can taste hollow; see [[Hop Fade and Oxidation]]. It remains
a respected but minor member of the [[Specialty and Experimental IPAs]] family.
