# Rye IPA

The **Rye IPA** — sometimes written *RyePA* — is an [[American IPA]] in which a
portion of the grain bill is replaced with rye malt. Rye adds a distinctive
peppery, grainy spice and a slick mouthfeel, giving the beer an extra dimension
of dryness and complexity that pairs naturally with citrus and resinous hops.

> [!example] Quick stats
> | | |
> |---|---|
> | **ABV** | 5.5–8.0% |
> | **IBU** | 50–90 |
> | **Color** | 7–14 SRM (gold to light amber) |
> | **Key hops** | Citrus-pine American — [[Simcoe]], [[Centennial]], [[Cascade]] |
> | **Glassware** | IPA glass or pint |

## What Rye Contributes

Rye is the defining ingredient — see [[Specialty Malts and Adjuncts]]. In
modest proportions (typically 10–25% of the grist) it brings:

- **Spice** — a dry, peppery, faintly earthy grain note.
- **Mouthfeel** — a characteristic silky, slick texture; see
  [[The Science of Mouthfeel]].
- **Color and complexity** — a slightly deeper hue and a drier finish.

> [!warning] The stuck-mash hazard
> Rye has no husk and is rich in gummy beta-glucans. Too much rye can produce a
> sticky, slow-running mash — see [[Lautering and Sparging]]. Rice hulls and a
> careful [[Mashing|mash schedule]] keep the lauter moving.

## Why Rye and Hops Work Together

Rye's peppery dryness amplifies the perception of hop bitterness and accentuates
spicy, resinous hop notes. It pushes the beer toward a leaner, more savory
profile than a malt-sweet [[Red IPA]] — making rye a popular partner for
pine-and-citrus hop bills.

## Sensory Profile

| Element | Character |
|---|---|
| Aroma | Citrus and pine hops with a grainy edge |
| Flavor | Hop-forward, dry, with peppery rye spice |
| Bitterness | Firm, sharpened by rye's dryness |
| Mouthfeel | Slick, medium, smooth |
| Finish | Notably dry and spicy |

## Place in the Family

The Rye IPA is one of the IPA family's *grain-forward* variations, a sibling to
the [[Red IPA]] and the [[Black IPA]] — each defined less by yeast or
fermentation than by a distinctive [[Malt]] choice.
