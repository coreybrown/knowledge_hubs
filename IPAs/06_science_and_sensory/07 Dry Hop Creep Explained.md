# Dry Hop Creep Explained

**Hop creep** is an unwanted refermentation triggered by [[Dry Hopping|dry hops]].
It can quietly push a finished IPA drier, stronger, and — most dangerously — over-
carbonated, sometimes to the point of bursting cans. It is one of the most important
process-safety topics in modern hop-forward brewing.

## The Surprising Cause: Hops Contain Enzymes

For most of brewing history, dry hopping was assumed to be chemically inert — just
aroma extraction. Research in the 2010s revealed otherwise: **hop cones carry
amylolytic (diastatic) enzymes**, principally **amylases** and **glucoamylase**.

These enzymes do exactly what mash enzymes do — they break down **dextrins**, the
long, normally unfermentable sugars left over after [[Mashing]]. The result is a
fresh supply of simple, fermentable sugar appearing in a beer that was supposed to
be finished.

> [!note] "Creep" in one sentence
> Dry hops add enzymes that chop unfermentable dextrins into fermentable sugar,
> which any residual [[Yeast]] then ferments — restarting fermentation after the
> brewer thought it was done.

## The Chain of Events

1. Beer reaches **terminal gravity** — fermentation appears complete.
2. **Dry hops are added**, introducing diastatic enzymes.
3. Enzymes degrade dextrins into **maltose, glucose, and other fermentables**.
4. Residual yeast (still present, just dormant) **wakes up and ferments** them.
5. Result: lower final gravity, **extra alcohol**, extra **CO2**, and often
   **diacetyl**.

> [!warning] The over-carbonation hazard
> The new CO2 has nowhere to go in a sealed can or keg. Hop creep is a documented
> cause of over-pressurized, even exploding, packages — a genuine safety issue at
> commercial scale.

## The Side Effects on Flavor

| Effect | Sensory consequence |
|---|---|
| Lower final gravity | Thinner body, drier finish — less "pillowy" |
| Extra alcohol | Slight ABV creep above the label number |
| Renewed fermentation | **Diacetyl** (buttery) — see [[Off-Flavors in IPA]] |
| Extra CO2 | Gushing, over-carbonation, package failure |

Note how this undermines the very [[The Science of Mouthfeel|soft mouthfeel]] a
[[New England IPA]] is built around — heavy dry hopping fights its own goal.

## Managing Hop Creep

> [!tip] Practical controls
> - **Dry hop warm and finish fermentation fully** — let the creep happen *before*
>   packaging, then re-stabilize.
> - **Allow extra conditioning time** after the dry hop charge.
> - **Cold-crash and reduce yeast** before late hop additions.
> - **Monitor gravity after dry hopping**, not just before.

This is closely tied to [[Biotransformation]] (the other thing live yeast does to
dry hops) and is covered from the process side in [[Hop Creep and Refermentation]]
and [[Double Dry Hopping]], where multiple charges multiply the risk.
