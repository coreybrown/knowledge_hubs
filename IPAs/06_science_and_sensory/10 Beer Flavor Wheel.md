# Beer Flavor Wheel

The **Beer Flavor Wheel** is a structured vocabulary for describing what a beer
smells and tastes like. It turns vague impressions ("it's kind of fruity, kind of
weird") into precise, shared language, which is the foundation of every meaningful
[[Tasting and Evaluating IPAs|sensory evaluation]].

## Why a Shared Vocabulary Exists

Flavor perception is private; communication about it is not. Without a common
framework, two tasters using the word "estery" or "harsh" may mean entirely
different things. The flavor wheel solves this by giving every descriptor a
defined place and, ideally, a known **chemical reference standard**.

> [!note] Origins
> The original beer flavor wheel was developed in the 1970s by Morten Meilgaard and
> colleagues, and it remains the backbone of modern brewing sensory science. It has
> since been adapted and expanded, including hop- and IPA-specific versions.

## How the Wheel Is Structured

The wheel works from **general to specific**, in concentric rings:

| Ring | Example |
|---|---|
| **Class** (innermost) | Hoppy |
| **Category** | Citrus |
| **Specific term** (outer) | Grapefruit |

A taster moves outward, narrowing from a broad sensation to a precise descriptor.

## Major Classes Relevant to IPA

| Class | Typical IPA descriptors | Usually from |
|---|---|---|
| **Hoppy / aromatic** | Citrus, pine, dank, tropical, floral | [[Hop Aroma Compounds]] |
| **Bitter** | Clean, lingering, harsh, resinous | [[The Chemistry of Hop Bitterness]] |
| **Malty / caramel** | Bready, toasty, biscuit | [[Malt]] |
| **Fruity / estery** | Banana, pear, stone fruit | [[Yeast]], [[Biotransformation]] |
| **Off-flavors** | Buttery, cooked-corn, papery, skunky | faults (see below) |

> [!example] From wheel to NEIPA
> Tasting a [[New England IPA]]: start at *hoppy* → narrow to *tropical* → land on
> *passionfruit*. That precise term points the brewer back to specific
> [[Thiols and Hop Burst|thiols]] and hop varieties like [[Galaxy]].

## The Fault Side of the Wheel

A large slice of the wheel is devoted to **off-flavors**: diacetyl (butter), DMS
(cooked corn), acetaldehyde (green apple), oxidation (papery), and light-struck
(skunky). Naming a fault precisely is the first step to diagnosing it. Each is
covered in [[Off-Flavors in IPA]], with skunking detailed in
[[Light-Struck Beer and Skunking]].

> [!tip] Use reference standards
> The most rigorous use of the wheel pairs each term with a **spiked sample**, a
> beer dosed with a known compound, so tasters calibrate to the same anchor. This
> is the core of [[Sensory Training and Panels]].

## Beyond Aroma and Taste

Modern wheels also include **mouthfeel** terms (body, carbonation, astringency,
warmth), bridging into [[The Science of Mouthfeel]]. A complete description of an
IPA always covers appearance, aroma, flavor, *and* texture.
