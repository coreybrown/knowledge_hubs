# 🌡️ Water Temperature for Brewing

After mineral content, **temperature** is the strongest dial water gives you over the cup, because heat governs how fast coffee solubles dissolve. The widely accepted pour-over window is roughly **90–96°C (195–205°F)**, with much of the specialty world parking near **92–94°C** for filter brewing. Hotter water extracts faster and harder; cooler water extracts slower and gentler. Temperature is one of the cleanest single-variable levers in brewing; see how it feeds the bigger picture in [[The Role of Temperature in Extraction]].

## What Temperature Does to Flavor 🔥

Higher temperatures speed up [[Solubility and What Dissolves|solubility]] and push [[Extraction Yield and Strength|extraction yield]] up. The catch is that the compounds dissolve in a rough order: pleasant acids and sugars first, then heavier, more bitter and astringent compounds. Brew too cool for the grind and you stop short: a sour, [[Under-Extraction and Over-Extraction|under-extracted]] cup. Brew too hot for the grind and you drag out the bitter tail, a harsh, over-extracted one.

| Water temp | Tendency | Use when |
|---|---|---|
| 96–100°C | Fast, aggressive extraction | Coarse grind, [[Light Roast and Specialty Coffee\|very light roasts]], hard-to-extract beans |
| 92–94°C | Balanced, the common default | Most pour over, most roasts |
| 88–91°C | Gentle, forgiving | [[Roast Levels for Pour Over\|darker roasts]], to tame bitterness |
| Below ~85°C | Slow, prone to sourness | Rarely ideal for hot pour over |

> [!tip] Temperature and grind are partners
> Temperature and [[Grind Size for Pour Over|grind size]] both move extraction in the same direction, so adjust **one at a time**. Many brewers fix temperature near 93°C and dial flavor with grind, the cleaner lever per [[Dialing In Grind Size]].

## Roast Level Changes the Target

Roast level shifts the ideal window. **Lighter, denser roasts** are harder to extract and generally reward **hotter water** (95–100°C) to coax out enough flavor. **Darker, more soluble and brittle roasts** give up their solubles easily and can turn bitter, so they often brew better **cooler** (88–92°C). This is why a single fixed temperature is a starting point, not a rule.

## The Altitude Asterisk 🏔️

Water boils at lower temperatures as elevation rises, because atmospheric pressure drops. At sea level water boils at **100°C**; near 1,500 m (≈5,000 ft) it boils around **95°C**; at 2,500 m (≈8,000 ft) closer to **91–92°C**.

> [!warning] Boiling water is *not* a fixed temperature
> At high altitude, "fresh off the boil" may already sit below your target window, so you may need to brew with near-boiling water and **not** let it cool. Very high-elevation brewers may struggle to reach ideal temperatures at all. Conversely, at sea level, water straight off the boil (100°C) is usually a touch hot for filter; a 30–45 second rest brings it into range. A variable-temperature [[Gooseneck Kettles|kettle]] removes the guesswork.

## A Myth to Retire

> [!warning] "Never use boiling water"
> This common rule is an oversimplification. Off-the-boil water is too hot for *some* darker roasts, but plenty of light-roast specialty recipes (including competition routines) deliberately use water at or very near 100°C. The right answer depends on roast, grind, and altitude, not a blanket prohibition.
