# 🧪 Building Water — Recipes and Remineralization

Once you understand that water is most of the cup, the logical next step is to stop hoping your tap is good and **build your own** to a known target. The recipe is always the same shape: **start from near-zero water** — distilled or reverse-osmosis (RO) — which is a blank slate with almost no [[TDS and Mineral Content|TDS]], then **add back a controlled amount of minerals** to hit a chosen [[Water Chemistry — Hardness and Alkalinity|GH and KH]]. This gives perfectly repeatable, competition-grade water anywhere in the world, independent of local supply.

## The Two Building Blocks ⚗️

Remineralization needs a source of **hardness** (calcium and/or magnesium) and, optionally, a source of **alkalinity** (bicarbonate) to buffer. The two most common DIY salts:

| Salt | Adds | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Epsom salt** (magnesium sulfate, MgSO₄·7H₂O) | Magnesium → GH | Brightness, extraction, fruit |
| **Baking soda** (sodium bicarbonate, NaHCO₃) | Bicarbonate → KH | Buffering, balance, tames sharpness |

Some recipes add **calcium** (e.g. calcium chloride or food-grade calcium sulfate/gypsum) for body. The headline rule from [[Water Chemistry — Hardness and Alkalinity|the chemistry note]] holds: keep **GH at or above KH** so extracting minerals outweigh buffering ones.

## Two Approaches

> [!note] Concentrates vs. commercial sachets
> **DIY concentrate method:** dissolve your salts into a small, strong "stock" solution, then dose a few millilitres of each concentrate into a litre of RO/distilled. Cheap, precise, infinitely tweakable. **Commercial sachets:** products like *Third Wave Water*, *Lotus*, *Aquacode*, or *Perfect Coffee Water* are pre-measured minerals you tip into a fixed volume — zero math, less control. Both start from the same blank RO/distilled base.

## A Worked Example 📐

Here is a classic, widely used **Barista Hustle–style** two-concentrate recipe targeting a balanced profile near the [[The SCA Water Standard|SCA ideal]] (~68 ppm GH, ~40 ppm KH). All weights are food-grade salts into distilled or RO water.

> [!example] Build sheet — balanced "everyday" water
> **Step 1 — make two concentrates (per 1 litre distilled water each):**
> | Concentrate | Salt | Amount |
> |---|---|---|
> | Hardness | Epsom salt (MgSO₄·7H₂O) | 2.45 g |
> | Buffer | Baking soda (NaHCO₃) | 1.68 g |
>
> **Step 2 — build a brewing litre:**
> | Into 1 L distilled/RO, add | Volume of concentrate |
> |---|---|
> | Hardness concentrate | 10 mL |
> | Buffer concentrate | 10 mL |
>
> **Resulting water (approx):** GH ≈ 68 ppm, KH ≈ 40 ppm, TDS ≈ 110–150 ppm — squarely in the target box.

Adjust to taste: **more hardness concentrate** for a brighter, more extractive water that flatters [[Light Roast and Specialty Coffee|light roasts]] and juicy [[Acidity in Coffee|acidity]]; **more buffer** to soften a sharp, sour cup or to suit [[Roast Levels for Pour Over|darker roasts]]. Always weigh salts on a precise scale and store concentrates refrigerated.

## Why Bother — and the Caveats ⚠️

> [!warning] Reverse-osmosis and distilled water are *not* ready to brew
> Never brew with bare RO or distilled water — its near-zero mineral content extracts poorly and tastes hollow, and some sources note it can leach metals from equipment. It is a **base**, to be remineralized first.

The payoff is total control and perfect repeatability: the same water, every brew, so when you change [[Dialing In Grind Size|grind]] or [[The Brew Ratio|ratio]] you know the water held constant. For brewers who don't want to mix salts, the practical middle ground — good filters and the right bottled water — lives in [[Filtered, Bottled, and Tap Water]].
