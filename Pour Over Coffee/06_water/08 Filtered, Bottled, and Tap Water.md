# 🚰 Filtered, Bottled, and Tap Water

Building water from scratch is the precise route, but most people brew with whatever comes out of a tap, a filter jug, or a bottle. The good news is that **sensible everyday water can brew excellent coffee** — you just need to know which sources help, which hurt, and what to avoid. This is the pragmatic counterpart to [[Building Water — Recipes and Remineralization]]: how to get into the [[The SCA Water Standard|target window]] without a scale and a bag of Epsom salt.

## Tap Water 🏠

Tap water is free and often fine — but it varies wildly by region, and two things commonly spoil it: **chlorine/chloramine** (added for safety, it causes off-flavors) and **high alkalinity** (hard, over-buffered water that flattens [[Acidity in Coffee|acidity]]). If your area has soft-to-moderate, low-chlorine water, a simple carbon filter may be all you need. If it is very hard, tap alone will mute most coffees.

> [!warning] Avoid water-softener output
> Household **ion-exchange softeners swap calcium and magnesium for sodium**. This stops limescale but is bad for coffee: it strips the [[Water Chemistry — Hardness and Alkalinity|hardness (GH)]] that drives extraction while leaving the **alkalinity (KH) untouched**, so you get flat, salty-tasting, poorly extracting water — often the worst of both worlds. Brew from an unsoftened line or a different source.

## Filtered Water 💧

Filtration is the best-value upgrade for most homes, but **not all filters do the same job**:

| Filter type | Removes | Affects minerals? |
|---|---|---|
| **Activated carbon** (Brita-style jug, faucet) | Chlorine, odors, some organics | Largely leaves GH/KH intact |
| **Ion-exchange / "softening" cartridges** | Hardness | Lowers GH (can over-soften) |
| **Reverse osmosis (RO)** | Nearly everything | Strips to near-zero — must remineralize |

For most tap water, a **carbon filter** is the sweet spot: it removes the chlorine that ruins flavor while keeping the minerals you want. RO is excellent **only** as a blank base for [[Building Water — Recipes and Remineralization|building water]] — never brew with bare RO, which extracts poorly and tastes hollow, as [[TDS and Mineral Content|the TDS note]] explains.

## Bottled Water 🍾

Bottled water is convenient but a minefield, because **"spring" and "mineral" tell you nothing about the brew**. Check the label for **bicarbonate / total dissolved solids**:

> [!tip] Read the bottle's mineral table
> Favor **low-to-moderate TDS, low-bicarbonate** waters. Many famous mineral waters are extremely high in bicarbonate and brew flat, chalky coffee. Some low-mineral bottled waters (e.g. certain "pure"/low-TDS labels) are popular precisely because they sit near the SCA target out of the bottle.

> [!warning] Myth — "bottled water is always best for coffee"
> False, and a costly habit. A high-alkalinity bottled water brews worse than decent carbon-filtered tap. The word on the label matters far less than the numbers on the back, per [[Why Water Matters]].

## A Quick Decision Guide 🎯

- **Soft, low-chlorine tap** → carbon filter, brew, done.
- **Hard, high-alkalinity tap** → low-bicarbonate bottled water, or go to [[Building Water — Recipes and Remineralization|built water]].
- **Chasing competition-level consistency** → RO base + remineralization.
- **Never** → softener output, bare RO/distilled, or unlabeled "mineral" water.

If your cups are dull across every coffee despite good [[Dialing In Grind Size|grind]] and [[Brewing Technique|technique]], suspect the water first — the [[Pour Over Troubleshooting Guide|troubleshooting guide]] agrees.
