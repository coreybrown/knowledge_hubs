# 🏛️ The Specialty Coffee Industry

The **specialty coffee industry** is the slice of the global coffee trade built around quality rather than commodity volume — coffees evaluated, scored, and sold on the basis of how good they taste, not merely how cheaply they can be produced. It is the economic and institutional ground that pour over grew out of, and the reason a bag of beans can carry a farm name, a varietal, a process, and a cupping score on its label.

## 📏 The 80-Point Line

The defining technical fact of specialty coffee is a **grading scale**. Trained tasters, called Q graders, evaluate a coffee on a 100-point scale during a structured [[Cupping|cupping]] — judging aroma, flavor, acidity, body, balance, and the absence of defects. A score of **80 points or above** is the conventional threshold for "specialty." Below it, coffee is graded as commercial or commodity.

> [!note] A score, not a guarantee
> The 80-point line is meaningful but imperfect. Scores depend on the taster, the roast, and the day; a "85-point" coffee from one cupper may cup lower elsewhere. Treat scores as informed signals, not absolute truth — a theme echoed in [[How to Taste Coffee]].

| Score band | Grade | Typical use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 90+ | Outstanding / "presentation" | Competition, micro-lots, auction coffees |
| 85–89 | Excellent | Premium single-origin filter coffee |
| 80–84 | Very good / specialty entry | Most quality [[Single Origin vs Blends\|single origins]] and blends |
| Below 80 | Commercial / commodity | Supermarket and instant coffee |

## 🏢 The SCA and the Institutions

The **Specialty Coffee Association (SCA)** — formed in 2017 from the merger of the American and European specialty associations — is the field's central body. It sets standards (including the [[The SCA Water Standard|water standard]] and the [[The Coffee Brewing Control Chart|brewing control chart]]), runs education and certification, publishes research, and organizes the World Coffee Championships, including [[The World Brewers Cup]]. Its standards quietly shape how cafés brew and how [[Notable Roasters|roasters]] talk about quality.

## 💰 Market Structure

The industry sits atop the **commodity market** but tries to escape it. Most of the world's coffee trades as a fungible commodity whose price is set on the futures market (the "C price"). Specialty coffee carves out a premium tier through differentiation — origin, varietal, process, score — and through alternative [[Direct Trade and Coffee Sourcing|sourcing models]] that promise to pay farmers above the commodity rate.

> [!warning] The value capture problem
> Most of the money in a premium cup is captured downstream — by roasters, cafés, and retailers — not by the farmer. Specialty pricing helps, but the structural imbalance is real and persistent. See [[Sustainability in Coffee]] for the farmer-economics side.

The industry is also defined by its **wave** narrative — commodity convenience, then branded café chains, then the quality-obsessed [[First, Second, and Third Wave Coffee|third wave]] that made [[Light Roast and Specialty Coffee|light-roasted]], origin-forward, manually brewed coffee the aspirational standard.
