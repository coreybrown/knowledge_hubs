# 🌍 Culture and Industry

Pour over is more than a brewing technique. It is a **culture**, an **industry**, and a **community** that together turned a niche manual method into a global craft. This is the map of the human side of coffee: the people who grow, roast, sell, brew, compete, film, and argue about it. Where the brewing notes explain *how* to make a cup, these notes explain *who* makes the cup possible, *where* the beans come from, and *why* the world started caring so much about a slow drip of water through ground coffee.

This folder is the hub for the **Culture & Industry** domain. Use it as a table of contents, then follow the links outward into history, equipment brands, and the recipes named after the people below.

## 🧵 How It All Connects

The story runs in a loop. Farmers and sourcing models (see [[Direct Trade and Coffee Sourcing]]) feed green coffee to [[Notable Roasters|roasters]], who shaped the [[Light Roast and Specialty Coffee|light-roast aesthetic]] that filter brewing flatters. Those roasters supply [[Coffee Shops and Cafe Culture|cafés]], where [[Baristas and Home Brewers|baristas]] turn brewing into performance, most visibly at [[The World Brewers Cup]]. The gear they use comes from [[Coffee Gear Brands|brands]] like Hario and Acaia, and the whole scene is documented, debated, and amplified by [[Online Coffee Communities and Content|online communities]]. Threaded through every link is the question of [[Sustainability in Coffee|sustainability]]: whether this culture can endure.

> [!note] A culture built on a movement
> Pour over's cultural moment grew directly out of the [[The Specialty Coffee Movement|specialty coffee movement]] and the [[First, Second, and Third Wave Coffee|third wave]]. The history notes tell that origin story; these notes pick it up in the present.

> [!warning] Romance vs. reality
> The coffee industry tells beautiful stories about itself: "direct trade," "sustainable," "single-origin," "ethically sourced." Many are partly true and partly marketing. These notes flag where the popular narrative outruns the evidence, especially around [[Direct Trade and Coffee Sourcing|sourcing]] and [[Sustainability in Coffee|sustainability]].
