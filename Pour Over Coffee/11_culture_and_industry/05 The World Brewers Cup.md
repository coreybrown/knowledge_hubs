# 🏆 The World Brewers Cup

The **World Brewers Cup (WBrC)** is the competition that put manual filter brewing on a global stage. Organized under the Specialty Coffee Association as one of the World Coffee Championships, it asks a deceptively simple question: who can brew the best cup of filter coffee, by hand, and explain why? Its annual routines have a measurable downstream effect: the techniques, recipes, and gear that win tend to ripple straight into home brewing.

## 🎯 The Format

Competitors face two services, judged by sensory and technical panels.

| Service | What happens | Coffee used |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Compulsory** | Brew using coffee supplied by the organizers, unseen until shortly before | Same beans for everyone, which levels the field |
| **Open** | Brew a coffee of the competitor's choice while delivering a presentation | Free choice, often a rare, high-scoring lot |

In the **open service**, the competitor brews (usually three cups for the judges), talks through the coffee's origin, processing, and intended flavors, and is scored on the cup *and* the presentation. The compulsory round strips that away, testing raw brewing skill on unfamiliar beans. Most competitors use a [[Hario V60|V60]] or another [[Conical vs Flat Bottom Drippers|conical or flat-bottom dripper]], a [[Gooseneck Kettles|gooseneck kettle]], and obsessively [[Building Brew Water|built water]].

> [!note] It rewards explanation, not just taste
> Unlike a blind cupping, the Brewers Cup judges the *story* as well as the cup. Clear reasoning about [[Extraction Yield and Strength|extraction]] and flavor is part of the score, which is why winners double as teachers.

## 🌟 Champions Who Shaped Home Brewing

Several champions translated their routines into recipes that home brewers still use:

- **Stefanos Domatiotis** (Greece, 2014 champion, of Taf Coffee); see [[Stefanos Domatiotis Brewers Cup Recipe]].
- **Emi Fukahori** (Switzerland, 2018 champion): see [[Emi Fukahori Brewers Cup Recipe]].
- **Matt Winton** (Switzerland, 2021 champion) popularized a precise multi-pour "triangulation" approach; see [[Matt Winton Triangulation Method]].
- **Tetsu Kasuya** (Japan, 2016 champion), whose adjustable [[Tetsu Kasuya 4:6 Method|4:6 method]] became one of the most widely taught home recipes in the world.

More recent champions (such as Austria's Martin Wölfl in 2024) continue the pattern of competition technique flowing outward.

> [!warning] Competition ≠ everyday
> Winning routines use rare, expensive coffees, lab-built water, and conditions tuned for three perfect cups, not a daily two-cup morning brew. Borrow the *principles* (even pours, controlled [[Agitation and Turbulence|agitation]], deliberate [[Pulse vs Continuous Pouring|pulse pouring]]); don't expect a competition cup from supermarket beans.

The Brewers Cup is the proving ground that links [[Baristas and Home Brewers|professional baristas]] to the [[Competition Recipes|competition recipes]] this knowledge base documents: a feedback loop between the stage and the kitchen counter.
