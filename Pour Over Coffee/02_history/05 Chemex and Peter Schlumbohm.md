# ⏳ Chemex and Peter Schlumbohm

In 1941, a German-born chemist named **Peter Schlumbohm** patented a coffee brewer that looked less like kitchenware and more like laboratory glass — because that is exactly what inspired it. The [[Chemex]] is a single piece of borosilectate glass shaped like an hourglass, with a conical top, a wooden collar, and a leather tie. Schlumbohm, who held dozens of patents, designed it as a chemist would: a funnel and a flask, joined, using a heavy bonded paper filter to produce coffee of striking clarity. It is one of the few brewers in [[History of Pour Over Coffee|coffee history]] to be celebrated as much for its form as its function.

## 🧪 A Chemist's Logic

Schlumbohm's insight was that brewing is **filtration**, and good filtration needs a thick filter and an unobstructed flask. His Chemex filters are 20–30% heavier than ordinary paper, folded into a multi-layer cone that sits in the glass throat. The result is an exceptionally **clean, bright, tea-like** cup — almost no oils, almost no fines, sometimes criticized as too thin by drinkers who prefer the heavier body of the [[French Press]] or a [[Coffee Filters — Paper, Metal, and Cloth|metal filter]].

> [!tip] Why Chemex coffee tastes so "clean"
> The thick bonded filter removes more oil and sediment than almost any other paper, so flavors read crisp and separated. If your Chemex cup tastes papery, [[Pre-Wetting the Filter|rinse the filter thoroughly]] first — that heavy paper carries the most taste of any.

## 🏛️ A Design Icon

The Chemex transcended the kitchen. It sits in the permanent collection of New York's **Museum of Modern Art (MoMA)**, was named one of the best-designed products of modern times by the Illinois Institute of Technology, and appeared in films, magazines, and even a James Bond novel. Few brewers carry this cultural weight — and that status helped pour over read as *sophisticated* decades later, when the [[The Specialty Coffee Movement|specialty movement]] needed objects that signaled craft.

## 🔁 Place in the Lineage

The Chemex bridges two worlds. Like [[Melitta Bentz and the Paper Filter|Melitta's filter]] it is paper-and-cone percolation; like the later [[Hario and the V60|V60]] it is a deliberate **object of design**. But its slow, retentive filter and gentle flow make it temperamental — pour too fast and it stalls, grind too fine and it clogs. Those quirks make it a natural partner to a [[Gooseneck Kettles|gooseneck kettle]] and a patient [[Pouring Technique|pour]], and a useful contrast to the free-flowing cones that followed.
