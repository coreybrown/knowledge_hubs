# ⭐ The Specialty Coffee Movement

Pour over's modern revival was not really about gear — it was about a new way of **thinking about coffee as an agricultural product with origin character**, the way wine drinkers think about a vineyard. That shift is the **specialty coffee movement**, and it is the cultural engine that put filter brewing, single origins, and careful technique at the center of the coffee world. Without it, the [[Hario and the V60|V60]] would be a curiosity rather than an icon, and this whole [[History of Pour Over Coffee|history]] would read very differently.

## 📏 What "Specialty" Actually Means

"Specialty" is not just marketing — it has a **technical definition**. Coffee scored on a 100-point scale by a certified Q-grader that earns **80 points or more** qualifies as specialty; below that is commodity grade. The term was coined by Erna Knutsen in 1974 to describe distinctive beans from specific microclimates. Bodies like the **Specialty Coffee Association of America (SCAA)**, founded in 1982 (now merged into the global SCA), built the grading, cupping, and [[The SCA Water Standard|water]] standards that professionalized quality.

> [!note] A score, not a vibe
> When a roaster calls a coffee "specialty," they are implying an 80+ cup score and traceable sourcing — not simply that it is expensive or hipster. The [[How to Taste Coffee|cupping protocol]] behind that score is rigorous and repeatable.

## 🌍 Origin Character Takes Center Stage

The movement's core claim is that **where and how coffee is grown and processed is tasteable**. A washed Ethiopian and a natural Brazilian are profoundly different drinks, and a clean filter brew reveals those differences better than milk-and-sugar espresso ever could. This made pour over the movement's signature method — its [[The Science of Extraction|extraction clarity]] showcases [[Coffee Growing Regions and Terroir|terroir]], [[Coffee Processing Methods|processing]], and [[Light Roast and Specialty Coffee|light roasting]].

| Commodity coffee | Specialty coffee |
| --- | --- |
| Blended, anonymous origin | Single origin, traceable |
| Roasted dark for consistency | Roasted light to show character |
| Brewed strong to mask defects | Brewed clean to reveal flavor |
| Price set by commodity market | Price set by quality / [[Direct Trade and Coffee Sourcing\|direct trade]] |

## 🔗 The Pour-Over Connection

Specialty and pour over reinforced each other. Roasters needed a brewing method that **flattered delicate light roasts**; consumers wanted to taste the [[The Coffee Flavor Wheel|flavor notes]] on the bag. Manual filter brewing — controllable, clean, articulate — was the obvious answer, and it gave rise to the [[Brewing Technique|technique]] and [[Recipes and Methods|recipe culture]] that fill this knowledge base. The movement also built the [[The Specialty Coffee Industry|industry]] and [[Coffee Shops and Cafe Culture|café culture]] that sustain it.

## 🌊 Movement, Not Just Market

The specialty movement is often described through the "[[First, Second, and Third Wave Coffee|wave]]" framework — first wave convenience, second wave coffee-as-experience, third wave coffee-as-craft. That model is useful but contested, as the next note explains. What is not contested is the result: a global community for whom a [[The Modern Pour Over Renaissance|hand-poured cup]] is the purest expression of what coffee can be.
