# 🫗 The Rise of Drip Coffee

[[Melitta Bentz and the Paper Filter|Melitta's paper filter]] solved the grit problem, but it took the better part of the 20th century for **drip coffee** to become the default way the Western world made its morning cup. The arc runs from manual pour-over cones, through percolators that dominated mid-century America, to the **electric drip machine** that put paper-filtered coffee on nearly every kitchen counter. This is the chapter where filter coffee goes from craft to commodity — and quietly sets the stage for the [[The Modern Pour Over Renaissance|manual revival]] that would push back against it.

## ⏳ Percolator Decades

For much of the early-to-mid 1900s, the **stovetop and electric percolator** ruled American kitchens. Percolators repeatedly cycle boiling water up through the grounds, which makes strong coffee but also boils it — over-extracting and producing the burnt, bitter "diner coffee" stereotype. Drip, by contrast, passes water through the bed **once**, gently, the way a [[What Is Pour Over Coffee|pour over]] does. As people compared the two, the cleaner drip cup steadily won.

## ⚡ The Electric Drip Machine

The decisive shift came in 1972, when **Mr. Coffee** launched the first hugely successful automatic drip machine in the United States — famously endorsed by baseball legend Joe DiMaggio. It automated the pour: a heating element dripped hot water over a paper-lined basket of grounds, no kettle or technique required.

> [!note] The machine that almost killed the kettle
> The automatic drip machine is, mechanically, a robot doing a sloppy pour over. By removing skill from the process it democratized clean coffee — but it also hid the variables (temperature, pour rate, [[The Bloom|bloom]]) that manual brewers would later rediscover and obsess over.

| Era | Dominant method | Cup tendency |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1900s–1950s | Percolator | Strong, often over-extracted |
| 1950s–1970s | Manual drip + percolator | Mixed |
| 1972–2000s | Automatic drip machine | Clean but inconsistent |
| 2000s+ | Revived manual pour over | Precise, controllable |

## 📦 Paper Becomes Ubiquitous

The drip machine made the **paper filter** a grocery staple — flat-bottom baskets and Melitta cones stacked beside the coffee aisle in every supermarket. A whole generation grew up assuming "filter coffee" simply meant whatever the machine produced. That ubiquity matters: it built the muscle memory and the supply chain that the [[The Specialty Coffee Movement|specialty movement]] would later exploit, swapping the machine's careless pour for a [[Gooseneck Kettles|gooseneck kettle]] and a [[Scales, Timers, and Servers|scale]].

## 🔄 Why Manual Came Back

By the 2000s, drip machines had made coffee convenient but forgettable. The reaction — drinkers wanting control over [[Water Temperature for Brewing|temperature]], [[The Brew Ratio|ratio]], and [[Pouring Technique|pour]] — is precisely what the modern pour over offers. Drip's rise and the [[Single Cup vs Batch Pour Over|batch-versus-single-cup]] tension it created are the backdrop against which the [[Hario and the V60|V60]] would feel like a revelation.
