# 🌀 Hario and the V60

No single object says "pour over" like the **Hario V60**. A simple cone of ribbed plastic, glass, ceramic, or metal, it became, within a decade of its 2004 launch, the global symbol of manual coffee. The company behind it, **Hario**, was founded in Tokyo in 1921 as a maker of heat-resistant laboratory glass (the name means "King of Glass"). That lab-glass heritage, married to Japan's [[Japanese Coffee Culture and the Kissaten|kissaten craft tradition]], produced the dripper that anchors the whole [[History of Pour Over Coffee|pour over story]].

## 📐 The Design, Decoded

The "V60" name encodes the geometry: a **V**-shaped cone with **60-degree** walls. Three features make it distinctive, and each is a deliberate argument against the slow, retentive cones that came before:

1. **A single large hole** at the bottom lets the brewer, not the dripper, control flow. Pour fast for a quick brew, slow for a long one.
2. **Spiral ribs** running up the cone hold the [[Coffee Filters|paper filter]] off the wall, letting air escape and water flow freely.
3. **The steep 60° cone** funnels the coffee bed into a deep center, encouraging even [[The Drawdown|drawdown]].

> [!tip] The V60 gives you rope to hang yourself
> Because flow is governed by *your* pour and [[Grind Size for Pour Over|grind]] rather than the dripper, the V60 is the most expressive *and* least forgiving common cone. It rewards good [[Pouring Technique|technique]] and punishes sloppiness, which is exactly why it became the competitor's and obsessive's favorite.

## 🏆 From Tokyo to the World

The V60 launched in 2004 and rode the rising wave of [[The Specialty Coffee Movement|specialty coffee]] to ubiquity. It became the default brewer on World Brewers Cup stages, the star of countless [[Online Coffee Communities and Content|YouTube tutorials]], and the brewer behind landmark recipes from [[James Hoffmann V60 Technique|James Hoffmann]] to [[Tetsu Kasuya 4:6 Method|Tetsu Kasuya]]. Its cheap plastic version put a competition-grade brewer in any kitchen for a few dollars.

## 🔧 Why It Won

The V60 arrived at the perfect moment. The [[The Rise of Drip Coffee|automatic drip era]] had made coffee convenient but dull; specialty roasters were producing bright, complex [[Light Roast and Specialty Coffee|light roasts]] that demanded a brewer capable of showcasing them. The V60's clean, articulate cup and total controllability were the answer. It did not invent the cone ([[Melitta Bentz and the Paper Filter|Melitta]] and [[Chemex and Peter Schlumbohm|Schlumbohm]] did that), but it perfected the cone *for the specialty era* and became its emblem.
