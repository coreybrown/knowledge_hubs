# 🌊 First, Second, and Third Wave Coffee

The most common way people narrate modern coffee history is the **"wave" framework**: three (and now four) successive eras, each redefining what coffee meant. It is a genuinely useful shorthand for understanding how we got from canned supermarket grounds to a hand-poured single origin. But it is also a **simplification invented in hindsight**, and this note presents both the model and its fair critique. It pairs closely with [[The Specialty Coffee Movement|the specialty movement]] in the larger [[History of Pour Over Coffee|history]].

## 📖 The Three (or Four) Waves

The term "third wave" was popularized by Trish Rothgeb in 2002, and writers retroactively defined the earlier waves to match.

| Wave | Roughly | Defining idea | Pour-over relevance |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **First** | Late 1800s–1960s | Coffee as **commodity**: canned, convenient, ubiquitous | The [[The Rise of Drip Coffee\|drip machine]] era |
| **Second** | 1970s–1990s | Coffee as **experience**: espresso, cafés, lattes (Starbucks) | Café culture, but not yet origin-focused |
| **Third** | 2000s–2010s | Coffee as **craft**: origin, [[Light Roast and Specialty Coffee\|light roast]], manual brewing | Pour over's golden age, the era of the [[Hario and the V60\|V60]] |
| **Fourth** | 2010s–now (debated) | Coffee as **science**: extraction data, [[Refractometers and Measuring Extraction\|refractometry]] | Recipe culture, precision brewing |

## ☕ What Each Wave Did

The **first wave** made coffee a cheap household staple: Folgers, Maxwell House, the percolator. The **second wave** (epitomized by Starbucks) made coffee a *destination and a lifestyle*, normalizing the café and the espresso drink, though it still roasted dark and rarely named origins. The **third wave** turned coffee into craft: traceable [[Single Origin vs Blends|single origins]], lighter roasts, [[How to Taste Coffee|cupping]], and the rise of manual pour over as the connoisseur's method.

## 🤨 The Critique

> [!warning] The waves are a story we told ourselves
> The framework was coined *retroactively* and reflects a mostly **American, Anglophone** perspective. It erases long-standing coffee cultures in Italy, the Nordic countries, and especially Japan, whose [[Japanese Coffee Culture and the Kissaten|kissaten]] were practicing "third-wave" craft decades before the term existed. The boundaries are fuzzy, the waves overlap, and "fourth wave" remains a marketing argument more than a consensus.

In other words: the model is a **useful map, not the territory**. Treat it as a teaching device, not a precise chronology. Plenty of working professionals roll their eyes at it even as they use the vocabulary.

## 🧭 Why It Still Helps

For all its flaws, the wave framework captures a real arc, from convenience to experience to craft to measurement. That last shift toward data and [[The Science of Extraction|extraction science]] feeds directly into the [[The Modern Pour Over Renaissance|modern renaissance]] and the [[Recipe Variables and Cup Outcomes|recipe-driven]] way people brew today.
