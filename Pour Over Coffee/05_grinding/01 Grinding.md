# ⚙️ Grinding

**Grinding** is the act of reducing roasted whole beans to particles ready for brewing — and it is arguably the single most important variable a pour-over brewer controls. Grinding determines the **surface area** the water meets, and surface area governs the speed of [[The Science of Extraction|extraction]]. Change nothing but your grind and you can swing a cup from sour and thin to bitter and muddy. This is the home of everything about particle size, the machines that produce it, and how to wield it.

## Why Grind Sits at the Center 🎯

Of all the levers in a pour over — [[The Brew Ratio|ratio]], [[Water Temperature for Brewing|temperature]], [[Water Chemistry — Hardness and Alkalinity|water chemistry]], [[Pouring Technique|pour]] — grind is the one most directly tied to extraction rate. A finer grind exposes more [[Grind Size and Surface Area|surface area]] and slows the water's path, raising [[Extraction Yield and Strength|extraction]]; a coarser grind does the reverse. Just as crucial is **consistency**: a grinder that produces evenly sized particles extracts evenly, while one that produces a chaotic mix over- and under-extracts at the same time. For that reason a good grinder usually does more for cup quality than an expensive dripper.

> [!tip] Grind first, then everything else
> When dialing in a new coffee, hold ratio, water, and technique steady and adjust grind alone. It is the cleanest single-variable lever you have. See [[Dialing In Grind Size]].

## How Grind Connects Outward

Grind never acts alone. It interacts with [[Roast Levels for Pour Over|roast level]] (darker roasts are more brittle and shatter into more fines), with the [[The Dripper Explained|dripper]] you choose, and with the whole [[Brewing Technique]] that follows. When a cup goes wrong, the [[Pour Over Troubleshooting Guide|troubleshooting guide]] will almost always send you back here first.
