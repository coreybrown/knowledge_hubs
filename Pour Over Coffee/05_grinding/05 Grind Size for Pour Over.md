# 🎯 Grind Size for Pour Over

So where exactly should you set the grinder? Pour over lives in a **medium** grind window — coarser than espresso, finer than [[French Press|French press]] — but the precise target shifts with your [[The Dripper Explained|dripper]], your [[Roast Levels for Pour Over|roast]], and your taste. There is no universal number; there is a starting zone and a method for moving within it.

## The Target Window 🪟

A useful mental reference is **table salt to coarse sand** — distinctly granular, not powdery, not chunky. In practical terms most pour over sits in the **medium to medium-fine** range, roughly 600–900 microns, though brewers describe it relative to their own grinder rather than in microns. The goal is a grind that lets a full brew run in a sensible [[Brew Time and Total Contact Time|total time]] (commonly 2:30–3:30 for a single cup) without stalling or gushing.

| Reference point | Relative grind |
|---|---|
| [[French Press]] | Coarse |
| [[Chemex]] | Medium-coarse |
| [[Kalita Wave]] | Medium |
| [[Hario V60]] | Medium / medium-fine |
| Espresso | Very fine |

## How It Shifts by Brewer

The brewer's geometry and [[Coffee Filters — Paper, Metal, and Cloth|filter]] change how fast water drains, so the grind must compensate to keep contact time in range.

> [!example] Dripper by dripper
> - **[[Hario V60]]** — a fast, open cone with a big single hole. Water races through, so it wants a **finer** grind (medium / medium-fine) to slow it and reach full [[Extraction Yield and Strength|extraction]].
> - **[[Kalita Wave]]** — a flat bed with three small holes that restricts flow. A **medium** grind suits it; go too fine and it clogs.
> - **[[Chemex]]** — uses a thick, restrictive paper filter. A **medium-coarse** grind keeps the bed from choking against that dense paper.

## How It Shifts by Roast 🔥

[[Roast Levels for Pour Over|Roast level]] changes both bean structure and how readily it gives up flavor. **Lighter roasts** are dense and harder to extract, so they generally want a **finer** grind (and hotter water) to coax out enough. **Darker roasts** are brittle and extract easily — and shatter into more [[Fines and Boulders|fines]] — so they want a **coarser** grind to avoid bitterness and clogging.

> [!warning] Don't copy microns blindly
> Grinder "click" and micron numbers from a recipe rarely transfer between machines. Burr design, alignment, and wear mean the same setting grinds differently on different grinders. Use any published number as a rough start, then [[Dialing In Grind Size|dial in by taste]].

## The Real Answer: Dial It In

The window above gets you in the neighborhood; your tongue finds the address. Brew, taste, and adjust grind toward the cup you want — sour pushes finer, bitter pushes coarser. That iterative loop is the whole craft.
