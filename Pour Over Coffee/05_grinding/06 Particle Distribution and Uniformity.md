# 📊 Particle Distribution and Uniformity

A grind is never a single size — it is a **distribution** of sizes around a target. Plot how many particles fall at each diameter and you get the grinder's signature curve. The **shape** of that curve, not just its center point, decides how evenly your coffee extracts. This is why two grinders set to the "same" medium can brew very differently: their distributions differ.

## Reading the Curve 📈

The key feature is the number of peaks. A **unimodal** distribution has one clear hump — most particles cluster near the target size, with few strays. A **bimodal** distribution has two humps — a main peak at the target plus a second, smaller peak of very fine dust ([[Fines and Boulders|fines]]). Almost every real grinder produces *some* fines, so distributions are rarely perfectly unimodal; the question is how tight the main peak is and how large the fines tail.

| Distribution | Shape | Extraction behavior |
|---|---|---|
| Unimodal (tight) | One narrow peak | Even — particles extract in step |
| Bimodal | Main peak + fines peak | Uneven — fines and boulders diverge |
| Wide / flat | Spread out | Chaotic — see [[Burr Grinders vs Blade Grinders\|blade grinders]] |

## Why Uniformity Matters 🎯

Extraction is a race against time, and every particle extracts at a rate set by its size. In a brew, all particles get the *same* contact time — so if they differ wildly in size, they cannot all finish in the right place. The [[Fines and Boulders|fines]] over-extract into bitterness while the [[Fines and Boulders|boulders]] under-extract into sourness, and the cup is the muddled average of both. A **uniform** grind lets you choose one contact time that suits nearly every particle, so the whole bed lands in the sweet spot of [[Extraction Yield and Strength|extraction]] together. That is the mechanism behind clean, sweet, well-defined pour over.

> [!note] Uniformity is why grinders cost money
> Much of the price difference between grinders buys a tighter distribution — sharper, better-aligned burrs that produce fewer strays. It is the main reason a good grinder out-brews a good dripper.

> [!warning] Some fines are useful — and unavoidable
> A modest population of fines is not pure evil. Fines add body and help build the bed's resistance to flow. The goal is **controlled** distribution, not zero fines — which no grinder achieves. Chasing perfection here has diminishing returns past a quality burr set.

## Geometry and Distribution

Burr geometry nudges the curve: many [[Conical vs Flat Burrs|flat burrs]] aim for a tighter unimodal peak, while conicals are sometimes described as slightly broader — though, as that note stresses, this is **contested** and grinder quality dominates the category.
