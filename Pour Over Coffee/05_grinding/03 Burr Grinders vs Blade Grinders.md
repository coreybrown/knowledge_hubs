# 🪓 Burr Grinders vs Blade Grinders

Not all grinders are created equal, and the gap between a **burr grinder** and a **blade grinder** is the largest single equipment upgrade most home brewers can make. The difference comes down to one word: **uniformity**. Burrs produce particles of a controlled, consistent size; blades produce chaos. For pour over, where [[Particle Distribution and Uniformity|even extraction]] is everything, that difference decides the cup.

## How Each One Works

A **burr grinder** crushes beans between two abrasive surfaces (either [[Conical vs Flat Burrs|conical or flat burrs]]) set a fixed distance apart. A bean cannot fall through until it has been reduced to a size smaller than that gap, so every particle exits at roughly the same target size. You set the gap; the gap sets the grind.

A **blade grinder** is really a tiny propeller. A spinning blade smashes beans by brute impact, flinging fragments in every direction. Some particles get pulverized to dust on the first hit; others bounce away nearly whole. The result is a wildly inconsistent mix, and the only "setting" is how long you hold the button.

| | Burr grinder | Blade grinder |
|---|---|---|
| Mechanism | Crushing at a fixed gap | Random impact |
| Size control | Precise, repeatable | None (timing only) |
| Distribution | Tight, mostly [[Particle Distribution and Uniformity\|unimodal]] | Wide, [[Fines and Boulders\|fines + boulders]] |
| Repeatability | High: set and forget | Low: never the same twice |
| Pour-over suitability | Excellent | Poor |

## What Blades Do Wrong ❌

The blade's core failure is that it produces an extreme spread of sizes simultaneously. The cloud of [[Fines and Boulders|fines]] over-extracts into bitterness while the [[Fines and Boulders|boulders]] sit barely touched and under-extract into sourness, all in the **same cup**. You cannot dial in what you cannot control, so [[Dialing In Grind Size|dialing in]] becomes guesswork. Blades also heat the coffee through friction and offer no way to repeat a result, since "ten seconds" never yields the same grind twice.

> [!warning] The common workaround doesn't fix it
> Pulsing a blade grinder or shaking it helps a little but cannot create true uniformity; the impact mechanism is the problem, not the technique. Even a modest hand burr grinder will out-brew the best blade grinder.

> [!tip] Where blades are tolerable
> For spices, or for very forgiving [[French Press|immersion]] brewing, a blade grinder is passable. For pour over, it is the weakest link in the chain.

## The Bottom Line

If you upgrade one thing, upgrade your grinder to a burr. Even an entry-level [[Hand Grinders vs Electric Grinders|hand grinder]] transforms the cup. From there, the next question is **what kind** of burr.
