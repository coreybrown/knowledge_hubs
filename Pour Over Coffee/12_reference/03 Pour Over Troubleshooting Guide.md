# 🔧 Pour Over Troubleshooting Guide

Something tastes off. Before you blame the beans, work through the symptom tables below; most bad cups trace back to one or two variables. The golden rule: **change one thing at a time** so you learn what each adjustment does. For the theory behind why these fixes work, lean on [[Under-Extraction and Over-Extraction]] and [[The Science of Extraction]].

> [!warning] Diagnose before you adjust
> Sour and bitter are the two anchor flavors. **Sour usually means under-extracted; bitter usually means over-extracted.** Almost every fix below nudges extraction up or down by changing grind, time, temperature, or agitation.

## Taste Problems

| Symptom | Likely cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Sour / sharp / lemony** | Under-extraction | Grind finer, raise water temp, slow the pour, or increase contact time → [[Grind Size for Pour Over]] |
| **Bitter / harsh** | Over-extraction | Grind coarser, lower water temp, shorten contact time, reduce agitation → [[Water Temperature for Brewing]] |
| **Astringent / drying** | Over-extraction or excess fines | Grind coarser, reduce agitation, check for stalled drawdown → [[Fines and Boulders]] |
| **Weak / watery / thin** | Low strength | Use less water per gram (tighter ratio) or grind slightly finer → [[The Brew Ratio]] |
| **Too intense / muddy** | High strength | Use more water per gram, or scale the recipe up evenly → [[Recipe Variables and Cup Outcomes]] |
| **Flat / dull / lifeless** | Low extraction *or* poor water | Check water hardness and alkalinity; raise temp → [[Water Hardness and Alkalinity]] |
| **Both sour AND bitter** | Uneven extraction | Reduce channeling: even the bed, pour gently, level the grounds → [[Channeling and Uneven Extraction]] |

## Flow Problems

| Symptom | Likely cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Drawdown too fast** | Grind too coarse, or too much bypass | Grind finer; pour in the center, not down the walls → [[Bypass and Channeling]] |
| **Drawdown too slow / stalled** | Grind too fine, too many fines, or clogged filter | Grind coarser; reduce agitation; pre-wet the filter → [[Pre-Wetting the Filter]] |
| **Water pooling on top** | Bed clogged by fines | Coarsen grind; consider a coarser-grinding burr → [[Particle Distribution and Uniformity]] |
| **Coffee draining unevenly** | Channeling or an uneven bed | Swirl or tap to settle the bed before drawdown → [[The Drawdown]] |
| **Big crater in the bed** | Pour too aggressive or off-center | Pour more gently in slow concentric circles → [[Pouring Technique]] |

## Aroma & Bloom Problems

| Symptom | Likely cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Little or no bloom** | Stale beans (degassed) | Use fresher coffee; bloom is CO₂ escaping → [[Coffee Freshness and Degassing]] |
| **Huge, foamy bloom that overflows** | Very fresh, gassy beans | Use a larger bloom-water volume and a longer bloom → [[The Bloom]] |
| **Papery / cardboard taste** | Filter not rinsed | Pre-wet the paper before brewing → [[Pre-Wetting the Filter]] |
| **Muted aroma** | Old or improperly stored coffee | Buy smaller, fresher; store airtight → [[Reading a Coffee Bag Label]] |

> [!tip] Still stuck? Reset to a known-good baseline
> Brew [[The Standard V60 Recipe]] at 1:16, 92–94°C, with a medium grind, and change exactly one variable per brew. A consistent baseline turns guesswork into [[Dialing In Grind Size|dialing in]].

## When It Is Not the Brew

Sometimes the cup is fine and the coffee simply is not for you. A dark, smoky roast will never taste like a bright washed Ethiopian no matter how you brew it. Check whether the issue is technique or [[Single Origin vs Blends|bean choice]] and roast. And rule out the usual suspects from [[Common Pour Over Mistakes]] before going deeper.
