📖Recipes & Methods

The Rao Spin

2 min read·404 words
recipescommunityv60rao

Scott Rao is one of coffee's most influential technical writers, and his V60 method is built around a single famous move: the spin. After the final pour, you give the dripper a firm rotational swirl to flatten the coffee bed and wash every last ground off the filter wall, driving the most even drawdown possible. It looks like a flourish; it's actually extraction engineering. ⚙️

◆☕ Recipe Card
FieldValue
BrewerHario V60
Dose22 g
Water352 g total
Ratio1:16
Temp94-96°C
Grindmedium-fine — see Grind Size for Pour Over
Bloom66 g for 30-45 s, with a spin
Brew time~3:30
Roastlight to medium
SourceScott Rao, 2010s
Resulting cupEven, sweet, high extraction; clean with good body

#Pour Schedule

  1. 0:00 — Add 66 g (cumulative 66 g, ~3× dose); spin the dripper to wet all grounds evenly; bloom 30-45 s.
  2. 0:30-0:45 — Pour in a single steady stream to 352 g (cumulative 352 g), keeping the slurry deep and the bed agitated.
  3. ~1:30 — As the water level drops, give the dripper a firm spin to flatten the bed and rinse the walls.
  4. ~3:30 — Drawdown completes over a flat, even bed.

#Why It Works

Rao's two priorities are even extraction and avoiding bypass. The spin after the bloom guarantees full saturation; the deep, continuous pour keeps the whole bed agitated so fines aren't left under-extracted; and the final spin levels the bed and pulls down the high grounds that would otherwise sit dry on the wall, raising overall extraction yield and uniformity. Done well, the spin gives you the consistency competitive brewers chase, with less fuss than counting many small pours.

A word of nuance: the spin does add agitation, so on very fine grinds or fragile coffees it can tip toward over-extraction or stalling — see Agitation and Turbulence. Rao's broader work also champions fresh, well-developed roasts and careful grinding (he favors flat burrs); the temperature and exact weights are a representative baseline to dial to your beans.
