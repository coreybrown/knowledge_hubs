# 🌡️ The Role of Temperature in Extraction

Heat is energy, and **extraction is an energy-driven process**. That makes water temperature one of the most powerful levers on how fast and how completely coffee dissolves. Hotter water dissolves more solids, faster; cooler water extracts gently and selectively. Sitting alongside [[Grind Size and Surface Area|grind]] and [[Brew Time and Total Contact Time|time]], temperature lets you steer [[Extraction Yield and Strength|extraction yield]] without touching your grinder. ♨️

## Why Hotter Extracts More

Higher temperature raises the **solubility** of many compounds and speeds the **diffusion** of flavor out of each particle. Practically, every degree hotter nudges your brew toward higher [[Extraction Yield and Strength|extraction yield]]: useful for dense, hard-to-extract [[Light Roast and Specialty Coffee|light roasts]], risky for fragile [[Roast Levels for Pour Over|dark roasts]] that turn bitter easily. Temperature also affects *which* compounds you favor: cooler water extracts acids relatively more and bitter phenolics relatively less, which is the principle behind some [[Iced and Cold Pour Over|cold and cooler-brew]] techniques.

## The Practical Window 🎯

| Water temp | Effect | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| 100°C / 212°F (boiling) | Maximal extraction, scalding risk | Very dense light roasts, high altitude |
| 92–96°C | The common pour-over sweet spot | Most coffees |
| 88–92°C | Gentler, more forgiving | Medium/dark roasts, lessening bitterness |
| < 85°C | Selective, under-extraction risk | Deliberate experiments |

The widely cited target is **90–96°C (195–205°F)**, the [[The SCA Water Standard|SCA-recommended]] range. See [[Water Temperature for Brewing]] for the practical kettle-side guidance.

> [!tip] Use temperature to fine-tune, not overhaul
> Grind is your coarse adjustment; temperature is the fine one. If a cup is *slightly* sour, a few degrees hotter can fix it without re-dialing the grinder.

## Two Persistent Myths ⚠️

> [!warning] "You must never pour boiling water on coffee"
> Boiling water won't "burn" coffee, which already survived roasting at 200°C+. Off-boil water simply gives a touch more headroom against bitterness. Plenty of skilled brewers (and most [[French Press|French press]] users) pour at or near boiling on light roasts deliberately.

> [!warning] "Hotter is always stronger"
> Hotter raises *extraction*, not necessarily perceived *strength*. Strength (TDS) is set mainly by the [[The Brew Ratio|ratio]]. Temperature changes how much of the coffee dissolves, which is a different axis. See [[Extraction Yield and Strength]].

## Temperature During the Brew

Water also **cools** as it sits in the kettle, travels through the air, and contacts a cold dripper and grounds. This is why [[Pre-Wetting the Filter|pre-heating the dripper]] matters, why [[Dripper Materials and Heat Retention|ceramic vs. plastic vs. metal]] changes the cup, and why later pours in a long brew run cooler than the first.
