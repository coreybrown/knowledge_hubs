# 🌸 The Bloom

The **bloom** is the first small pour of a brew — a brief **pre-infusion** that wets all the grounds and lets trapped gas escape before the main pours begin. Watch fresh coffee bloom and you will see the bed swell, dome, and bubble like rising dough. It is the most visually satisfying moment in pour over, and one of the most functional.

## Why Bloom at All? 💨

Roasted coffee is full of **carbon dioxide** produced during [[Coffee Roasting Explained|roasting]]. When hot water hits the grounds, that CO₂ rushes out as gas. If you skip the bloom and pour all at once, those bubbles physically push water *away* from the coffee, creating dry pockets and uneven, often [[Under-Extraction and Over-Extraction|under-extracted]] cup. Blooming degasses the bed first so the main pours can saturate it evenly. The chemistry is covered in [[CO2, Degassing, and the Bloom Science]].

> [!note] Fresher coffee blooms harder
> A vigorous, foamy bloom signals fresh, well-gassed beans. Stale coffee barely reacts. Bloom is an informal [[Coffee Freshness and Degassing|freshness]] test you run every morning.

## How Much Water?

The standard guidance is **2 to 3 times the coffee weight** in bloom water.

| Coffee dose | Typical bloom water |
|---|---|
| 15 g | 30–45 g |
| 20 g | 40–60 g |
| 30 g | 60–90 g |

Use just enough to wet every grain. Too little leaves dry clumps; too much and the bloom drains away before degassing finishes. A gentle swirl or a careful stir helps reach any stubborn dry pockets — a mild form of [[Agitation and Turbulence|agitation]].

## How Long?

Most recipes bloom for **30 to 45 seconds**. Light, [[Light Roast and Specialty Coffee|fresh light roasts]] hold more gas and can reward a longer bloom (up to 60 seconds); older or [[Roast Levels for Pour Over|darker]] coffee degasses faster and needs less. Watch the bed: when the doming relaxes and bubbling slows, the bloom has done its job.

> [!tip] Bloom by sight, not just the clock
> The timer is a starting point. The bed itself tells you when degassing is done — that is the real cue to begin your next pour.

## Common Bloom Mistakes

- **Too little water** — dry patches survive into the main brew, causing uneven [[Bypass and Channeling|extraction]].
- **Channeling the bloom pour** — dumping water in one spot drills a hole; pour gently to wet the whole surface.
- **Endless bloom** — past ~60 s the grounds cool and you lose [[Water Temperature for Brewing|temperature]] for little gain.

The bloom is stage three in [[The Anatomy of a Pour Over]] and the foundation for everything that follows. A good bloom rarely makes a cup by itself, but a bad one almost always shows up later.
