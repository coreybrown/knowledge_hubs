# 🫗 Single Cup vs Batch Pour Over

Brewing one cup and brewing for a table are not the same act scaled up — they are different problems. As you increase the dose and water, the **bed gets deeper**, the **water column gets taller**, and the physics of [[The Drawdown|drawdown]] and [[Extraction Yield and Strength|extraction]] shift. Technique that nails a 15 g [[Hario V60|V60]] can ruin a 50 g batch. This note covers what changes and how to adjust.

## What Changes When You Scale Up 📈

> [!note] Bed depth is the hidden variable
> Doubling the coffee does not double the brewer's width — it mostly deepens the bed. Water now travels through more coffee, so it spends longer in contact and extracts more aggressively per pass.

| Factor | Single cup | Batch |
|---|---|---|
| Bed depth | Shallow | Deep |
| [[Brew Time and Total Contact Time\|Contact time]] | 2:30–3:30 | 5:00–8:00 |
| Extraction tendency | Baseline | Runs higher |
| [[Bypass and Channeling\|Channeling]] risk | Moderate | Higher |
| Pour management | Easy | Demanding |

## Adjusting Technique for Batch

Because a deep bed extracts more and drains slower, you typically:

- **Grind coarser.** A deeper bed lengthens contact time on its own; coarsening compensates to avoid [[Under-Extraction and Over-Extraction|over-extraction]] and a stalled drawdown.
- **Keep the [[The Brew Ratio|ratio]] fixed.** Strength is set by ratio regardless of size — scale both numbers together.
- **Pour patiently in stages.** Big water volumes are easy to dump; deliberate [[Pulse vs Continuous Pouring|pulses]] keep the deep bed even and reduce [[Bypass and Channeling|channeling]].
- **Mind temperature.** A larger brew loses heat over its longer run; preheat the [[The Dripper Explained|dripper]] and consider the role of [[Water Temperature for Brewing|temperature]].

> [!tip] Use a brewer built to scale
> Flat-bottom and large brewers — the [[Kalita Wave|Kalita Wave]] 185, the [[Chemex]], big [[Flat Bottom Drippers — April and Orea|flat drippers]] — keep a shallower, more even bed than a steep cone as volume grows. They drain more uniformly and forgive deeper beds. A full [[Batch Pour Over Recipe|batch recipe]] lives in the recipes domain.

## Single Cup Strengths

The single cup is where you learn. It is fast, cheap on coffee, and gives quick feedback for [[Dialing In Grind Size|dialing in]] — brew, taste, adjust, repeat in minutes. Most technique notes here assume a single cup precisely because the feedback loop is tight.

## When Batch Wins

For guests, for the office, or for cold storage, batch is simply efficient — one brew, many cups. The trade is a longer, less forgiving process where small errors compound across the deeper bed. Get your single-cup fundamentals solid first; batch rewards a brewer who already reads [[The Drawdown|drawdown]] and [[Brew Time and Total Contact Time|contact time]] fluently.
