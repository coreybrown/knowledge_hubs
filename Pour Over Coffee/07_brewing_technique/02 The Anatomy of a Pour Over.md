# 🧭 The Anatomy of a Pour Over

Every manual brew, from a humble [[Hario V60|V60]] to a four-cup [[Chemex]], follows the same arc. Knowing the **stages** turns a recipe from a list of numbers into a story you can read and adjust. This note maps the brew start to finish; the deeper craft of each stage lives in its own note.

## The Five Stages

> [!example] One brew, five acts
> Rinse → Dose → Bloom → Pours → Drawdown. Each stage hands off to the next, and a fault in one shows up later as an off cup.

| Stage | What happens | Roughly | Deep dive |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Rinse | Wet the paper, preheat the brewer | 0:00 | [[Pre-Wetting the Filter]] |
| 2. Dose | Add ground coffee, level the bed | · | [[The Brew Ratio]] |
| 3. Bloom | Wet all grounds, let CO₂ escape | 0:00–0:45 | [[The Bloom]] |
| 4. Pours | Add the remaining water in stages | 0:45–2:30 | [[Pouring Technique]] |
| 5. Drawdown | Last water drains through the bed | until ~3:00 | [[The Drawdown]] |

## 1. Rinse the Filter 🚿

Pour hot water through the empty paper [[Coffee Filters|filter]] to rinse away papery taste and preheat the [[The Dripper Explained|dripper]] and server. Discard that water before you add coffee. Whether it changes the cup is debated (see [[Pre-Wetting the Filter]]).

## 2. Dose and Level

Weigh your coffee on a [[Scales, Timers, and Servers|scale]] and add the [[Grind Size for Pour Over|right grind]]. A quick tap settles the bed flat so water meets every particle evenly. An uneven bed is the first seed of [[Bypass and Channeling|channeling]].

## 3. Bloom

The first small pour, typically two to three times the coffee weight, saturates the grounds and releases trapped [[CO2, Degassing, and the Bloom Science|CO₂]]. Fresh coffee swells and bubbles. The bloom sets up an even extraction for everything that follows.

## 4. The Main Pours

Now you add the bulk of the water, either as discrete [[Pulse vs Continuous Pouring|pulses]] or one [[Pulse vs Continuous Pouring|continuous]] stream, controlling [[Agitation and Turbulence|agitation]] with your [[Gooseneck Kettles|kettle]]. Pour pattern, height, and flow rate all live here; see [[Pouring Technique]].

## 5. Drawdown

Once your final pour is in, the remaining water drains through the bed and the [[The Drawdown|drawdown]] begins. Its speed reveals whether your grind and pour were balanced. When the dripper stops dripping, the brew is done, and total [[Brew Time and Total Contact Time|contact time]] is your headline number.

> [!tip] Read the bed afterward
> A flat, level bed of spent grounds signals an even brew. A crater, a high-sided "donut," or coffee stranded up the walls hints at [[Bypass and Channeling|channeling]] or sloppy pouring.
