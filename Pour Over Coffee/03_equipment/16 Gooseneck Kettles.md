# 🦢 Gooseneck Kettles

The **gooseneck kettle** is the second-most-iconic piece of pour over gear after the dripper itself, and arguably the one that most improves a beginner's coffee. Its long, curved, narrow spout gives you precise control over **where, how fast, and how steadily** water lands on the coffee bed. Combine that with accurate **temperature control**, and you control the two pour-side variables that shape every cup: [[Pouring Technique|flow]] and [[Water Temperature for Brewing|heat]].

## Why the Gooseneck Shape Matters

A standard kettle dumps water in a wide, uncontrollable gush, disastrous on a [[Hario V60|conical]] bed, where it gouges channels and causes [[Bypass and Channeling|channeling]]. The gooseneck's narrow spout delivers a **thin, steady, aimable stream** so you can pour in slow concentric circles, control [[Agitation and Turbulence|agitation]], and keep the bed even. This is what makes deliberate techniques like the [[Pulse vs Continuous Pouring|pulse pour]] and the [[Tetsu Kasuya 4:6 Method]] possible at all.

> [!tip] Spout shape changes the pour
> Kettles with a sharply curved, tapered spout give a delicate trickle for high control; straighter spouts pour faster for high-flow brewers like the [[Origami Dripper]]. Try to match flow style to your dripper.

## Stovetop vs Variable-Temperature Electric

| Feature | Stovetop gooseneck | Variable-temp electric |
|---|---|---|
| Temp control | Use a thermometer / off-boil | Set to the exact degree |
| Hold temperature | No | Yes, many hold for batches |
| Cost | Lower | Higher |
| Convenience | Manual | One-button, repeatable |

> [!note] Why temperature precision helps
> [[The Role of Temperature in Extraction|Temperature drives extraction]]. [[Light Roast and Specialty Coffee|Light roasts]] often want 93–96 °C to extract fully, while darker roasts brew cooler. A variable-temperature kettle makes [[Building Brew Water|recipes]] repeatable to the degree; a stovetop kettle works fine if you keep a thermometer handy or learn your off-boil timing. See [[Water Temperature for Brewing]].

> [!warning] You don't *need* the most expensive kettle
> A gooseneck genuinely helps, but a basic stovetop gooseneck plus a cheap thermometer brews coffee indistinguishable from a premium electric kettle. The premium models buy **convenience and repeatability**, not inherently better coffee. Spend on a [[Burr Grinders vs Blade Grinders|grinder]] first.
