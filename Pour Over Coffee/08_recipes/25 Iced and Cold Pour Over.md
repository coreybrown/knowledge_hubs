# 🧊 Iced and Cold Pour Over

Japanese-style iced coffee (also called **flash brewing**) is pour over's best summer trick: you brew **hot, directly onto ice**, so the coffee is chilled the instant it's extracted. This is *not* cold brew. The hot water still extracts the bright acidity and floral aromatics that cold brew mutes, and the ice **flash-cools and dilutes** the concentrate to drinking strength in one move, locking in a vivid, clean iced cup. ❄️

## The Split: Ice + Hot Water

The trick is dividing your total water into **ice in the server** and **hot brew water through the cone**. A common split for 300 g total is **~120 g ice** in the server and **~180 g hot water** poured through. The ice melts into the brew, so it *counts* toward your ratio. Skip it and the coffee comes out thin and over-diluted.

## Pour Schedule

1. **Setup**: Put **~120 g ice** in the [[Scales, Timers, and Servers|server]] under the dripper; rinse the filter; add 20 g coffee.
2. **0:00**: Pour **50 g** hot water (cumulative 50 g brew water); [[The Bloom|bloom]] 45 s.
3. **0:45**: Pour to **120 g** (cumulative 120 g brew water) in circles.
4. **1:30**: Pour to **180 g** (cumulative 180 g brew water).
5. **~2:30-3:00**: Drawdown completes onto the ice; **swirl the server** to melt remaining ice, then serve over fresh ice.

## Why It Works

Brewing hot captures the **volatile aromatics and clean acidity** that only dissolve well at high temperature (the very compounds slow cold brew leaves behind), while the ice **arrests the brew before it warms and dulls**, preserving them. Because roughly **40% of your water is ice**, you brew a stronger-than-usual concentrate (effectively a tighter ratio through the cone), then the melt brings it to balance. Grind slightly finer than your hot recipe to compensate for the smaller volume of liquid actually passing through the bed.

This flatters bright, fruity, [[Washed Process|washed]] and [[Light Roast and Specialty Coffee|light-roasted]] coffees spectacularly. For a chilled batch instead, scale up via the [[Batch Pour Over Recipe]] with the same ice-in-the-server principle.
