# ☕ Hario V60 Official Recipe

Hario's own method for the [[Hario V60]] is the natural starting point for the world's most popular pour-over cone. It is intentionally simple, just a single bloom and two or three steady pours at a 1:15 [[The Brew Ratio|ratio]], and it shows off exactly what the cone was engineered to do: a fast, clean, gravity-driven [[The Drawdown|drawdown]] through tall spiral ribs and a single large hole. 🌀

## Pour Schedule

1. **0:00**: Add **30 g** (cumulative 30 g) to wet all grounds; let it [[The Bloom|bloom]] 30 s.
2. **0:30**: Pour in slow concentric circles to **150 g** (cumulative 150 g).
3. **1:15**: Pour again to **225 g** (cumulative 225 g), keeping the bed level.
4. **~2:00-3:00**: Let it draw down fully; the bed should leave a flat, even wall.

## Why It Works

The V60's open cone and single large aperture mean **the brewer controls flow with the pour and the grind, not the dripper**: water leaves almost as fast as you add it. That demands a slightly finer grind than a flat-bottom brewer and rewards a controlled [[Pouring Technique|pour]] from a [[Gooseneck Kettles|gooseneck kettle]]. Keep the slurry moving in gentle circles and avoid pouring straight down the side, which causes [[Bypass and Channeling|bypass]]. Hario scales the recipe linearly: many shops brew 20 g to 300 g or 30 g to 450 g with the same rhythm.

The official numbers are a baseline, not gospel. Hario itself prints slightly different figures across markets and years, so treat the temperature and counts as approximate. From here, the more developed [[James Hoffmann V60 Technique]] and [[Tetsu Kasuya 4:6 Method]] are the natural next steps, both built on this same cone.
