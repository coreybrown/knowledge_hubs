# 🌀 The Rao Spin

Scott Rao is one of coffee's most influential technical writers, and his V60 method is built around a single famous move: **the spin**. After the final pour, you give the dripper a firm rotational swirl to **flatten the coffee bed and wash every last ground off the filter wall**, driving the most even [[The Drawdown|drawdown]] possible. It looks like a flourish; it's actually extraction engineering. ⚙️

## Pour Schedule

1. **0:00** — Add **66 g** (cumulative 66 g, ~3× dose); **spin** the dripper to wet all grounds evenly; [[The Bloom|bloom]] 30-45 s.
2. **0:30-0:45** — Pour in a single steady stream to **352 g** (cumulative 352 g), keeping the slurry deep and the bed agitated.
3. **~1:30** — As the water level drops, give the dripper a firm **spin** to flatten the bed and rinse the walls.
4. **~3:30** — Drawdown completes over a flat, even bed.

## Why It Works

Rao's two priorities are **even extraction** and **avoiding bypass**. The spin after the bloom guarantees full saturation; the **deep, continuous pour** keeps the whole bed agitated so fines aren't left under-extracted; and the **final spin** levels the bed and pulls down the high grounds that would otherwise sit dry on the wall, raising overall [[Extraction Yield and Strength|extraction yield]] and uniformity. Done well, the spin gives you the consistency competitive brewers chase, with less fuss than counting many small pours.

A word of nuance: the spin **does add agitation**, so on very fine grinds or fragile coffees it can tip toward over-extraction or stalling — see [[Agitation and Turbulence]]. Rao's broader work also champions **fresh, well-developed roasts** and careful grinding (he favors flat burrs); the temperature and exact weights are a representative baseline to dial to your beans.
