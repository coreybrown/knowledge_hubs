# 🔺 Matt Winton Triangulation Method

Matt Winton won the 2021 World Brewers Cup with a deceptively plain-looking V60 routine: **five equal pours of 60 g each**, every one with the same circular motion, spaced evenly in time. The name "triangulation" reflects his methodical, repeatable approach — controlling agitation and temperature so precisely that each of the five identical pours lands the brew on the same target, building extraction in even, predictable increments. 🧮

## Pour Schedule

Five equal **60 g** pours, each followed by a ~30 s wait, with the kettle allowed to cool between pours:

1. **0:00** — Pour to **60 g** (cumulative 60 g) at ~93°C; saturate all grounds, [[The Bloom|bloom]].
2. **~0:45** — Pour to **120 g** (cumulative 120 g), circular motion centre-to-edge.
3. **~1:30** — Pour to **180 g** (cumulative 180 g).
4. **~2:15** — Pour to **240 g** (cumulative 240 g), now nearer ~88°C.
5. **~3:00** — Pour to **300 g** (cumulative 300 g).
6. **~3:30-6:50** — Long, slow final drawdown completes.

## Why It Works

By making every pour **identical in size and motion**, Winton removes pour-to-pour variability — the biggest source of inconsistency in free-pour V60. Each 60 g addition stirs the bed gently and tops the water back up, building extraction in equal steps. The **staged temperature** — hot at the start to extract the dense, [[Light Roast and Specialty Coffee|light-roasted]] grounds, cooler at the end — front-loads extraction while keeping the finish from turning harsh. The notably **long total time** (his competition brew ran past six minutes) suits the medium grind and the goal of complete, even extraction.

This method rewards a clean, well-developed light roast and a [[Gooseneck Kettles|gooseneck kettle]] you can pour very consistently. The exact temperatures and intervals are reconstructed from interviews and demonstrations and are approximate; the load-bearing idea is **five equal pours, evenly spaced**.
