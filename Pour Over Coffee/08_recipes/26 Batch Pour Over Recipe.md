# 🫖 Batch Pour Over Recipe

Brewing for a table is its own skill. Scaling a single-cup recipe straight up doesn't quite work: a deep bed of coffee resists [[The Drawdown|drawdown]], holds more heat, and over-extracts the bottom while the top runs fast. This recipe adapts the fundamentals for **larger flat-bottom brewers and the [[Chemex]]** — roughly **60 g to 960 g (about four cups)** — with the tweaks that keep a big brew even. 👥

## Pour Schedule

1. **Setup** — Rinse the filter generously ([[Pre-Wetting the Filter|pre-wet]]), add 60 g coffee, level the deep bed.
2. **0:00** — Add **120 g** (cumulative 120 g, ~2× dose); [[The Bloom|bloom]] 45 s, stirring or swirling to wet the deep bed fully.
3. **0:45** — Pour to **400 g** (cumulative 400 g) in wide circles.
4. **1:45** — Pour to **680 g** (cumulative 680 g).
5. **2:45** — Pour to **960 g** (cumulative 960 g), keeping the bed level.
6. **~5:00-6:00** — Drawdown completes; stir or swirl the server before pouring so the cup is uniform.

## Why It Works — and What Changes at Scale

Three things shift when you go big. First, the **deeper bed extracts unevenly**, so you grind a touch **coarser** and stir the bloom to saturate it fully — see [[Single Cup vs Batch Pour Over]]. Second, the **larger thermal mass holds heat**, pushing extraction up, which the coarser grind offsets. Third, the **drawdown is slow**, so pours are bigger and fewer, and the [[Coffee Filters — Paper, Metal, and Cloth|filter]] choice matters — the thick [[Chemex]] paper is naturally suited to volume. A final **swirl of the carafe** before serving evens out strength stratification between the first and last cup poured.

For café-scale volumes most shops switch to automatic [[Single Cup vs Batch Pour Over|batch brewers]], but a Chemex or large flat-bottom dripper handles a dinner party beautifully. The numbers scale linearly from there; just expect longer total times as the bed deepens.
