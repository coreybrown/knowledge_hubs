# 🇨🇭 Emi Fukahori Brewers Cup Recipe

Emi Fukahori of MAME in Zurich won the 2018 World Brewers Cup — and did it with two memorable departures from the crowd. She brewed an experimental **Laurina** (a naturally low-caffeine variety from Daterra in Brazil) rather than the near-ubiquitous gesha, and she **staged her water temperature dramatically across the brew**, starting cool, going hot in the middle, then cool again, to shape exactly which flavors each pour pulled. 🌡️

## Pour Schedule

A reconstruction of her temperature-staged three-pour routine (treat figures as approximate):

1. **0:00** — Pour **~50 g** at **~80°C** (cumulative ~50 g); gentle, sweetness-first; rest ~45 s ([[The Bloom|bloom]]).
2. **~0:45** — Pour **~100 g** at **~95°C** (cumulative ~150 g) to "open up" the coffee.
3. **~1:50** — Pour **~70 g** at **~80°C** (cumulative ~220 g) for a clean, cool finish.
4. **~2:55** — Drawdown completes.

> [!warning] Specifics are approximate
> The temperatures, pour weights, and timings above are reconstructed from interviews and published summaries of her 2018 performance and should be treated as approximate. The signature idea — **cool, then hot, then cool** — is the reliable takeaway.

## Why It Works

Temperature is one of the strongest levers on [[Extraction Yield and Strength|extraction]], and Fukahori used it like a dimmer. The **cool first pour** coaxes out delicate sweetness and fruit without harshness while the grounds are most soluble; the **hot middle pour** drives the bulk of the extraction and develops body; the **cool final pour** rinses through cleanly without dragging out bitterness. Pulling this off requires either two kettles or a precise variable-temperature kettle and careful timing — see [[The Role of Temperature in Extraction]] and [[Water Temperature for Brewing]].

Her win is also a reminder that **bean choice is a competitive weapon**: a fragile, low-caffeine [[Coffee Varietals|variety]] like Laurina rewards exactly this gentle, staged handling. At home, you can borrow the idea by simply letting your kettle cool a few degrees for the final pour.
