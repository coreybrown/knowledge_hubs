# 👅 Tasting and Sensory

Everything else in this knowledge base (the [[The Coffee Plant|plant]], the [[Coffee Processing Methods|processing]], the [[Grind Size for Pour Over|grind]], the [[Water for Coffee|water]], the [[The Brew Ratio|ratio]], the [[The Science of Extraction|extraction]]) exists to serve one moment: the cup arriving in your mouth. **Tasting and sensory** is the domain where the chemistry becomes experience. It is the skill of perceiving what is in a brew, deciding whether you like it, and describing it precisely enough that the description survives the trip from your palate to someone else's ear. This folder teaches you to do all three.

Tasting is often mistaken for a gift, something a lucky few are born with. It is not. 🧠 The physiology of smell and taste is remarkably even across healthy adults; what separates a novice from a professional is **attention, vocabulary, and reference experience**, all of which are trainable. The notes here build that capacity from the ground up, then connect it back to the brewing decisions that produced the flavors you are now naming.

## Why It Matters for Brewing

Sensory skill closes the feedback loop. When you can tell that a cup is hollow and sour rather than balanced, you know to grind finer or pour hotter; when it is harsh and drying, you know to back off. This is the same diagnostic logic that powers [[Under-Extraction and Over-Extraction]] and the [[Pour Over Troubleshooting Guide]], but those notes describe causes, while your palate detects effects. The two only work together. 🔁

> [!tip] Taste everything, every time
> The fastest way to improve is to taste your own brews deliberately, even the bad ones. *Especially* the bad ones. A cup you cannot describe is a lesson you cannot repeat.

## A Note on Subjectivity

Flavor perception is partly personal: genetics, culture, and memory all shape what you notice and prefer. Professionals manage this not by pretending to be objective but by anchoring to **shared references**: the [[Cupping|cupping table]], the [[The Coffee Flavor Wheel|flavor wheel]], and a common [[Describing Coffee|vocabulary]]. Calibration, not innate superiority, is what lets two tasters agree.
