# 🍯 Sweetness and Body

If [[Acidity in Coffee|acidity]] is coffee's brightness, **sweetness** is its reward and **body** is its weight. Together they form the backbone of a satisfying cup: sweetness makes flavors pleasant and balanced, while body gives the coffee presence and texture on the tongue. Both are widely misunderstood — sweetness in black coffee surprises people, and "body" gets used as a vague compliment when it actually describes something specific and measurable. This note pins down what each is and what you can do to maximize it. 

## Sweetness — A Real, Perceptible Quality

Coffee can taste genuinely sweet without any sugar added. Green coffee contains sugars that, during [[Coffee Roasting Explained|roasting]], partly caramelize and partly drive the **Maillard reaction**, generating caramel, honey, chocolate, and toasted notes. Some sweetness is true taste; much is **aroma read as sweetness** by the brain — a vanilla or caramel smell makes a cup *seem* sweeter even though sugar levels are low.

> [!note] Sweetness signals good extraction
> Sweet compounds dissolve in the *middle* of a brew, after the early sour acids and before the late bitter ones. A balanced, fully sweet cup is therefore evidence of even [[The Science of Extraction\|extraction]]. Cups that finish sour are usually [[Under-Extraction and Over-Extraction\|under-extracted]]; cups that turn harsh and dry are over-extracted, and in both cases the sweetness goes missing.

What drives sweetness:
- 🌱 **Ripe, well-processed cherry** — sugar starts in the fruit. [[Natural Process|Naturals]] and [[Honey and Experimental Processing|honey processing]] often read sweeter than [[Washed Process|washed]].
- 🔥 **Roast development** — under-developed roasts taste grassy and sour; well-developed ones unlock caramelized sweetness.
- 💧 **Even extraction** — a clean [[Pouring Technique|pour]] without [[Bypass and Channeling|channeling]] lets the sweet middle of the brew come through.

## Body — The Texture of the Cup

**Body** (or *mouthfeel*) is the tactile weight and texture of coffee, sensed by touch rather than taste — from thin and tea-like to thick, syrupy, and creamy. It comes from physical material suspended in the brew: **insoluble oils, fine colloids, and tiny coffee particles** that survive the [[Coffee Filters — Paper, Metal, and Cloth|filter]].

| Factor | More body | Less body |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Filter | Metal / cloth — see [[French Press]] | Paper — see [[Chemex]] |
| Dripper | [[Kalita Wave\|flat-bottom]], slower flow | Fast [[Hario V60\|conical]] |
| Grind | Finer (more [[Fines and Boulders\|fines]]) | Coarser, cleaner |
| Roast | Darker roasts | Light roasts |

> [!tip] Body and clarity trade off
> Paper filters trap oils and fines, producing a *clean, light-bodied, transparent* cup that shows off [[Acidity in Coffee\|acidity]] and delicate flavors. Metal and cloth let more through, giving a *heavier, rounder, muddier* cup. Neither is better — it is a stylistic choice you control with equipment, explained in [[Conical vs Flat Bottom Drippers]].

## Tasting Them

Assess sweetness by what lingers pleasantly after you swallow, and body by pressing the liquid against your palate and noting its weight — the way you would distinguish skim milk from cream. Both are scored on the [[Cupping]] form, and both are named through [[The Coffee Flavor Wheel|the flavor wheel]] (caramel, honey) and the [[Describing Coffee — The Vocabulary|structural vocabulary]] (silky, syrupy, watery).
