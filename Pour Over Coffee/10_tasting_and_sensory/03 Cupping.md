# 🍵 Cupping

**Cupping** is the coffee industry's standardized method for tasting and scoring coffee. Roasters, buyers, graders, and competitors all use it because it strips away nearly every brewing variable — no [[The Dripper Explained|dripper]], no [[Pouring Technique|pour pattern]], no [[Grind Size for Pour Over|grind decision]] — leaving the coffee itself as the only thing that differs from cup to cup. When a green buyer in one country and a roaster in another need to agree on what a lot tastes like, cupping is the common language. ☕

## Why Standardize at All

A [[Hario V60|V60]] brew reflects the brewer as much as the bean. Cupping removes that confound by fixing every parameter: a set [[The Brew Ratio|ratio]], a single [[Grind Size for Pour Over|grind]], one [[Water Temperature for Brewing|temperature]], and an identical steeping time across every sample. Differences you taste must therefore come from the coffees — different [[Coffee Growing Regions and Terroir|origins]], [[Coffee Processing Methods|processes]], or [[Roast Levels for Pour Over|roasts]] — making cupping a fair comparison and a reliable diagnostic.

## The Protocol

> [!example] The standard SCA cupping setup
> - **Ratio:** ~8.25 g coffee per 150 mL water (about 1:18), weighed per cup
> - **Grind:** coarse, like sea salt — ground fresh, one sample at a time
> - **Water:** clean, ~93 °C, poured to saturate all grounds
> - **Cups:** typically 5 per sample, to catch a single tainted bean
> - **Method:** immersion — no filter, grounds steep in the cup

The sequence is fixed:

1. **Smell the dry grounds** and record fragrance.
2. **Pour water** over each cup and let it steep for ~4 minutes; a crust of grounds forms on top.
3. **Break the crust** by pushing through it three times with a spoon while leaning in to inhale the released aroma — a signature, almost ceremonial step. 👃
4. **Skim** the floating grounds and foam off the surface.
5. **Slurp** from a spoon as the coffee cools, evaluating across temperatures.

## What Gets Scored

The SCA cupping form rates each attribute on a 6–10 scale; the sum, after deductions for defective cups, yields the cup's overall score. A coffee scoring **80 points or above is "specialty" grade** — the threshold that defines the entire [[The Specialty Coffee Movement|specialty]] category.

| Attribute | What it captures |
| --- | --- |
| Fragrance / Aroma | Smell of dry grounds and wet crust |
| Flavor & Aftertaste | The core taste and what lingers |
| Acidity | Quality of brightness — see [[Acidity in Coffee]] |
| Body | Weight and texture — see [[Sweetness and Body]] |
| Balance | How the attributes integrate |
| Sweetness, Clean Cup, Uniformity | Often scored as present/absent across the 5 cups |

Tasters describe what they find using [[The Coffee Flavor Wheel]] and the shared [[Describing Coffee — The Vocabulary|vocabulary]], and flag any [[Identifying Defects and Off-Flavors|taints or faults]] that pull the score down.

> [!note] Cupping is for evaluation, not enjoyment
> An immersion cup at 1:18 is not how you would *drink* a coffee — it is a microscope. The flavors it reveals still predict how a coffee will behave in your [[Brewing Technique|pour over]], which is why even home brewers benefit from learning to cup.
