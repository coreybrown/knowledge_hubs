# 🚫 Identifying Defects and Off-Flavors

Learning what *good* coffee tastes like is only half of palate training; the other half is recognizing what is *wrong* and tracing it to a cause. Off-flavors come from two very different places: **defects baked into the coffee** before you ever touched it — at the farm, the mill, or the roaster — and **brewing faults** you create at the [[Brewing Technique|brew]] stage. The first you can only avoid by buying better; the second you can fix this morning. Telling them apart is the diagnostic skill this note builds. 🔍

## Brewing Faults — You Can Fix These

These are the most common complaints from home brewers, and almost all trace back to [[Under-Extraction and Over-Extraction|extraction]]:

| Off-flavor | Tastes like | Likely cause | Fix |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Sour | Sharp, hollow, harsh | Under-extraction | Grind finer, hotter [[Water Temperature for Brewing\|water]], longer [[Brew Time and Total Contact Time\|brew]] |
| Bitter | Acrid, lingering, dry | Over-extraction | Grind coarser, cooler water, less [[Agitation and Turbulence\|agitation]] |
| Weak / watery | Thin, flavorless | Wrong [[The Brew Ratio\|ratio]] or under-extraction | Less water per gram, finer grind |
| Astringent | Mouth-puckering, drying | Channeling / [[Fines and Boulders\|fines]] | Even [[Pouring Technique\|pour]], better grinder |
| Ashy / burnt | Smoky, charred | Dark roast or scorched roast | Lighter [[Roast Levels for Pour Over\|roast]] |

> [!warning] Sour and bitter are opposite problems
> The single most useful diagnostic in coffee: **sour means too little extraction, bitter means too much.** They sit at opposite ends of the same dial. If a cup is both — sour *and* harsh — you usually have uneven extraction from [[Bypass and Channeling|channeling]], where some grounds over-extract while others barely brew. The [[Pour Over Troubleshooting Guide]] walks through each case.

## Astringency Is Not Bitterness

A frequent confusion: **astringency** is a *tactile* drying, puckering sensation (like over-steeped tea), not a taste. It comes from certain chlorogenic acid breakdown products and excess [[Fines and Boulders|fines]], and it signals uneven or excessive extraction. Naming it correctly points you to grinder quality and pour evenness rather than to the dial of grind size alone.

## Green-Coffee and Roast Defects — You Cannot Brew These Out

Some faults are intrinsic to the bean and will haunt every cup no matter how well you brew:

- 🛢️ **Baggy / faded / woody** — stale green coffee held too long; flat, papery, cardboard notes.
- 🍷 **Ferment / fermented** — over-fermentation during [[Coffee Processing Methods|processing]]; boozy, rotten, or onion-like. A little funk is fashionable in modern naturals; *too much* is a defect — the line is contested and divides tasters.
- 🥔 **Potato defect** — a raw-potato taint linked to a bug, notably in some East African coffees.
- 🌫️ **Phenolic / medicinal** — harsh, iodine-like, often a processing or storage fault.
- **Quakers** — under-ripe beans that fail to roast properly; pale, peanutty, papery.

> [!note] Fresh ≠ flawless, and old ≠ broken
> A defect is a *quality* problem, not just an age problem. Conversely, a coffee can be perfectly clean yet taste dull simply because it is past peak [[Coffee Freshness and Degassing|freshness]] — that is staling, not a defect. Use [[Building Your Palate|side-by-side tasting]] to learn the difference.
