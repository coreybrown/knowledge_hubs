# 🫧 Coffee Freshness and Degassing

A perfectly grown, processed, and roasted coffee can still brew badly if it is too young, too old, or poorly stored. **Freshness** is a moving target: roasted coffee needs a few days to settle before it brews well, then a few weeks of peak, then a slow slide into staleness. Understanding **degassing** — the release of CO2 created during [[Coffee Roasting Explained|roasting]] — is the key to timing it.

## Degassing and the Rest Period

[[Coffee Roasting Explained|Roasting]] traps large amounts of **carbon dioxide** inside the porous bean. Over the days after roasting, that CO2 slowly escapes — this is **degassing**. It matters for brewing because excess CO2:

- creates the vigorous **[[The Bloom|bloom]]** when hot water first hits the grounds, and
- if there is too much of it, **repels water** and causes uneven, [[Under-Extraction and Over-Extraction|under-extracted]] cups.

That is why fresh-off-the-roaster coffee tastes oddly thin and gassy, and why **resting** improves it. See [[CO2, Degassing, and the Bloom Science]] for the deeper mechanism.

> [!tip] Optimal rest after roast
> A good rule of thumb for **filter roasts**: rest whole beans **4–14 days** after the roast date before brewing pour over. Lighter roasts often peak a little later (a week or more); darker roasts degas faster and peak sooner. Trust your palate — if the bloom is explosive and the cup tastes hollow, give it a few more days.

## The Freshness Timeline 📅

| Age after roast | State | What to expect |
|---|---|---|
| 0–3 days | Too gassy | Erratic bloom, thin, "green" cup |
| 4–14 days | Peak | Sweet, aromatic, balanced |
| 2–5 weeks | Good | Slowly fading; still enjoyable |
| 6+ weeks | Staling | Flat, papery, dull; lost aromatics |

## Staling and How to Slow It

Three enemies degrade roasted coffee: **oxygen** (oxidation, the main cause of stale, cardboard flavors), **moisture**, and **heat/light**. Aromatics escape and lipids go rancid over time, leaving a flat, lifeless cup no [[Recipe Variables and Cup Outcomes|recipe]] can fix.

> [!tip] Storage best practice
> - Buy **whole bean** and grind just before brewing — ground coffee stales in *minutes to hours* because of the huge surface area (see [[Grind Size and Surface Area]]).
> - Keep beans in an **airtight, opaque container** at room temperature, away from heat and light.
> - One-way **valve bags** let CO2 out while keeping O2 out — store coffee in its bag, sealed, if it has a valve.
> - Buy in quantities you'll finish in **3–4 weeks**.

> [!warning] Two freshness myths
> **1. "Freeze your daily coffee."** Long-term freezing of unopened, well-sealed coffee can preserve it, but repeatedly taking a bag in and out of the freezer invites condensation and moisture damage. **2. "Oily beans are fresh."** Surface oil indicates a dark roast or advancing staleness, not freshness — see [[Roast Levels for Pour Over]].

## Why It Matters for Pour Over

Freshness shows up most visibly in the [[The Bloom|bloom]]: a lively dome of foam means active degassing and a recently roasted bean, while a flat, lifeless bloom suggests stale coffee. Always check the **roast date** on the bag — see [[Reading a Coffee Bag Label]] — and prefer roasters who print it over those who hide behind a distant "best before" date.
