# 🎯 Single Origin vs Blends

When you buy coffee, you face a basic fork: a **single origin** that showcases one place, or a **blend** that combines several for a designed effect. Pour over culture overwhelmingly favors single origin — but understanding both, and the marketing around them, makes you a sharper buyer.

## Definitions

> [!note] What the terms actually mean
> **Single origin** means the coffee comes from one defined source — sometimes a whole country, sometimes a single farm, lot, or [[Coffee Varietals\|variety]]. The tighter the definition (single farm or micro-lot), the more **traceable** it is. **Single estate** or **micro-lot** are the most specific. A **blend** is a deliberate mix of two or more coffees — different origins, [[Coffee Processing Methods\|processes]], or [[Coffee Varietals\|varieties]] — combined by a [[Notable Roasters\|roaster]] to hit a target flavor.

## The Trade-offs ⚖️

| | Single Origin | Blend |
|---|---|---|
| **Goal** | Express one place / [[Coffee Growing Regions and Terroir\|terroir]] | Achieve a consistent, designed profile |
| **Character** | Distinctive, sometimes "spiky" | Balanced, rounded, reliable |
| **Consistency** | Varies by harvest and lot | Stable year-round |
| **Traceability** | High | Often lower |
| **Best for** | Exploration, pour over clarity | Espresso, milk drinks, everyday |

**Single origins** are about *discovery* — the blackcurrant of a [[Coffee Growing Regions and Terroir|Kenyan]], the jasmine of a washed Ethiopian, the blueberry of a [[Natural Process|natural]]. Because they express one place, they shine through the transparent pour over method and reward the palate-building work in [[How to Taste Coffee]] and [[Building Your Palate]].

**Blends** are about *design and consistency*. A roaster can balance a bright coffee against a heavy one to build sweetness, [[Sweetness and Body|body]], and a flavor that stays the same all year — invaluable for espresso, where consistency and crema matter, and for crowd-pleasing house coffees.

## Why Pour Over Culture Favors Single Origin

The whole point of pour over is **clarity and transparency** — revealing what makes a coffee unique. That aligns naturally with single origin, where there is a single, traceable story to reveal. The [[The Specialty Coffee Movement|specialty movement]] and [[Direct Trade and Coffee Sourcing|direct trade]] also champion single origin because traceability supports better prices for farmers and honest [[Reading a Coffee Bag Label|labeling]]. For learning, single origins are simply more *instructive*: each one teaches you something specific about [[Coffee Growing Regions and Terroir|terroir]], [[Coffee Varietals|variety]], or [[Coffee Processing Methods|process]].

> [!warning] Neither is "better"
> It is a myth that blends are inherently inferior. A thoughtfully built blend can be more delicious, sweet, and consistent than a one-dimensional single origin — and many legendary espresso coffees are blends. Conversely, "single origin" guarantees nothing about quality: a poorly grown, [[Identifying Defects and Off-Flavors|defective]] single origin is still bad coffee. Judge by the cup, not the category.
