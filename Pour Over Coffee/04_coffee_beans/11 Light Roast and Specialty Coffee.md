# 🌅 Light Roast and Specialty Coffee

Over the last two decades, **light roasting** went from a curiosity to the defining house style of [[The Specialty Coffee Industry|third-wave specialty coffee]]. Championed by Scandinavian roasters and spread by the [[The Specialty Coffee Movement|specialty movement]], the light-roast philosophy treats coffee like fine wine: the roaster's job is to *reveal* the bean, not to stamp it with roast flavor. For pour over drinkers this is both a flavor revolution and a technical challenge.

## What Light Roasting Is Trying to Do 🎯

The goal is **transparency** — showcasing the [[Coffee Growing Regions and Terroir|terroir]], [[Coffee Varietals|variety]], and [[Coffee Processing Methods|process]] that make a specific lot unique. By stopping the roast not long after [[Coffee Roasting Explained|first crack]], the roaster preserves:

- **High, complex [[Acidity in Coffee|acidity]]** — the bright, juicy quality specialty prizes.
- **Delicate [[Aroma and Flavor Compounds|aromatics]]** — florals, fruit, tea-like notes that dark roasting destroys.
- **Origin clarity** — so a Yirgacheffe tastes like Yirgacheffe, not "coffee."

> [!note] "Nordic roast"
> The very light end of the spectrum is often called a **Nordic** or **Scandinavian** roast, associated with roasters like Tim Wendelboe. These coffees are dropped barely past first crack — maximally origin-forward, minimally roast-forward.

## The Extraction Challenge ⚙️

> [!warning] Light roasts are harder to brew well
> Because they are **denser and less soluble** than darker roasts (see [[Roast Levels for Pour Over]]), light roasts are prone to **[[Under-Extraction and Over-Extraction\|under-extraction]]** — they come out sour, thin, grassy, and astringent when you don't pull enough flavor out of them. This is the single most common frustration for newcomers to specialty coffee.

The fixes all push extraction *up*:

| Lever | Direction for light roasts |
|---|---|
| [[Water Temperature for Brewing\|Temperature]] | Hotter — often 93–96°C, sometimes full boil |
| [[Grind Size for Pour Over\|Grind]] | Finer than you'd use for a dark roast |
| [[Agitation and Turbulence\|Agitation]] | More — stir, swirl, or pour with energy |
| [[Brew Time and Total Contact Time\|Contact time]] | Longer drawdowns help |
| [[The Brew Ratio\|Ratio]] | A touch higher ratio keeps acidity bright and clean |

Techniques built for these beans — the [[Tetsu Kasuya 4:6 Method]] and [[James Hoffmann V60 Technique]] among them — emphasize even, complete extraction. A refractometer (see [[Refractometers and Measuring Extraction]]) helps confirm you are hitting a healthy [[Extraction Yield and Strength|yield]].

## Is Lighter Always Better?

> [!warning] A matter of taste, not virtue
> The specialty world sometimes implies light roast is objectively "correct," but that is a stylistic stance, not a fact. Many excellent coffees are roasted to a balanced medium for sweetness and body, and plenty of drinkers genuinely prefer that. Light roasting also **exposes every flaw** — green-coffee [[Identifying Defects and Off-Flavors|defects]] and brewing mistakes have nowhere to hide. Brew to the cup *you* enjoy.
