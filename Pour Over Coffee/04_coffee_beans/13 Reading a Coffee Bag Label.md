# 🏷️ Reading a Coffee Bag Label

A specialty coffee bag is a dense little dossier. Learning to read it lets you predict how a coffee will taste and how to brew it before you ever open the bag, and it teaches you to spot the roasters who value **transparency** over marketing. This note ties together everything in the [[Coffee Beans and Roast|beans and roast]] domain at the point of purchase.

## Reading the Story the Label Tells

The best labels let you reconstruct the [[Coffee Beans and Roast|bean's journey]]. A label reading *"Ethiopia, Guji, 2,050 m, Heirloom, Natural, filter roast, roasted 12 May, notes of blueberry and jasmine"* tells you to expect a fruit-forward, [[Sweetness and Body|heavy-bodied]], floral cup from a [[Natural Process|natural]] [[Light Roast and Specialty Coffee|light roast]], and to brew it gently. By contrast, a vague *"Premium Dark Roast Blend, 100% Arabica"* tells you almost nothing.

> [!tip] Roast date over "best before"
> The **roast date** is the single most useful thing on the bag. A roaster who prints it respects freshness; one who only prints a far-off "best before" is hiding age. Aim to buy coffee roasted within the last couple of weeks. See [[Coffee Freshness and Degassing]].

> [!warning] Marketing red flags
> - **"100% Arabica"** is about species, not quality (see [[The Coffee Plant]]).
> - **"Italian / French / Espresso roast"** are roast-*style* names, not origins, and usually mean dark.
> - **No roast date** = treat as old until proven otherwise.
> - **"Strong / bold"** describes [[Roast Levels for Pour Over\|roast]] marketing, not [[Extraction Yield and Strength\|actual strength]] (which your [[The Brew Ratio\|ratio]] controls).

## Putting It to Use at the Brewer

Once you've decoded the label, it should shape your [[Recipe Variables and Cup Outcomes|recipe]]: a high-altitude, lightly roasted washed coffee wants hotter [[Water Temperature for Brewing|water]] and a finer [[Grind Size for Pour Over|grind]]; a natural or experimental lot wants gentler [[Agitation and Turbulence|agitation]]. The label is the bridge between buying well and brewing well. A good [[Single Origin vs Blends|single origin]] label gives you far more of this information than a typical blend.
