# 🌍 Coffee Growing Regions and Terroir

**Terroir** — borrowed from wine — is the idea that *place* leaves a fingerprint on flavor. A coffee's origin, altitude, soil, rainfall, and climate all shape the chemistry of the bean before it is ever [[Coffee Processing Methods|processed]] or [[Coffee Roasting Explained|roasted]]. Combined with [[Coffee Varietals|variety]], terroir is why a Kenyan and a Colombian can taste like different fruits entirely.

## The Bean Belt

Coffee grows in a band roughly between the Tropics of Cancer and Capricorn, where the climate is warm but tempered by altitude. **Higher elevation** is the single most important terroir factor for [[The Coffee Plant — Arabica and Robusta|Arabica]]: cooler temperatures slow the cherry's maturation, letting sugars and acids concentrate into denser, more complex beans. This is why bags proudly print altitude (often as **MASL**, metres above sea level) — see [[Reading a Coffee Bag Label]].

> [!note] What "hard bean" means
> Terms like **SHB** (Strictly Hard Bean) or **SHG** (Strictly High Grown) on Central American coffees simply mean the beans grew above ~1,350 m. The cooler, slower growth makes them physically denser — which also means they often need a hotter [[Water Temperature for Brewing|brew temperature]] and a touch finer [[Grind Size for Pour Over|grind]].

## Classic Regional Profiles 🗺️

These are useful generalizations, not laws — processing and variety can override them.

| Region | Origin examples | Typical character |
|---|---|---|
| **East Africa** | Ethiopia, Kenya | Floral, citrus, berry, bright [[Acidity in Coffee\|acidity]] |
| **Central America** | Guatemala, Costa Rica | Balanced, chocolate, caramel, clean |
| **South America** | Colombia, Brazil | Nutty, cocoa, soft acidity, full [[Sweetness and Body\|body]] |
| **Asia / Pacific** | Sumatra, Java, PNG | Earthy, herbal, syrupy, low acidity |

**Ethiopia**, coffee's birthplace, gives delicate jasmine-and-stone-fruit cups when [[Washed Process|washed]] and explosive blueberry when [[Natural Process|natural]]. **Kenya** delivers blackcurrant intensity thanks to [[Coffee Varietals|SL28/SL34]] and meticulous washing. **Brazil**, the world's largest producer, leans nutty and chocolatey with low acidity — a backbone for [[Single Origin vs Blends|blends]]. **Sumatra's** distinctive earthy body comes partly from its unusual *giling basah* (wet-hulled) processing, not terroir alone.

> [!warning] Terroir is collaborative, not deterministic
> It is a myth that origin alone decides flavor. A famously "fruity" region produces clean, tea-like lots when [[Washed Process|washed]] and grown high; a "balanced" region can taste wild when [[Natural Process|naturally]] processed. Always read terroir alongside [[Coffee Processing Methods|process]] and [[Coffee Varietals|variety]].

## Why It Matters for Pour Over

Because pour over is so transparent, terroir comes through vividly. Knowing a coffee's origin helps you anticipate its acidity and body and dial a [[Recipe Variables and Cup Outcomes|recipe]] accordingly — a bright Kenyan loves a higher [[The Brew Ratio|ratio]] to keep it juicy, while an earthy Sumatran can take a heavier hand. This is also where [[Direct Trade and Coffee Sourcing|sourcing]] and [[Sustainability in Coffee|sustainability]] meet the cup, since terroir is also a community and a livelihood.
