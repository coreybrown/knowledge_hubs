# 🍒 Coffee Processing Methods

Between a ripe coffee cherry picked off a tree and a green bean ready to [[Coffee Roasting Explained|roast]] lies **processing**: the post-harvest work of removing the fruit and drying the seed. It sounds like mere logistics, but it is one of the most powerful flavor decisions in coffee, often shaping the cup as much as [[Coffee Growing Regions and Terroir|terroir]] or [[Coffee Varietals|variety]]. This note maps the landscape; the detailed methods each have their own page.

## Anatomy of a Coffee Cherry

To understand processing you have to know what is being removed. From the outside in:

1. **Skin (exocarp)**: the cherry's outer fruit skin.
2. **Pulp (mesocarp)**: sweet, sticky fruit flesh, including the **mucilage**.
3. **Parchment (endocarp)**: a papery hull around the seed.
4. **Silverskin**: a thin membrane (becomes [[Coffee Roasting Explained|roasting]] chaff).
5. **The seed**: the green bean itself.

Processing decides **how much fruit stays in contact with the seed, and for how long, while it dries.** More fruit contact generally means more sweetness, body, and fermentation-driven flavor; less means more clarity and cleaner [[Acidity in Coffee|acidity]].

## The Three Main Families 🗺️

| Method | What happens | Cup tendency |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Washed Process\|Washed]]** | Fruit removed *before* drying | Clean, bright, transparent |
| **[[Natural Process\|Natural]]** | Cherry dried *whole*, fruit on | Fruity, heavy, wild |
| **[[Honey and Experimental Processing\|Honey]]** | Some mucilage left on to dry | Between the two; rounded sweetness |

These three sit on a spectrum of fruit contact, from least (washed) to most (natural). The modern frontier, **anaerobic** and **carbonic maceration** ferments, pushes that spectrum further and is covered in [[Honey and Experimental Processing]].

> [!note] Fermentation is the hidden hero
> Nearly every method involves controlled **fermentation**, where microbes break down sugars and create new flavor compounds. Done well it adds complexity and sweetness; done poorly it creates [[Identifying Defects and Off-Flavors|defects]] like ferment-y, boozy, or vinegary off-notes. Process is a balance of intention and risk.

## Why It Matters for Pour Over

Because pour over is a high-clarity brewing method, it reveals process character with unusual honesty. A [[Washed Process|washed]] coffee gives a crisp, see-through cup that flatters a delicate [[Coffee Varietals|variety]]; a [[Natural Process|natural]] brings jammy fruit and heavier [[Sweetness and Body|body]]. Knowing the process on a [[Reading a Coffee Bag Label|bag label]] tells you what to expect and how to brew. Naturals and experimentals often benefit from gentler [[Agitation and Turbulence|agitation]] to keep their intensity from tipping into muddiness.

> [!warning] Don't assume "natural = better"
> Process is a stylistic choice, not a quality ranking. Some of the world's cleanest, most expensive coffees are washed; some are natural. Match the process to the flavors *you* enjoy.
