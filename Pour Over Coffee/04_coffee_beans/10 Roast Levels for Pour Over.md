# 🎚️ Roast Levels for Pour Over

Roast level is the bean variable that most directly changes *how you brew*. It runs along a continuous spectrum from light to dark, set by how far past [[Coffee Roasting Explained|first crack]] the roaster takes the bean. For pour over and filter brewing in general, the specialty world leans **light to medium**. This note explains why, and how roast reshapes [[The Science of Extraction|extraction]].

## The Spectrum

| Roast | Color | Acidity | Body | Best showcases | Common use |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Light** | Light brown, dry | High, bright | Lighter | Origin, [[Coffee Varietals\|variety]], [[Coffee Processing Methods\|process]] | Specialty filter |
| **Medium** | Mid brown, dry | Balanced | Fuller | Balance, sweetness | Versatile, blends |
| **Medium-dark** | Dark brown, faint sheen | Lower | Heavy | Chocolate, caramel | Espresso, diner drip |
| **Dark** | Very dark, oily | Minimal | Roast-dominated | Roast flavor itself | Espresso, dark-roast fans |

## Why Filter Brewing Favors Lighter Roasts ✨

Pour over is a **high-clarity, origin-revealing** method, and its whole purpose is to showcase what makes a coffee distinctive: the floral [[Coffee Varietals|Geisha]], the blackcurrant [[Coffee Growing Regions and Terroir|Kenyan]], the blueberry [[Natural Process|natural]]. Lighter roasts preserve those delicate, [[Aroma and Flavor Compounds|aromatic]] and acidic compounds that darker roasting burns away. Conversely, dark roasts mute origin in favor of generic "roasty" flavor, which is better suited to milk drinks and [[AeroPress|espresso]] than to a transparent filter cup. This is why most [[The Specialty Coffee Industry|specialty roasters]] sell their single origins at a filter-friendly light-to-medium level.

## How Roast Changes Extraction

> [!tip] Lighter roast = harder to extract
> Lighter roasts are **denser, less porous, and less soluble** because they spent less time developing. To get enough flavor out, they generally want **hotter [[Water Temperature for Brewing\|water]] (often 93–96°C), a finer [[Grind Size for Pour Over\|grind]], and more [[Agitation and Turbulence\|agitation]]**. Darker roasts are brittle and very soluble, so they extract fast and easily, and you should back off with **cooler water, a coarser grind, and gentler pours** to avoid bitterness.

This single relationship (roast level driving temperature, grind, and agitation) is one of the most useful mental models in all of brewing. When you switch beans and your [[Recipe Variables and Cup Outcomes|recipe]] suddenly tastes off, roast level is the first thing to check.

> [!warning] Two persistent myths
> **1. "Dark roast = strong / high caffeine."** False. [[Extraction Yield and Strength\|Strength]] is set by your [[The Brew Ratio\|brew ratio]], not roast color, and dark roasts actually have *slightly less* caffeine by mass than light. **2. "Oily beans are fresh."** Surface oil signals a dark roast (and often advancing staleness), not freshness. See [[Coffee Freshness and Degassing]].

## Choosing for Your Brew

For pour over, start with a **light-to-medium single origin** from a roaster who prints a [[Reading a Coffee Bag Label|roast date]]. If your cups come out thin and sour, you may be under-extracting a light roast: go finer and hotter. If they come out flat and bitter, you may be over-roasted or over-extracting: go coarser and cooler. See [[Under-Extraction and Over-Extraction]] and [[Dialing In Grind Size]].
