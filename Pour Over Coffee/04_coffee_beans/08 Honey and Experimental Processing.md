# 🍯 Honey and Experimental Processing

Between the clean [[Washed Process|washed]] and the wild [[Natural Process|natural]] sits a growing world of **honey** and **experimental** methods that deliberately tune fermentation and fruit contact to engineer specific flavors. This is the modern frontier of coffee — exciting, sometimes spectacular, and genuinely controversial.

## Honey Process

In honey processing the cherry skin is removed (as in washed) but **some or all of the sticky mucilage is left on the bean to dry** (as in natural). That mucilage — never actual honey — gives the method its name and a sweetness and body that splits the difference between the two extremes. Producers grade it by how much mucilage remains and how much sun the beans get:

| Honey type | Mucilage left | Cup tendency |
|---|---|---|
| **White / Yellow** | Least | Closer to washed; clean, light sweetness |
| **Red** | Medium | Balanced, syrupy, rounded |
| **Black** | Most | Closer to natural; heavy, fruity, intense |

Common in Costa Rica and El Salvador, honey coffees offer the rounded [[Sweetness and Body|sweetness and body]] many pour over drinkers love without going as far as a full natural.

## The Experimental Frontier 🧪

The last decade has seen producers borrow tools from winemaking and brewing to control fermentation precisely:

- **Anaerobic fermentation** — cherries ferment in **sealed, oxygen-free tanks**, often pressurized, producing intense, sometimes funky tropical and boozy notes.
- **Carbonic maceration** — borrowed from Beaujolais wine, whole cherries ferment in **CO2-saturated** tanks, yielding vivid, candy-like fruit.
- **Thermal shock, inoculated yeasts, co-fermentation** — adding specific microbes or even fruit/spices to steer flavor.

> [!example] Why competitors love them
> Many recent [[The World Brewers Cup\|World Brewers Cup]] routines feature anaerobic or carbonic lots precisely because they deliver loud, memorable, easily-described flavors that score well — see [[Competition Recipes]].

## The Controversy ⚠️

> [!warning] Process flavor vs coffee flavor
> Critics argue that aggressive ferments can **mask terroir and variety**, making a Colombian and an Ethiopian taste like the same "tutti-frutti" novelty. Some co-fermented lots blur the line between coffee and a flavored beverage, raising questions of transparency on the [[Reading a Coffee Bag Label\|label]]. Inconsistency and runaway fermentation also risk [[Identifying Defects and Off-Flavors\|defects]]. Whether these coffees represent the future or a fad is a live debate in [[The Specialty Coffee Industry\|specialty coffee]].

## Brewing These Coffees

Experimental lots are usually **bold and aromatic**, so restraint pays. Favor gentle [[Agitation and Turbulence|agitation]] and a clean [[Coffee Filters — Paper, Metal, and Cloth|filter]] to keep the cup articulate, and treat them like intense [[Natural Process|naturals]] when dialing your [[Recipe Variables and Cup Outcomes|recipe]]. They are best enjoyed [[Light Roast and Specialty Coffee|lightly roasted]], which preserves the very ferment-driven aromatics they were made for.
