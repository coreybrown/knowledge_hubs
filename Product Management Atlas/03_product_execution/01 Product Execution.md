# Product Execution

> [!quote] The definition I use
> **Product Execution** — *Define, build, and launch exceptional products.*

This is the first of the four competency areas in [[The PM Competency Model]], and I put it first on purpose. Execution is the foundation the entire PM career is built on. Strategy, vision, influence — all of it is theory until something ships and changes a number. I've watched brilliant strategists wash out because they couldn't get anything across the line, and I've watched relentless executors get promoted past them. The order is not an accident.

## My point of view: execution is the one thing a PM can never be bad at

You can be spiky everywhere else. The [[The PM Competency Model|competency model]] is explicit that you should be — nobody is great at all twelve, and chasing a flat profile is a recipe for being mediocre at everything ([[The T-Shaped PM and Knowing Your Shape]]). But execution is the exception I'll fight for. A PM who can't define a clear spec, ship to plan, and hold a quality bar isn't a junior PM — they're not yet doing the job. Everything else is a multiplier on a base of *can this person reliably get good product out the door.* If the base is zero, the multiplier doesn't matter.

This is also why execution dominates the early ladder. At [[Associate Product Manager|APM]] and [[Product Manager|PM]] levels, you are largely *judged* on execution — can you spec a feature, work with engineering, and ship it without it falling over. Strategy and influence are where careers *mature* ([[The Product Career Ladder]]), but they're built on top of an execution reputation you earn in years one through four.

## Where execution connects

Execution doesn't live in a vacuum. Good [[Feature Specification|specs]] are downstream of good [[Continuous Discovery|discovery]] — you can't define what to build until you understand the problem and the [[The Four Big Product Risks|four big risks]]. What you choose to execute on is a [[Prioritization Frameworks|prioritization]] decision. And the whole point of shipping is to move a [[Business Outcome Ownership|business outcome]], which is why [[Outcomes Over Outputs]] sits at the end of this domain rather than the start of the strategy one.

> [!warning] The trap at the top
> Senior PMs often try to "graduate" from execution into pure strategy. The best leaders I've worked with never do. They stop doing the spec themselves and start raising the *team's* execution bar — better processes, better reviews, fewer surprises. The work changes altitude; it never stops being your responsibility.
