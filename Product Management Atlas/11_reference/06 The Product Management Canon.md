# The Product Management Canon

The reading list I actually recommend — not everything ever written about product, just the work that shaped how I think and that I'd hand to a PM at any level. I've ordered it roughly by where it lands in a career, and I say why each one matters. Read fewer of these more deeply than all of them once.

> [!tip] How to use a reading list
> Don't binge. Pick one book that maps to your current gap (use [[Identifying Growth Areas]]), read it slowly, and *apply* one idea before moving on. A book you applied beats ten you skimmed.

## The foundations

**Marty Cagan — *INSPIRED* (and *EMPOWERED*).** *INSPIRED* is the closest thing the field has to a canonical text on how strong product teams work; *EMPOWERED* is its sequel on product leadership. If you read one PM book, read *INSPIRED*. It's the source of [[The Four Big Product Risks]] (value, usability, feasibility, viability), the [[Product Teams vs Feature Teams|product-vs-feature-team]] distinction, and the line I repeat most: we want *competent people with character*. *EMPOWERED* picks up where most ladders go thin — the craft of [[Team Leadership|leading]] and coaching product people. Both are by Cagan and the SVPG partners.

**Teresa Torres — *Continuous Discovery Habits*.** The most practical book on discovery I know. Torres turns "talk to users" into a weekly habit owned by the [[Continuous Discovery|product trio]], and gives you the [[The Opportunity Solution Tree|Opportunity Solution Tree]] to connect outcomes to solutions. If your problem is "we build things nobody wanted," this is the fix.

**Melissa Perri — *Escaping the Build Trap*.** Perri names the disease — shipping features instead of moving outcomes — and prescribes the cure. It's the book I give anyone whose org confuses being busy with making progress. The whole of [[Outcomes Over Outputs]] lives here.

## Strategy and judgment

**Dan Olsen — *The Lean Product Playbook*.** The clearest step-by-step on product-market fit and the [[Build-Measure-Learn and the MVP|MVP]] I've found. Olsen's Product-Market Fit Pyramid is a genuinely usable model when you're starting from zero. Tactical where Cagan is philosophical.

**Gibson Biddle — DHM and the strategy talks.** Biddle (ex-Netflix) isn't primarily a book, but his [[The DHM Model|DHM model]] — strategy should **D**elight, be **H**ard to copy, and be **M**argin-enhancing — is the sharpest one-line test for a product strategy I use. His "How to Define Your Product Strategy" essays are required reading for anyone moving into [[Strategic Impact|strategy]].

## Leveling, growth, and the PM as a person

**Petra Wille — *Strong Product People*.** The best book on the *human* side: coaching, growth, and self-awareness for PMs and their leaders. Wille's [[The PM Wheel|PMwheel]] is a self-assessment radar, and her thesis — *great product leaders make strong product people* — underwrites the entire [[Assessment and Coaching]] domain. This is the one most reading lists miss.

**Ravi Mehta & Reforge — the competency model.** The intellectual backbone of this Atlas. Mehta's "Peak Product Manager" work (with Reforge) is where the [[The PM Competency Model|4 areas × 12 competencies]] come from, and where the [[The T-Shaped PM and Knowing Your Shape|spiky-not-well-rounded]] idea is best argued. Reforge's broader courses are the most rigorous paid PM education going, if you can get access.

## The newsletters and the modern layer

**Lenny Rachitsky — *Lenny's Newsletter*.** The default starting point for the field today. Lenny's compiled [[The Product Career Ladder|career ladders]] from dozens of companies, ranks the core skills (communication → execution → product sense), and his guest essays are a rolling syllabus. If you subscribe to one thing, subscribe to this.

**Shreyas Doshi.** Not a book — threads and talks — but his frameworks punch above their weight: the [[The Three Levels of Product Work|three levels of product work]] (impact / execution / optics), the [[The LNO Framework|LNO]] effort model, and "most execution problems are strategy problems." Read him when you feel busy but not impactful.

**John Cutler.** Coined the [[Outcomes Over Outputs|"feature factory"]] anti-pattern and popularized the [[The North Star Framework|North Star Framework]]. Sharp, contrarian, and good for breaking lazy product habits.

> [!note] The AI-PM voices (the new canon, forming now)
> The AI shift is being written in real time on Substack and in courses, not books yet. The voices I track: **Lenny Rachitsky** and **Reforge** ("Moving to Higher Ground," the AI Evals course) on how AI reshapes the job; **Aakash Gupta** on AI product sense and [[Writing Evals for AI Products|evals]]; **Claire Vo** (ChatPRD, "Product Management Is Dead") on the generalist-specialist shift; **Kevin Weil** (OpenAI) on evals as a core PM skill; **Cat Wu** (Anthropic) on building on a moving model; and **Ethan Mollick** (*Co-Intelligence*) on the [[The 70% Problem|jagged frontier]]. This is the reading list for [[AI and the Modern PM]].

## A note on the "CEO of the product" books

A lot of older PM writing leans on the "PM is the CEO of the product" framing. I think it's actively misleading and I argue against it in [[Myths and Misconceptions]]. Read those books for tactics if you like, but don't internalize the metaphor.
