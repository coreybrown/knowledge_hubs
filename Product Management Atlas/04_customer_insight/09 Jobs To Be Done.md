# Jobs To Be Done

**Jobs To Be Done (JTBD)** is the lens I reach for when a team is drowning in feature requests and demographic personas and still can't explain *why* people actually use the product. The core idea, popularized by **Clayton Christensen** and developed in parallel by **Tony Ulwick** (Outcome-Driven Innovation): **customers don't buy products; they "hire" them to do a job.** Your product is competing not with the obvious rivals but with every other way a customer might get that job done — including doing nothing.

## The milkshake, briefly

Christensen's famous example: a chain couldn't sell more milkshakes by tweaking flavors. Reframed by *job*, the data showed morning commuters "hiring" the shake to make a dull drive less boring and stay full until lunch — its real competitors were bananas, bagels, and boredom. Demographics were useless; the *job* explained everything. That's the whole method: stop describing *who* the customer is, start understanding *what progress they're trying to make*.

> [!tip] The job is the stable thing
> Christensen's deeper claim is that **jobs are stable over time while products and technologies churn.** People have wanted to "get from A to B quickly" forever — horses, cars, ride-share, someday robotaxis all hired for the same job. Anchoring strategy to the *job* rather than the current solution is what lets you see disruption coming and keeps your roadmap from being a list of competitor features. This is a close cousin of [[Outcomes Over Outputs|outcome thinking]].

## The shape of a job statement

A well-formed job is solution-agnostic and built around progress:

> **When** [situation], **I want to** [motivation], **so I can** [expected outcome].

For example: *"When I'm watching a game with friends and the odds shift, I want to place a bet in seconds, so I can act before the moment passes."* No app, no feature, no demographic — deliberately, so the solution space stays wide open. It also exposes the three layers of a job: **functional** (place the bet fast), **emotional** (don't look slow in front of friends), and **social** (be the one who called it). Most products over-serve the functional and ignore the other two, which is where loyalty actually lives.

## JTBD vs personas — the contrast I care about

This is where the framework is best known, and where I have a strong view.

| | Persona | Job To Be Done |
|---|---|---|
| **Organizes around** | *Who* the user is (attributes, demographics) | *What* the user is trying to accomplish |
| **Stability** | Drifts as your audience shifts | Stable across time and technology |
| **Failure mode** | "Marketing Mary, 34, likes yoga" — fiction that predicts nothing | Over-engineering a niche job nobody pays for |
| **Best for** | Building team empathy; design grounding | Strategy, positioning, finding non-obvious competitors |

My take: **personas tell you who's in the room; jobs tell you why they came.** They're complementary, but when forced to choose, the job is the more durable foundation for *strategy* — a persona built on demographics will happily predict the wrong thing (the milkshake buyers spanned every demographic). I use jobs for "what business are we even in," and [[User Personas and Journey Mapping|personas and journeys]] to ground day-to-day design. The danger with personas is they become theater; with JTBD, an abstract framework debate that never touches a real decision.

## How I actually use it

- To find the **real competitor** — what does the customer do *today* to get this job done? Usually it's not who you think; often it's a spreadsheet, a text to a friend, or nothing.
- To frame **opportunities** in an [[The Opportunity Solution Tree|Opportunity Solution Tree]] — unmet jobs and under-served outcomes are exactly the opportunity branches.
- To write **outcome-based** product goals instead of feature lists, feeding [[Strategic Impact]].
- As the thing to *listen for* in a [[User Research and Discovery|customer interview]] — ask about the last time they did the job, not whether they'd like your feature.

## How AI is changing it

AI is good at the first pass of job discovery — read a thousand reviews and support tickets and it'll cluster recurring "I was trying to…" patterns into candidate jobs faster than any analyst. That accelerates surfacing the functional job. But the emotional and social layers, and the judgment to tell a real, fundable job from a plausible-sounding cluster, remain human work — a model will confidently name a "job" that's really an artifact of how people phrase complaints. Use it to widen the net, not to make the call. See [[AI in Discovery and Research]].
