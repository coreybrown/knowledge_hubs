# User Experience Design

## What it is

The verbatim definition from my competency model: **the ability, individually and with the design team, to define requirements and deliver UX designs that are easy to use, leverage UX best practices, and dovetail with the product's predominant UX patterns.**

Notice what this competency is *not*. It is not "the PM does the design". I have never met a great PM who wanted to push pixels, and the ones who try usually make worse products. The phrase that matters is **"individually and with the design team."** The PM owns the *problem* and the *requirements* and is accountable for the experience being usable. The designer owns the *craft* of the solution. Confusing those two is the fastest way I know to a resentful designer and a mediocre product. In Marty Cagan's framing of [[The Four Big Product Risks]], usability is the designer's risk to own, and the PM's job is to make sure it gets owned, not to own it instead.

## The behaviors

My model names two behaviors, and the second one surprises people in a UX competency.

**Design Sense & Feedback.** Advocating intuitive, user-centered design; giving clear context, feedback, and **problem framing**; balancing usability against feasibility and impact; championing **consistency and simplicity**; and iterating from feedback. The keyword is *problem framing*, because a PM's most valuable design contribution is a sharply framed problem and clear constraints, not a sketch of the solution. How to do this without being a designer is the whole of [[Design Sense and Critique]].

**Competitive Landscape Expertise.** Monitoring competitors and trends; analyzing strengths, weaknesses, and positioning; spotting **differentiation**; and translating market context into product priorities and messaging. This sits inside the UX competency in my model because so much of UX is *relative*. Users judge your experience against every other app on their phone, and a PM who does not know the competitive bar is designing blind. The full method is in [[Competitive Landscape Analysis]].

> [!warning] "Make it pop" is not feedback
> The cardinal sin of PM design feedback is prescribing the solution: "move this button, make it blue, add a carousel". That tells the designer *what* without *why*, strips them of the craft they are better at than you, and usually fixes a symptom rather than the cause. Useful feedback names the **problem** ("users aren't noticing this action") and the **constraint** ("and it has to work one-handed"), then trusts the designer to solve it. More in [[Design Sense and Critique]].

## How it shows up by level

| Level | What User Experience Design looks like |
|---|---|
| [[Associate Product Manager\|APM]] | Writes clear requirements for one feature; gives basic, problem-framed feedback; knows the product's core patterns. |
| [[Product Manager\|PM]] | Partners a designer through an area; champions consistency and simplicity; weighs usability vs feasibility; tracks key competitors. |
| [[Senior Product Manager\|Sr PM]] | Sets the experience bar for a product area; runs effective design critique; uses competitive insight to find differentiation. |
| [[Director and VP of Product\|Director/VP]] | Stewards design quality and pattern consistency across the org; builds the PM-design partnership as a cultural norm. |

## How to grow it

- **Learn to critique, not redesign.** Practice giving feedback that names the problem and leaves the solution to the designer. This is the highest-leverage UX skill a PM can build ([[Design Sense and Critique]]).
- **Build a pattern vocabulary.** You do not need to design, but you should be able to name the product's predominant patterns and notice when a new screen breaks them. Inconsistency is a usability tax your users pay.
- **Audit competitors with your thumbs, not your eyes.** Actually *use* three competitor products for a real task each month. Reading reviews is not the same as feeling the friction ([[Competitive Landscape Analysis]]).
- **Sit in usability tests.** Watching real users fail at your design rewires your instincts faster than any best-practices doc ([[User Research and Discovery]]).

## How AI is changing it

The biggest shift is that PMs can now *prototype* the experience themselves. Tools like v0 and Lovable turn a prompt into a working interface in minutes, so a PM can communicate intent as a clickable artifact instead of a wall of text. That raises the floor on a PM's design contribution and tightens the loop with the designer. It does not replace design sense or craft. A generated UI is a starting point that still needs a designer's judgment on hierarchy, accessibility, and fit with the product's patterns. The premium stays on **taste**, on knowing which of a hundred AI-generated options is actually good. See [[AI in Prototyping and Delivery]].
