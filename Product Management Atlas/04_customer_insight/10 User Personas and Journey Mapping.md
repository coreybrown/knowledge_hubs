# User Personas and Journey Mapping

I'll lead with the controversial part: **most personas are theater.** A laminated card reading "Marketing Mary, 34, drives a Subaru, loves yoga and oat-milk lattes" tells you nothing about what to build and gives the team a false sense that they've "done the user work." I've sat through too many kickoffs where a beautiful persona deck stood in for actual understanding. So this note is about the narrow conditions under which these tools are genuinely useful — and the much wider conditions under which they're a waste of a designer's afternoon.

## What they are

**Personas** are composite, archetypal users built from research — a named, humanized stand-in for a segment, capturing goals, contexts, behaviors, and pain points. **Journey maps** chart a persona's end-to-end experience accomplishing something: the stages they move through, what they do and feel at each, and where the experience breaks. Done right, both are *artifacts that carry research into the team's daily decisions* — the "comprehension" half of [[Voice of the Customer]].

## When personas help vs become theater

> [!warning] The test for a real persona
> A persona is only worth making if it would **change a product decision**. The honest test: cover the name and photo — does anything left on the card change what you'd build? If it's just demographics and hobbies, no. If it's goals, contexts, and behaviors that distinguish how this person uses the product, yes. Demographics are usually decoration; **behavior and goals** are the signal.

| Personas *help* when… | Personas are *theater* when… |
|---|---|
| Built from real research, updated as you learn | Invented in a workshop from assumptions |
| Centered on goals, contexts, behaviors | Centered on demographics, hobbies, a stock photo |
| Used to settle "who is this *for*" in real debates | Made for a deck, then never opened again |
| Few, sharp, and distinct | A dozen overlapping near-identical cards |

My rule: build the *fewest* personas that capture *behaviorally distinct* ways people use the product, ground every one in research, and kill any you can't tie to a decision. A persona built on assumptions doesn't reduce risk — it *encodes* your bias and then lends it the authority of a printed artifact, which is worse than no persona at all.

## Where I find journey maps more useful than personas

In my experience **journey maps earn their keep more reliably than personas do**, because the act of mapping is itself diagnostic. Laying out the real end-to-end flow — including the parts before and after your product touches the user — surfaces the gaps, the hand-off cracks, and the moments of friction a feature-by-feature view hides. The map points your [[Product Analytics and Metrics|funnel]] at *where* drop-off happens and your [[User Research and Discovery|research]] at *why*. The value is in the *making*, not the poster on the wall.

Two things separate a useful journey map from a decorative one:

- **It's grounded in observed behavior**, not how you wish the flow went. Map what users *actually* do (warts, workarounds, abandonment), not the happy path.
- **It crosses the edges of your product.** The most valuable insights live in what the user does *before* they arrive and *after* they leave — the parts you don't control but that shape whether your bit works.

## How these relate to JTBD

I get asked whether personas compete with [[Jobs To Be Done]]. They don't — they answer different questions. A persona describes *who* is doing the job; the job describes *what progress* they want; the journey map describes *how* the experience of getting there unfolds. I use jobs for strategy and positioning, personas for grounding day-to-day design empathy, and journeys for finding where the experience breaks. The mistake is treating any one as the whole picture.

## How AI is changing it

AI will happily generate a full persona set and a journey map from a one-line prompt — which is exactly the danger, because it produces *plausible fiction* at scale, the laminated-card problem industrialized. Used well, AI does the opposite: synthesize *real* interviews and support data into evidence-backed personas, and draft a journey map you then validate against actual behavior. The discriminator is whether the artifact is grounded in research or hallucinated from priors — and that judgment is the PM's. See [[AI in Discovery and Research]].
