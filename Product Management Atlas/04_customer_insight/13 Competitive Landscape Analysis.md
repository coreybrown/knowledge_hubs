# Competitive Landscape Analysis

My contrarian opinion on competitive analysis: **the goal is not to copy competitors — it's to know exactly where you'll *refuse* to compete.** Most competitive work I see is a feature-parity spreadsheet that turns the roadmap into catch-up and the product into a beige average of everyone else. Useful analysis does the opposite: it tells you what to *ignore* so you can be sharply better at what matters. This is the "Competitive Landscape Expertise" behavior of [[User Experience Design]].

## Why this lives in Customer Insight

It feeds strategy, but I treat it as customer insight because **users don't experience your product in a vacuum.** Every interaction is judged against the other apps on their phone. A betting app that feels slow isn't slow in the abstract; it's slow *relative to the one they used last night*. You can't understand how customers perceive your experience without understanding the alternatives they compare it to. Competitive analysis is the market half of understanding the customer.

## Who your competitors actually are

The first mistake is too narrow a definition. Through a [[Jobs To Be Done]] lens, your competitor is **anything the customer hires to do the same job** — including:

- **Direct** competitors (the obvious rivals doing the same thing).
- **Indirect** substitutes (a different product solving the same job — a group chat instead of your social feature).
- **The status quo** — doing nothing, or a manual workaround. The most underrated competitor, and usually the one you're actually losing to: "we lost the deal to a spreadsheet" is the most common loss there is.

## What to monitor, and what to ignore

> [!tip] Watch the moves that change the customer's expectations
> You don't need to track every competitor's every release — that's how you become a reactive feature factory. Track two things: moves that **reset table stakes** (a rival makes one-tap something users now expect everywhere) and moves that signal a **strategic bet** (a pivot, a new segment, a pricing change). The rest is noise. Filter for what shifts the customer's baseline, not everything.

A few structured lenses I actually use — [[SWOT and Porter's Five Forces|SWOT and Porter]] are worth knowing, but I keep it lighter day to day:

- **Feature/experience teardown** — periodically *use* competitor products for a real job (thumbs, not slides — see [[Design Sense and Critique]]) and map strengths, weaknesses, and gaps.
- **Positioning map** — plot players on the two axes customers actually care about; the empty quadrants are where differentiation might live.
- **Win/loss analysis** — why you actually win and lose deals or users, from the people who chose. The most honest competitive signal there is.

## From analysis to differentiation to priorities

The output of competitive work isn't a report — it's three decisions:

1. **Where we'll be *clearly* better** (our differentiation — the spike).
2. **Where we'll be *good enough*** (table stakes; meet the bar, don't over-invest).
3. **Where we'll *deliberately not play*** (ceding ground on purpose to focus our leverage).

The framework I anchor to is Gibson Biddle's **DHM**: a strong strategy should **Delight** customers in a way that's **Hard to copy** and **Margin-enhancing** ([[The DHM Model]]). Differentiation that's trivial to copy isn't differentiation; it's a head start a competitor erases next quarter. The point of knowing the landscape is to find the *durable* delight, then translate it into [[Prioritization Frameworks|priorities]] and [[Storytelling with Data|messaging]].

> [!warning] The feature-parity death spiral
> Chasing parity on every competitor feature is how products die slowly. You spend your roadmap matching others, ship a little later and a little worse on each, and end up with no spike of your own — a worse copy of three different products. Competitive analysis should produce *focus and a point of view*, not a longer backlog. If your competitive work only ever adds to the to-do list, you're doing it wrong.

## How AI is changing it

AI lowers the cost of monitoring sharply: it can watch competitor release notes, reviews, pricing pages, and social chatter continuously and summarize the shifts, turning competitive intelligence from a quarterly scramble into a standing feed. That's real leverage on the *gathering*. What it can't do is decide where *you* refuse to compete or what differentiation is durable for *your* strategy — and a tool surfacing every competitor move makes the feature-parity trap *easier* to fall into. The judgment about what to ignore stays human. See [[AI in Discovery and Research]] and [[Competencies AI Commoditizes vs Elevates]].
