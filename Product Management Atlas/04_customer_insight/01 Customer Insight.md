# Customer Insight

This is the area where average PMs and great ones diverge. Everyone can run a standup and write a ticket. Far fewer can look at the same dashboard, the same five user interviews, and the same competitor launch — and walk out knowing *what to build next* and *why*. That's Customer Insight: in my model it's the competency area defined as **"understand and fulfill customer needs,"** and it's the engine room of [[Product Sense and Judgment]].

Here's my blunt take after a decade of B2C mobile: most product failures aren't execution failures. The team shipped the thing, on time, bug-free — and nobody wanted it. That's a *customer insight* failure dressed up as a strategy or execution problem. You can out-execute your way past a lot, but you cannot out-execute building the wrong thing. This area is your insurance against that.

## The three competencies in this area

| Competency | What it covers | The behaviors |
|---|---|---|
| [[Fluency with Data]] | Reading the quantitative truth — funnels, retention, experiments | Feature Evaluation & A/B Testing · Actionable Insight Generation |
| [[Voice of the Customer]] | Reading the *qualitative* truth — why users do what they do | User Persona & Journeys Comprehension · User Insights Application |
| [[User Experience Design]] | Turning insight into usable, well-crafted product | Design Sense & Feedback · Competitive Landscape Expertise |

The pairing of the first two is the whole point. Data tells you *what* is happening; research tells you *why*. A PM who only has one half is dangerous — the data-only PM optimizes a local maximum into the ground; the research-only PM ships heartfelt features that nobody actually uses. The job is to hold both, and let them argue. theScore's product principles put it as **"balance game sense with data"** — qual, quant, and intuition together. I still run my teams that way.

## Insight without discovery is just opinion

The competency model tells you *what* good looks like; it's thinner on the *practice* that produces insight continuously. So I treat **discovery** as a first-class discipline that sits across all three competencies:

- **[[User Research and Discovery]]** — the methods toolkit, and when to use which.
- **[[Continuous Discovery]]** — Teresa Torres' case for weekly customer contact by the team building the product, not a quarterly research project.
- **[[The Opportunity Solution Tree]]** — Torres' way of keeping discovery tied to an outcome instead of a feature wishlist.
- **[[Jobs To Be Done]]** — Christensen and Ulwick's reframe: customers "hire" products to make progress.
- **[[User Personas and Journey Mapping]]** — useful when grounded in research, theater when not.

> [!tip] The cheapest insurance in product
> Discovery is the cheapest risk reduction you will ever buy. A week of customer conversations costs you a week; a mis-built quarter costs you a quarter and the team's morale. Marty Cagan frames it as de-risking value and usability *before* you build — see [[The Four Big Product Risks]]. Insight is how you stop being a [[Product Teams vs Feature Teams|feature factory]].

## How this area escalates

At [[Associate Product Manager|APM]] you pull a funnel report and run an interview your manager designed. By Senior PM you decide *which* questions are worth answering and own the insight that reshapes the roadmap. By [[Director and VP of Product|Director/VP]] you build the research and analytics *muscle* of the whole org — instrumentation, a discovery cadence, a culture that treats "we don't actually know" as a respectable answer. The competency stays the same; the altitude climbs (see [[The Master Competency Matrix]]).

## How AI is changing this area

This is the area AI is reshaping fastest. Tools synthesize interviews, reviews, tickets, and survey free-text in hours instead of weeks, and self-serve analytics lets anyone ask the data a question in plain English. That commoditizes the *gathering*; it raises the premium on *framing the right question* and *judging which insight matters*. I dig into this in [[AI in Discovery and Research]] and [[Competencies AI Commoditizes vs Elevates]].
