# Design Sense and Critique

Here's the trap every PM falls into at least once: you open a design review, see a button you'd have placed differently, and say "can we move that to the top-right and make it blue?" Congratulations — you just did the designer's job worse than they would have, and taught them you don't trust them. **A PM's job in a design critique is to frame problems, not push pixels.** That reframe is the difference between a designer who dreads your feedback and one who seeks it out.

## You don't need to be a designer. You need design *sense*.

Design sense is not the ability to *produce* good design; it's the ability to *recognize* it and articulate *why* something does or doesn't work for the user. The two come apart completely — I can tell a confusing flow from a clear one and say exactly why without being able to draw either. That recognition, plus the discipline to express it as a problem rather than a solution, is the whole competency. It's the "Design Sense & Feedback" behavior of [[User Experience Design]].

## The cardinal rule: problem framing over solution prescription

> [!tip] Feedback as a problem, not a fix
> Bad: *"Move the button up and make it bigger."*
> Good: *"I'm worried users won't notice this is the primary action — three buttons are competing for attention. And it has to work one-handed on a phone."*
> The first hands the designer a fix and strips them of their craft. The second hands them a **problem and a constraint** and trusts them to solve it — often better than your fix would have. State what you observe and what outcome you need; leave the *how* to the person who's better at the how.

When I catch myself reaching for a solution, I run it backwards: *what problem is my fix trying to solve?* Then I say **that** instead. The fix was a symptom of a problem I hadn't named, and naming it usually produces a better answer from the designer.

## What a PM brings instead of pixels

If not pixels, what? Four things only the PM reliably holds:

- **The problem and the user** — why this screen exists, who it's for, what job they're doing ([[Jobs To Be Done]]). Designers solve better when the problem is sharp.
- **The constraints** — feasibility, scope, the [[Understanding Trade-offs|trade-offs]], the deadline. "It has to ship this sprint and work offline" is information only you may have.
- **Consistency and simplicity** — does this fit the product's predominant patterns, or invent one users must learn? Is it the *simplest* thing that solves the problem? Championing these is explicitly in the competency.
- **The data and the research** — what the [[Product Analytics and Metrics|funnel]] and [[User Research and Discovery|usability tests]] say. Evidence beats opinion in a critique every time.

The hardest line I hold: **usability is objective; taste is the designer's domain.** If a user can't complete the task, I'll escalate it. If I just prefer a different shade of green, I keep quiet — it's not my craft and it's not my call.

## Pixel-pushing is a symptom of a deeper failure

When a PM micromanages a design, it's usually because the *upstream* work was thin — the problem wasn't framed, the requirements were vague, so the PM grabs control at the pixel level. The fix isn't better critique technique; it's doing the [[Feature Specification|specification]] and [[Voice of the Customer|research]] work that should have set the designer up to succeed. Good critique starts long before the review.

## How AI is changing it

PMs can now generate clickable prototypes themselves, which changes critique in a subtle way: instead of describing a problem in prose, a PM can hand the designer a rough working artifact that says "something like this, but you make it good." That can sharpen the conversation — or, badly used, it becomes pixel-pushing with extra steps, the PM imposing a generated solution. AI can also pre-screen a design against heuristics and accessibility rules, but it can't supply *taste* — judging which of many options actually serves the user is still the human bar.
