# Team Leadership

This is the second competency in [[Influencing People]], and the one that behaves differently from all the others. Where [[Stakeholder Management]] and [[Managing Up]] start on day one, this is the one competency you cannot grow in your first few years. It needs people to lead, and junior PMs do not have any.

## What it is

> [!quote] The definition I use
> **Team Leadership**: *The ability to manage and mentor direct reports so they deliver on their product areas, improve against these competencies, deliver outcomes, and achieve career objectives.*

Notice the object: **direct reports**. This is the line where "influence" turns into "management." It carries a duty the other competencies do not, because you are now accountable not just for outcomes but for people and their careers. This is the spine of the entire manager track on the career ladder.

## The behaviors

Four named behaviors sit under this competency.

### Coaching & Performance
Actionable feedback through regular 1:1s, tailored to each person's strengths and goals, modeling strong practices yourself, and creating a safe space to learn. This is the heart of the job. Cagan calls coaching a manager's number one responsibility, worth roughly half their time. It flows into [[Coaching and Feedback]] and [[The Manager as Coach]].

### Team Impact
Aligning the team's goals to the business, holding accountability for **outcomes, not activity**, and removing blockers so the team can move. The shift here is profound. You stop being measured on what *you* ship and start being measured on what your *team* achieves ([[Outcomes Over Outputs]]).

### Product-Wide Influence & Leadership
Being a company-wide advocate for the product discipline: improving processes, tools, and standards, investing in development and hiring, and fostering a healthy product culture. This is where a leader's impact stops being contained to their own team.

### Resource Management & Hiring
Defining role expectations, attracting diverse high-potential candidates, evaluating holistically, making inclusive evidence-based decisions, and aligning org structure to the work. The detailed craft lives in [[Hiring and Building a Product Team]].

## How it shows up by level

> [!warning] The leveling quirk: N/A until you lead
> In the matrix I use, Team Leadership is scored **"Not applicable" at the [[Associate Product Manager\|APM]] and Early PM levels.** You can be a fantastic individual PM with this competency at zero, and that is fine, because there is no one to lead yet. It is the clearest proof that the competency model is leveled: the same twelve competencies, but the bar, and even the relevance, changes with altitude.

| Level | What Team Leadership looks like |
|---|---|
| [[Associate Product Manager\|APM]] / Early PM | **Not applicable.** No reports; influence happens through the other competencies |
| Later [[Product Manager\|PM]] | Begins *informally*: mentoring an APM, modeling practices, no formal authority yet |
| [[Senior Product Manager\|Senior PM]] | Coaches juniors seriously; may have a dotted-line report; owns team outcomes |
| [[Group Product Manager\|GPM]] | First true people-management. Direct reports, performance, hiring; runs the operating rhythm |
| [[Director and VP of Product\|Director / VP]] | Leads managers-of-managers; sets product culture, processes, and the org's hiring bar company-wide |

> [!note] This is where the ladder forks
> Up to [[Senior Product Manager|Senior PM]], everyone grows the same way. After that, the manager track is *defined* by maxing this competency, while the IC track (Staff, Principal) scales influence and ambiguity **without** taking on reports. Choosing between them is a real decision, not a default. See [[The IC vs Manager Decision]].

## How to grow it

- **Before you have reports, mentor.** As a Later PM, take an APM under your wing. Practice the coaching muscle before it is your job.
- **Separate the two halves.** Cagan splits leadership into *management* (coaching and staffing) and *leadership* (vision and strategic context). Both matter, and they are different skills.
- **Measure the team, not yourself.** This is the hardest reflex to unlearn. Stop optimizing your own output and start removing your team's blockers ([[Outcomes Over Outputs]]).
- **Run the leveling honestly.** Use the same model on your reports that was used on you, and turn the gaps into a real plan ([[Building a Growth Plan]]).

## How AI is changing it

This is the competency AI touches least, and it is worth being clear about why. A model can draft a performance review, summarize a 1:1, or surface team-health signals, which is real mechanical leverage. What it cannot do is coach a struggling PM through a hard quarter, earn a team's trust, make a difficult people call, or build a culture. The durable core is judgment, taste, and the kind of leadership people choose to follow ([[Competencies AI Commoditizes vs Elevates]]). If anything, AI raises the premium here. As the mechanical PM work gets automated, leading the humans who do the irreducible work becomes a larger share of what a senior leader is actually for.
