# Team Leadership

The second competency in [[Influencing People]], and the one that behaves differently from all the others. Where [[Stakeholder Management]] and [[Managing Up]] start on day one, Team Leadership is **the one competency you cannot grow in your first few years** — it requires people to lead, and junior PMs don't have any.

## What it is

> [!quote] The definition I use
> **Team Leadership** — *The ability to manage and mentor direct reports so they deliver on their product areas, improve against these competencies, deliver outcomes, and achieve career objectives.*

Note the object: **direct reports.** This is the line where "influence" becomes "management." It carries a duty the other competencies don't — you're now accountable not just for outcomes, but for *people* and their careers. This is the spine of the entire manager track on the career ladder.

## The behaviors

Four named behaviors sit under this competency.

### Coaching & Performance
Actionable feedback through regular 1:1s, tailored to each person's strengths and goals; modeling strong practices yourself; creating a safe space to learn. The heart of the job — Cagan calls coaching a manager's #1 responsibility, worth ~half their time. It flows into [[Coaching and Feedback]] and [[The Manager as Coach]].

### Team Impact
Aligning the team's goals to the business, holding accountability for **outcomes, not activity**, and removing blockers so the team can move. The shift is profound: you stop being measured on what *you* ship and start being measured on what your *team* achieves ([[Outcomes Over Outputs]]).

### Product-Wide Influence & Leadership
Being a company-wide advocate for the product discipline: improving processes, tools, and standards; investing in development and hiring; fostering a healthy product culture. This is where a leader's impact stops being contained to their own team.

### Resource Management & Hiring
Defining role expectations, attracting diverse high-potential candidates, evaluating holistically, making inclusive evidence-based decisions, and aligning org structure to the work. The detailed craft lives in [[Hiring and Building a Product Team]].

## How it shows up by level

> [!warning] The leveling quirk: N/A until you lead
> In the matrix I use, Team Leadership is scored **"Not applicable" at the [[Associate Product Manager\|APM]] and Early PM levels.** You can be a fantastic individual PM with this competency at zero — and that's *fine*, because there's no one to lead yet. It's the clearest proof that the competency model is leveled: the same twelve competencies, but the bar (and even the *relevance*) changes with altitude.

| Level | What Team Leadership looks like |
|---|---|
| [[Associate Product Manager\|APM]] / Early PM | **Not applicable** — no reports; influence happens through the other competencies |
| Later [[Product Manager\|PM]] | Begins *informally* — mentoring an APM, modeling practices, no formal authority yet |
| [[Senior Product Manager\|Senior PM]] | Coaches juniors seriously; may have a dotted-line report; owns team outcomes |
| [[Group Product Manager\|GPM]] | First true people-management — direct reports, performance, hiring; runs the operating rhythm |
| [[Director and VP of Product\|Director / VP]] | Leads managers-of-managers; sets product culture, processes, and the org's hiring bar company-wide |

> [!note] This is where the ladder forks
> Up to [[Senior Product Manager|Senior PM]], everyone grows the same way. After that, the manager track is *defined* by maxing this competency, while the IC track (Staff, Principal) scales influence and ambiguity **without** taking on reports. Choosing between them is a real decision, not a default — see [[The IC vs Manager Decision]].

## How to grow it

- **Before you have reports, mentor.** As a Later PM, take an APM under your wing. Practice the coaching muscle before it's your job.
- **Separate the two halves.** Cagan splits leadership into *management* (coaching + staffing) and *leadership* (vision + strategic context). Both matter; they're different skills.
- **Measure the team, not yourself.** The hardest reflex to unlearn: stop optimizing your own output and start removing your team's blockers ([[Outcomes Over Outputs]]).
- **Run the leveling honestly.** Use the same model on your reports that was used on you, and turn the gaps into a real plan ([[Building a Growth Plan]]).

## How AI is changing it

This is the competency AI touches *least*. It can draft a performance review, summarize a 1:1, or surface team-health signals — mechanical leverage. But coaching a struggling PM, earning a team's trust, making a hard people call, building culture? None of that commoditizes; the durable human core is judgment, taste, and *leadership* ([[Competencies AI Commoditizes vs Elevates]]). If anything, AI raises the premium here: as mechanical PM work gets automated, *leading the humans who do the irreducible work* becomes a larger share of what a senior leader is for.
