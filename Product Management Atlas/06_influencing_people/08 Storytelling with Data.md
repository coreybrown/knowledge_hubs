# Storytelling with Data

This is the persuasion engine of the whole [[Influencing People]] area. It shows up by name in the **Org-Based Communications** behavior under [[Stakeholder Management]] — "storytelling + data" — and it's how a PM turns a spreadsheet into a decision. My core claim: **the story is the unit of persuasion, and data is what makes the story believable.** Neither works alone.

## Why neither half works alone

I've watched both failure modes, repeatedly.

- **Data without narrative** is a wall of charts that lets everyone draw their own conclusion — usually the one they walked in with. Numbers don't interpret themselves. A dashboard is not an argument.
- **Narrative without data** is a nice anecdote that any skeptic deflates with "do we actually know that?" A story without evidence is just a strongly-held opinion.

The combination is what moves people: a clear narrative that says *what's going on and what we should do*, with data placed at exactly the points where a reasonable person would otherwise doubt you.

> [!quote] The reframe I use
> People don't remember your numbers. They remember your *story*, and they trust it *because* of your numbers. Build the story first; recruit the data to defend it.

## The structure I use

Most persuasive product narratives follow the same skeleton — it's the change-arc every good story has:

1. **The world as it is** — the status quo, the metric today, the user's current pain.
2. **The tension** — what's wrong, what it's costing us, why it can't continue. (This is where the problem-data lives.)
3. **The turn** — the insight or opportunity. What we now understand that we didn't.
4. **The resolution** — what we should do, and the outcome we expect if we do it.

Then I sequence the data to serve each beat: the retention drop is the *tension*, the cohort cut that reveals *why* is the *turn*, the projected lift is the *resolution*. The numbers aren't a separate "appendix of evidence" — they're load-bearing beats in the story itself.

## Keeping the story honest

Storytelling can slide into spin — picking the chart that flatters the conclusion you already wanted. I hold a hard line: the story has to be the *honest* read of the data, including the parts that complicate it. This connects straight to [[Fluency with Data]]: causation vs correlation, valid samples, statistical significance. A narrative built on a misread number isn't persuasion — it's a time bomb that detonates the moment someone checks your work. Credibility is the asset; one caught exaggeration spends years of it.

> [!tip] Find the one number
> Every strong data story has a single hero metric — the one figure that, once the audience grasps it, makes the conclusion feel inevitable. Before I build the narrative I ask: *what's the one number that changes the room?* Everything else supports it. A deck with twenty equally-weighted charts has no hero, and so it has no point.

## Tailoring the story

The story bends to the audience, same as all [[Communication]]:

- **Executives** get the hero number and the recommendation first ([[Executive Communication]]) — the "so what," not the cohort analysis.
- **Your team** gets the full arc — they're going to *build* the resolution, so they need the reasoning.
- **Skeptical stakeholders** get the most data, placed precisely at their objections.

## How AI is changing it

AI is genuinely good at the assembly — pulling numbers, drafting the chart, even proposing a narrative arc from a dataset ([[How AI Is Reshaping Product Management]]). That removes real grunt work, but it *sharpens* the human contribution rather than removing it. The persuasive frame — which tension matters, which number is the hero, what the honest story actually is — is judgment AI doesn't have. And in an org where everyone can generate ten charts in a minute, the burden of choice goes *up*: the scarce skill becomes deciding which single story is worth the room's attention. AI makes charts cheap; it makes a true, well-chosen story more valuable.
