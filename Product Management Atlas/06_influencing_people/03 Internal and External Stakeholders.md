# Internal and External Stakeholders

A stakeholder is anyone whose interests your product touches — whose support you need, whose constraints you inherit, or who can derail you if surprised. The first behavior under [[Stakeholder Management]] is *proactively identifying* them, and I take "proactively" literally: you map them before the project, not in the post-mortem.

## The trap of treating them as one blob

New PMs say "I need to manage my stakeholders" as if it's a single task. It isn't. A sales leader who wants a feature for one deal, a platform owner whose policy you must comply with, and an engineering manager you share a team with are three completely different relationships. Lump them together and you'll over-communicate with the people who don't care and under-communicate with the ones who'll sink you.

So I split them on two axes: **internal vs external**, and **how much they can affect the outcome.**

## Internal stakeholders

People inside your company. You'll see them in the hallway, share OKRs, and need them repeatedly — so the relationship compounds. The major types:

| Type | What they want | How I manage them |
|---|---|---|
| **Engineering & Design** | Clear problems, stable scope, the "why" | Daily partnership; this is [[Cross-Functional Collaboration]], not "stakeholder management" |
| **Executives / leadership** | Confidence the bet is sound; no surprises | [[Managing Up]] — brevity, business framing, [[Executive Communication]] |
| **Sales / GTM / Marketing** | Things to sell; launch dates; messaging | Translate their asks into problems; never let one deal set the roadmap |
| **Support / Ops** | Fewer tickets; advance warning of changes | A rich [[Voice of the Customer]] channel; warn them before launches |
| **Data / Legal / Finance** | Correctness, compliance, viable economics | Pull them in *early* as consultants, not last-minute approvers |

## External stakeholders

People outside the building. Higher stakes per interaction, less frequent contact, and you represent the whole company when you talk to them.

- **Customers and users** — the most important external voice, but treat feedback as input, not instruction. (How I separate signal from the loudest request: [[Voice of the Customer]].)
- **Partners** — in my world, league/media partners and co-marketing deals. Mutual value or the relationship rots.
- **Vendors / platform owners** — Apple, Google, payment processors. You don't control their constraints; you design around them. Manage expectations transparently and keep credibility intact.

## Mapping: who needs what

Identification isn't enough — I sort everyone by **influence on the outcome** and **interest in it**, the classic 2×2. It tells me where to spend communication effort.

| | Low interest | High interest |
|---|---|---|
| **High influence** | *Keep satisfied* — execs who must not be surprised | *Manage closely* — partners, decision-makers, your core team |
| **Low influence** | *Monitor* — a light update is plenty | *Keep informed* — engaged users, adjacent teams |

The failure mode is inverting it: pouring energy into vocal low-influence stakeholders while a high-influence one quietly loses confidence.

## The tie to decision rights

Here's where mapping gets sharp. A stakeholder map tells you who *cares*; it does **not** tell you who *decides*. Conflating the two is how PMs end up paralyzed — treating everyone with an opinion as a veto. That's why I always pair the map with [[RACI and Decision Rights]]: every stakeholder gets an explicit role — Owner, Partner/Approver, Consulted, or merely Informed. "You're a consulted stakeholder, not an approver on this one" is one of the most useful sentences a PM can say, and it's only possible once the decision rights are written down.

> [!tip] Run the map at kickoff
> Five minutes at the start of any non-trivial initiative: list everyone the work touches, place them in the 2×2, assign each a [[RACI and Decision Rights|RACI]] role, and decide your cadence for each. It's the cheapest insurance against a late-stage blindside I know of.
