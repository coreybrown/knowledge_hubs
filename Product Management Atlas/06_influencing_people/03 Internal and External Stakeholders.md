# Internal and External Stakeholders

A stakeholder is anyone whose interests your product touches: whose support you need, whose constraints you inherit, or who can derail you if they are surprised. The first behavior under [[Stakeholder Management]] is *proactively identifying* them, and I take "proactively" literally. You map them before the project, not in the post-mortem.

## The trap of treating them as one blob

New PMs say "I need to manage my stakeholders" as if it is a single task. It is not. A sales leader who wants a feature for one deal, a platform owner whose policy you have to comply with, and an engineering manager you share a team with are three completely different relationships. Lump them together and you will over-communicate with the people who do not care and under-communicate with the ones who will sink you.

So I split them on two axes: internal versus external, and how much they can actually affect the outcome.

## Internal stakeholders

People inside your company. You will see them in the hallway, share OKRs with them, and need them repeatedly, so the relationship compounds over time. The major types:

| Type | What they want | How I manage them |
|---|---|---|
| **Engineering & Design** | Clear problems, stable scope, the "why" | Daily partnership; this is [[Cross-Functional Collaboration]], not "stakeholder management" |
| **Executives / leadership** | Confidence the bet is sound; no surprises | [[Managing Up]]: brevity, business framing, [[Executive Communication]] |
| **Sales / GTM / Marketing** | Things to sell; launch dates; messaging | Translate their asks into problems; never let one deal set the roadmap |
| **Support / Ops** | Fewer tickets; advance warning of changes | A rich [[Voice of the Customer]] channel; warn them before launches |
| **Data / Legal / Finance** | Correctness, compliance, viable economics | Pull them in *early* as consultants, not last-minute approvers |

## External stakeholders

People outside the building. The stakes per interaction are higher, the contact is less frequent, and you represent the whole company when you talk to them.

- **Customers and users** are the most important external voice, but treat their feedback as input, not instruction. (How I separate signal from the loudest request: [[Voice of the Customer]].)
- **Partners**, which in my world meant league and media partners and co-marketing deals. Mutual value, or the relationship rots.
- **Vendors and platform owners** like Apple, Google, and payment processors. You do not control their constraints, so you design around them. Manage expectations transparently and keep your credibility intact.

## Mapping: who needs what

Identification on its own is not enough. I sort everyone by **influence on the outcome** and **interest in it**, the classic 2x2. It tells me where to spend communication effort.

| | Low interest | High interest |
|---|---|---|
| **High influence** | *Keep satisfied* (execs who must not be surprised) | *Manage closely* (partners, decision-makers, your core team) |
| **Low influence** | *Monitor* (a light update is plenty) | *Keep informed* (engaged users, adjacent teams) |

The failure mode is inverting it: pouring energy into vocal low-influence stakeholders while a high-influence one quietly loses confidence in you.

## The tie to decision rights

This is where mapping gets sharp. A stakeholder map tells you who *cares*. It does not tell you who *decides*. Conflating the two is how PMs end up paralyzed, treating everyone with an opinion as a veto. That is why I always pair the map with [[RACI and Decision Rights]], so every stakeholder gets an explicit role: Owner, Partner/Approver, Consulted, or merely Informed. "You are a consulted stakeholder, not an approver on this one" is one of the most useful sentences a PM can say, and it is only possible once the decision rights are written down.

> [!tip] Run the map at kickoff
> Take five minutes at the start of any non-trivial initiative: list everyone the work touches, place them in the 2x2, assign each a [[RACI and Decision Rights|RACI]] role, and decide your cadence for each. It is the cheapest insurance I know against a late-stage blindside.
