# Managing Up

The third competency in [[Influencing People]], and the one most ambitious PMs are quietly bad at. We're trained to manage *down* and *across*. Managing *up* feels like politics, so people avoid it — a mistake. Your manager and the executives above you are your most powerful lever, and learning to use them well isn't careerism; it's the job.

## What it is

> [!quote] The definition I use
> **Managing Up** — *The ability to leverage senior managers/executives to help achieve goals, deliver outcomes, and positively influence the strategic direction of the team and company.*

The operative word is **leverage**. Executives have what you don't: budget, headcount, air cover, and the ability to unblock things across the org with one sentence. Managing up is the discipline of giving them what *they* need — clarity, confidence, early warning — so they give you what *you* need. It runs in both directions: you also *influence* their thinking, shaping strategy from below.

## The behaviors

Two named behaviors sit under this competency.

### Strategic Influence & Alignment
Connecting your product vision to the company's strategy, and using rationale, storytelling, and data to influence the people who set direction. The skilled moves: navigate ambiguity and competing executive interests, champion shared outcomes over your own turf, and build genuine trust with senior functions. This is how a PM's idea becomes a company bet — by being framed in the language of the company's goals, not the team's preferences.

### Executive Communications
A craft specific enough that I gave it its own note: [[Executive Communication]]. The essence — concise and strategic-impact-focused, framed in business terms tied to KPIs and ROI, data-backed, with questions and risks already handled, delivered calm and solution-oriented. Executives are chronically short on time and long on context; communication that ignores both fails however good the underlying work is.

## How it shows up by level

The escalation tracks **whose attention you can hold and influence** — from your own manager to the C-suite and board.

| Level | What managing up looks like |
|---|---|
| [[Associate Product Manager\|APM]] | Keeps their manager informed; flags blockers early; no surprises in the 1:1 |
| [[Product Manager\|PM]] | Communicates outcomes upward independently; makes asks for help with clear rationale |
| [[Senior Product Manager\|Senior PM]] | Influences leadership on direction; trusted in exec forums; frames work in business terms |
| [[Group Product Manager\|GPM]] / [[Director and VP of Product\|Director]] | Shapes strategy *with* executives; manages sideways across peer leaders; owns exec narratives |
| VP+ | *Is* the executive — and now manages up to the CEO and board ([[The Chief Product Officer]]) |

> [!tip] Make your manager's job easy
> The simplest reframe that works: your manager is also managing *up*. Every clean, well-framed update you give them is something they can pass upward with confidence. Be the report they never have to translate, chase, or worry about. That reputation is worth more than any single ask.

## How to grow it

- **Lead with the answer.** Adopt BLUF — bottom line up front — in everything you send up. Detail follows the conclusion, never precedes it. ([[Executive Communication]].)
- **Frame in their currency.** Translate "we shipped X" into "this moves [the metric they're accountable for]" ([[Business Outcome Ownership]], [[OKRs and Goal-Setting]]).
- **Anticipate the question.** Before any exec conversation, write the three hardest questions they'll ask and answer them in advance. Half of managing up is doing the executive's thinking for them.
- **Build trust in low-stakes moments.** Don't let the first real exec interaction be a crisis. Bank credibility through consistent, surprise-free delivery.

## How AI is changing it

The mechanical half — turning a messy quarter into a crisp exec summary, drafting the board slide, pulling the KPI deltas — is squarely in the zone AI now does in minutes ([[Competencies AI Commoditizes vs Elevates]]). I lean on it for first drafts. But the high-value half is untouched: knowing *which* number an executive cares about, reading the currents between competing leaders, and earning the trust that makes influence real. AI is a fast speechwriter with no standing in the room. The judgment of *what to say to whom, and why now* stays human.

> [!warning] Myth: managing up is brown-nosing
> The cynical read — that managing up is flattery and self-promotion — keeps good PMs from doing it, and that hurts their teams. Done right it's the opposite of politics: it's giving leadership the clarity to make good decisions and securing the resources your team needs. A PM who refuses to manage up isn't principled; they're leaving their team under-supported.
