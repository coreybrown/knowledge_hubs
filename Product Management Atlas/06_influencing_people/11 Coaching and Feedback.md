# Coaching and Feedback

This is the gateway skill into [[Team Leadership]] — specifically its **Coaching & Performance** behavior — and the one most people are quietly terrible at in both directions. We're bad at *giving* feedback because it's uncomfortable, and bad at *receiving* it because it stings. Getting good at both is, in my experience, the single highest-leverage thing you can do for the people around you, whether or not you ever manage anyone.

## My point of view: feedback is a gift you owe people

The kindest thing you can do for a colleague is tell them the truth about their work, clearly and early. Withholding feedback to spare someone's feelings isn't kindness — it's a quiet betrayal that lets them keep failing in ways they can't see. Niceness that costs someone their growth isn't nice.

This is also why coaching is the heart of leadership, not a side duty. Cagan calls it a manager's **#1 responsibility** — roughly half the job. The shift from "I do the work" to "I make the people who do the work better" *is* the transition into management ([[The Manager as Coach]]).

## Giving feedback well

A few rules I hold to, several of them straight from the appraisal do's and don'ts in my leveling system:

- **First-hand and specific.** On things you *observed*, anchored to examples — never rumor or "people are saying." Vague ("be more strategic") is useless; specific ("in that review you led with the solution before the problem") is actionable.
- **Job-related, never personal.** Critique the work, not the personality. "This doc buried the recommendation" is fair; "you're disorganized" is an attack.
- **Both kinds, regularly** — through **regular touchpoints**, not saved for the annual review. Feedback six months late is an ambush, not coaching.
- **Don't sugarcoat — and don't only criticize.** Both are failure modes in my model. Softening the message until it disappears is as bad as a barrage of only-negatives.

> [!tip] Tailor to the person
> The Coaching & Performance behavior is explicit that feedback is **tailored to each person's strengths and goals.** A confident senior PM needs bluntness; a shaky APM needs scaffolding. Same content, different delivery. Coaching is not one-size-fits-all, and the manager who delivers identical feedback to everyone is really just talking to themselves.

## Receiving feedback well

The half people skip — and the one that gates your own growth. If you get defensive, people stop telling you the truth, and you go blind.

- **Assume good intent and just listen.** Your first job is to *understand* it, not rebut it. Argue in the moment and you've taught them not to bother next time.
- **Separate the signal from the delivery.** Even clumsily-delivered feedback usually contains a real signal. Don't discard the message because you didn't like the wrapping.
- **Close the loop.** Thank them, and actually *act* on it visibly. Nothing makes people invest in your growth like seeing their input change your behavior.

> [!warning] Myth: feedback is a manager-to-report thing
> Feedback flows in every direction — to peers, to your own manager, up the chain. Some of the most valuable I've given and gotten was lateral. And you don't need a title to coach: a Later [[Product Manager\|PM]] mentoring an [[Associate Product Manager\|APM]] is doing exactly this work, which is why the skill matters long before [[Team Leadership]] formally "applies" to you.

## How AI is changing it

AI can scaffold the *mechanics* — draft a review from your notes, summarize a quarter of 1:1s, suggest framing for a tough message ([[Competencies AI Commoditizes vs Elevates]]). Useful for getting past the blank page on an uncomfortable write-up. But the substance is irreducibly human: knowing *what* a person needs to hear, reading whether they're ready to hear it, and having the relationship that makes hard feedback land as care rather than attack. A model can wordsmith the message; it cannot do the caring, the noticing, or the trust. Coaching is the part of the job AI touches least.
