# Stakeholder Management

This is the first competency in [[Influencing People]], and the one I see PMs underrate until it bites them. You can have the right strategy, a clean spec, and a shipping team, and still get blocked, blindsided, or overruled by someone you forgot to bring along.

## What it is

> [!quote] The definition I use
> **Stakeholder Management**: *The ability to proactively identify stakeholders impacted by the PM's area and to work with them to factor their requirements into product decisions.*

Two words in there carry the weight. The first is **proactively**, because the failure mode is always reactive: finding out about a constraint after it is already a crisis. The second is **factor in**, not obey. The job is not to satisfy every stakeholder. It is to surface their needs early and make a defensible decision that accounts for them. Saying no to a stakeholder, and doing it well, is stakeholder management too.

## The behaviors

I break this competency into three named behaviors.

### Internal Stakeholder Influence & Alignment
Building relationships with the people inside your company that your work depends on or affects, through **regular touchpoints**, not just when you need something. The core moves are to align on goals, priorities, and metrics before the conflict, communicate persuasively and adapt to the audience, and resolve conflict with empathy rather than escalation. The PMs who are good at this rarely seem to be fighting battles, because they have already had the conversation offline.

### External Stakeholder Management
Trusted relationships with the people outside the building: customers, partners, vendors, sometimes regulators. The work is to translate external feedback into product direction without letting the loudest customer set your roadmap, represent the company credibly, manage expectations transparently, and create mutual value. In my B2C mobile world this meant league partners, payment providers, and platform owners like Apple and Google, whose constraints I did not control but had to design around.

### Org-Based Communications
Articulating vision, strategy, and priorities at every level of the org, and adapting the message to each one. The same roadmap update lands differently for an engineer, a marketer, and a VP. This behavior leans hard on [[Storytelling with Data]]: transparent updates that combine narrative and numbers so people stay aligned without you in the room.

## How it shows up by level

The escalation here is about **scope of influence**, from one feature out to the whole company.

| Level | What stakeholder management looks like |
|---|---|
| [[Associate Product Manager\|APM]] | Identifies the obvious stakeholders for *one feature*, with manager support; keeps them informed |
| [[Product Manager\|PM]] | Independently maps and aligns stakeholders for complex work; runs the touchpoints; handles routine conflict |
| [[Senior Product Manager\|Senior PM]] | Owns alignment across multiple teams; trusted to manage high-stakes external relationships; resolves real disputes |
| [[Group Product Manager\|GPM]] / [[Director and VP of Product\|Director]] | Aligns stakeholders at the org level; sets the *communication cadence* others follow; manages executive-tier partners |
| VP+ | Builds the company-wide norms for how product communicates and aligns; the relationships *are* the strategy |

## How to grow it

A few things I actually coach people to do:

- **Make a stakeholder map and keep it current.** Start with [[Internal and External Stakeholders]]: who is impacted, who decides, who just wants to be informed. Pair it with [[RACI and Decision Rights]] so "stakeholder" does not quietly blur into "person who can veto me."
- **Front-load the relationship.** Schedule the touchpoint before you need the favor. Influence runs on a trust balance you fund in advance.
- **Practice the persuasive update** until people act on your [[Storytelling with Data|data stories]] without needing a meeting.
- **Get good at the no.** Acknowledge the need, explain the trade-off, offer the alternative. A respected no beats a resented yes, generally.

## How AI is changing it

The mechanical layer here is the first thing AI takes off your plate: drafting the stakeholder update, summarizing the meeting, writing the status report. It is a top example in [[Competencies AI Commoditizes vs Elevates]], and I now generate a first-draft update in seconds. What does not commoditize is the judgment about whose requirements to weight, the read on a room, and the trust you have banked. Lenny Rachitsky calls the durable PM core the "people/glue" work, and stakeholder management is the heart of it. AI will write the memo. It cannot have the hard hallway conversation for you.

> [!warning] Myth: stakeholder management means keeping everyone happy
> It does not. That is how you get a watered-down, design-by-committee product. The goal is **alignment**, not universal approval: everyone heard, the trade-offs explicit, the decision owned. A PM optimizing for zero unhappy stakeholders is optimizing for the wrong thing.
