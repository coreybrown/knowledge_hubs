# Influencing People

> [!quote] The definition I use
> **Influencing People** — *Rally people around the team's work.*

This is the fourth of the four competency areas in [[The PM Competency Model]], and it's the one that separates PMs who *do the job* from PMs who *move the org*. The other three areas — [[Product Execution]], [[Customer Insight]], [[Product Strategy]] — are about figuring out the right thing and getting it built. This area is about the uncomfortable truth underneath all of them: you can't do any of it alone, and you can't make anyone do anything.

## My point of view: a PM's power is borrowed, never owned

Here is the single most important thing I tell new PMs. **You have no authority.** You don't own the engineers' time, the designer's calendar, the budget, or the roadmap of any team but the slice you've been handed. Everything you "get done" is done by people who don't report to you, who could ignore you, and who often outrank you. The job is not to command — it's to make people *want* to point their effort at the work you believe in.

That's why I think influence isn't *one* of the things a PM does. It's the *whole job*, wearing different hats. Discovery is influence (getting users and the team to tell you the truth). Strategy is influence (getting executives to bet on a direction). Execution is influence (getting a cross-functional team to move in sync). Strip the influence out and you're left with a person writing documents nobody acts on.

> [!warning] The "CEO of the product" myth
> The most damaging metaphor in our field. CEOs hire, fire, and direct. PMs do none of that — they earn followership or get nothing. I unpack why I reject this framing in [[Leading Without Authority]] and [[What Is Product Management]].

## The three competencies

This area breaks into three competencies, each with its own note and named behaviors drawn from my model. Note the asymmetry: two of these are about influencing *outward and upward* and start day one — but the middle one, leadership, **doesn't apply at the junior levels at all.**

| Competency | What it covers | The behaviors |
|---|---|---|
| [[Stakeholder Management]] | Aligning everyone your work touches | Internal Stakeholder Influence & Alignment · External Stakeholder Management · Org-Based Communications |
| [[Team Leadership]] | Managing and growing people | Coaching & Performance · Team Impact · Product-Wide Influence & Leadership · Resource Management & Hiring |
| [[Managing Up]] | Leveraging senior leaders | Strategic Influence & Alignment · Executive Communications |

> [!note] Team Leadership starts late
> Unlike every other competency, [[Team Leadership]] is rated **"Not applicable"** at the [[Associate Product Manager|APM]] and Early PM levels in the matrix I use — it only begins (informally) once you're a Later [[Product Manager|PM]] mentoring others. You influence people for years before you ever *lead* them. More on that leveling quirk in the note itself.

## Where influence connects

Influence isn't a separate phase — it's woven through every other area. Your [[Storytelling with Data|stories]] are only as good as your [[Fluency with Data|data fluency]]. Your stakeholder alignment is enforced through [[RACI and Decision Rights|decision rights]]. The leadership behaviors here flow straight into the coaching domain ([[The Manager as Coach]]) and the [[The Product Career Ladder|career ladder]] — because the entire manager track *is* this area, scaled.
