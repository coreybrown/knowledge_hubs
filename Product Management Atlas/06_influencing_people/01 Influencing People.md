# Influencing People

> [!quote] The definition I use
> **Influencing People**: *Rally people around the team's work.*

This is the fourth of the four competency areas in [[The PM Competency Model]], and in my experience it is what separates the PMs who do the job from the PMs who move the org. The other three areas, [[Product Execution]], [[Customer Insight]], and [[Product Strategy]], are about figuring out the right thing and getting it built. This area sits on the uncomfortable fact underneath all of them: you cannot do any of it alone, and you cannot make anyone do anything.

## My point of view: a PM's power is borrowed, never owned

Here is the first thing I tell new PMs. You have no authority. You do not own the engineers' time, the designer's calendar, the budget, or the roadmap of any team but the slice you have been handed. Everything you "get done" is done by people who do not report to you, who could ignore you, and who often outrank you. So the job was never to command. It is to make people *want* to point their effort at the work you believe in.

That is why I do not think of influence as one of the things a PM does. It is the whole job, wearing different hats. Discovery is influence, because you are getting users and the team to tell you the truth. Strategy is influence, because you are getting executives to bet on a direction. Execution is influence, because you are getting a cross-functional team to move in sync. Take the influence out and what is left is someone writing documents nobody acts on.

> [!warning] The "CEO of the product" myth
> This is the framing I push back on most with new PMs. CEOs hire, fire, and direct. A PM does none of that, so you earn followership or you get nothing. I unpack why I reject it in [[Leading Without Authority]] and [[What Is Product Management]].

## The three competencies

This area breaks into three competencies, each with its own note and named behaviors drawn from my model. There is an asymmetry worth noticing. Two of these are about influencing outward and upward, and they start on day one. The middle one, leadership, does not apply at the junior levels at all.

| Competency | What it covers | The behaviors |
|---|---|---|
| [[Stakeholder Management]] | Aligning everyone your work touches | Internal Stakeholder Influence & Alignment · External Stakeholder Management · Org-Based Communications |
| [[Team Leadership]] | Managing and growing people | Coaching & Performance · Team Impact · Product-Wide Influence & Leadership · Resource Management & Hiring |
| [[Managing Up]] | Leveraging senior leaders | Strategic Influence & Alignment · Executive Communications |

> [!note] Team Leadership starts late
> Unlike every other competency, [[Team Leadership]] is rated **"Not applicable"** at the [[Associate Product Manager|APM]] and Early PM levels in the matrix I use. It only begins, informally, once you are a Later [[Product Manager|PM]] mentoring others. You influence people for years before you ever lead them. I get into that leveling quirk in the note itself.

## Where influence connects

Influence is not a separate phase. It runs through every other area. Your [[Storytelling with Data|stories]] are only as good as your [[Fluency with Data|data fluency]]. Your stakeholder alignment is enforced through [[RACI and Decision Rights|decision rights]]. The leadership behaviors here flow straight into the coaching domain ([[The Manager as Coach]]) and the [[The Product Career Ladder|career ladder]], because the entire manager track is this area, scaled up.
