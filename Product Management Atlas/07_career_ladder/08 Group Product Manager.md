# Group Product Manager

The first people-management rung of [[The Product Career Ladder]], and the rung where the job changes the most. GPM is the management-track twin of [[Staff and Principal Product Manager|Staff PM]] — same level on the ladder, completely different shape. If you take only one idea from this note: at GPM, **your output stops being your work and becomes your team's work.**

> [!tip] My one-line definition
> A GPM is a Senior PM who now scales *through people*. They typically still own a slice of product directly, *and* manage a small team of PMs — which makes this the hardest transition on the whole ladder, because it's the first time "doing it yourself" is the wrong answer.

This is one half of the fork after [[Senior Product Manager|Senior PM]]; the other is [[Staff and Principal Product Manager]]. See [[The IC and Manager Tracks]].

## Scope, autonomy, complexity

The first rung of the management mastery progression — Subject Matter Expert in the craft, *Gaining Experience* in management. See [[The Six Stages of Mastery]].

| Dial | At GPM |
|---|---|
| **Complexity** | Sets priorities; translates objectives into *operational plans*; builds cohesive operating rhythms for the team. |
| **Autonomy** | General direction; manages the day-to-day for *one function* (a small PM team). |
| **Scope** | A *group* of related product areas — owned through the PMs who run them. |
| **Ambiguity** | Absorbs ambiguity *on behalf of the team* and converts it into clear plans they can run. |

## The competency that takes over: Team Leadership

At every prior rung, [[Team Leadership]] was *Not applicable* ([[Associate Product Manager|APM]]), then *informal* ([[Product Manager|PM]] / [[Senior Product Manager|Senior]]). At GPM it becomes the **dominant** competency and acquires formal weight.

> [!quote] What Team Leadership now means
> *Coaching & Performance* (regular, tailored, actionable feedback; model strong practice) · *Team Impact* (align the team's goals to the business; hold the team accountable for **outcomes, not activity**; remove blockers) · the first taste of *Resource Management & Hiring* (define role expectations; help [[Hiring and Building a Product Team|hire]]).

The other eleven competencies don't vanish — but you increasingly exercise them *through* your PMs. You're no longer the one writing every [[Feature Specification|spec]]; you're the one ensuring your team's specs are excellent ([[Coaching and Feedback]]).

> [!warning] The transition trap (the one that ends careers)
> **Doing the work instead of multiplying it.** New GPMs, especially great former ICs, swoop in and do their PMs' jobs because they can do it faster. It feels productive. It is *quietly fatal* — it caps your output at one person, starves your PMs of growth, and earns you a reputation as a bottleneck. Your job is now to make *them* great. The hardest skill to learn here is **letting work be done worse than you'd do it, so a person can grow.** See [[The Manager as Coach]].

## How to be a great GPM

- **Reframe your scoreboard.** Stop counting what *you* shipped. Start counting whether your *team* hit its outcomes and whether each PM grew. That's the whole job ([[Outcomes Over Outputs]]).
- **Coaching is the role, not a chore.** The literature is right that coaching is the #1 manager responsibility — plan to spend roughly half your time on it. [[The Manager as Coach]], [[Coaching and Feedback]].
- **Hire ahead of need and level honestly.** You now own [[Hiring and Building a Product Team|staffing]] and a seat at [[Calibration and Promotion|calibration]]. Bad hires and inflated levels are your debts to pay.
- **Manage up *and* down at once.** You translate exec strategy down into plans and your team's reality up into the room. [[Managing Up]], [[Executive Communication]].

> [!note] You can change your mind
> If you reach GPM, hate managing, and miss the craft — go back to the IC track. That's not failure; it's information. Plenty of strong product people round-trip the [[The IC and Manager Tracks|fork]]. Better an honest IC than a resentful manager. ([[The IC vs Manager Decision]].)

## What promotion requires (and to where)

You move toward [[Director and VP of Product|Director]] when you reliably run a team to outcomes, develop PMs who get promoted, and start influencing *function-level* decisions — managing through other *managers*, not just ICs. Mechanics: [[Score-Gated Leveling]].
