# The IC and Manager Tracks

For the first three rungs of [[The Product Career Ladder]], there's only one path. Everyone does the same job at rising altitude. Then, after [[Senior Product Manager|Senior PM]], the ladder forks — and a lot of careers get confused right here. So let me lay the two tracks out plainly.

## The fork, and where it opens

> [!tip] The fork is after Senior PM — not before
> Up to and including Senior PM, you're a pure individual contributor and there's nothing to choose. The fork opens *because* a strong Senior PM has more value to give than one product area can absorb. You can scale that value two ways: **wider influence as an IC**, or **leverage through a team as a manager.** That's the decision in [[The IC vs Manager Decision]].

```
                          ┌─► Staff / Principal PM ─► (Distinguished)   [IC: scale influence]
APM ─► PM ─► Senior PM ──►┤
                          └─► Group PM ─► Director ─► VP ─► CPO          [Mgr: scale through people]
```

## The two tracks, side by side

| | **IC track** | **Manager track** |
|---|---|---|
| **Rungs above Senior** | [[Staff and Principal Product Manager\|Staff → Principal → Distinguished]] | [[Group Product Manager\|GPM]] → [[Director and VP of Product\|Director → VP]] → [[The Chief Product Officer\|CPO/SVP]] |
| **What you scale** | Depth and breadth of *influence* | *Output through other people* |
| **Output is** | Your own work + the bar you raise | Your team's work |
| **Core competency that lights up** | [[Strategic Impact]], [[Product Vision and Roadmapping]] | [[Team Leadership]] |
| **Headcount?** | No direct reports | Direct (then indirect) reports |
| **Mastery stage** *(see [[The Six Stages of Mastery]])* | SME → Master → Thought Leader, applied to *the craft* | SME → Master → Thought Leader, applied to *people and org* |

## They are peers, not a ranking

This is the point I hammer hardest. A Staff PM and a GPM are the **same level** — same compensation band, same influence expectation, different *shape* of contribution. The senior IC track is not where you go when you "can't" manage. Some of the highest-leverage product people I've worked with never managed a soul; they shaped strategy across an entire portfolio and made every team around them better. Treating the IC ceiling as lower than the manager ceiling is how companies lose their best builders.

> [!note] What flips at the fork
> On the IC track, the twelve competencies keep escalating but **[[Team Leadership]]** expresses as *influence and coaching without authority* ([[Leading Without Authority]]) rather than formal people management. On the manager track, [[Team Leadership]] becomes the *dominant* competency — your evaluation increasingly rests on whether your *team* succeeds, not whether *you* ship. See [[The Manager as Coach]].

> [!warning] Two myths to kill
> **Myth 1: "Management is the promotion."** No — it's a lateral move into a different job, detailed in [[The IC vs Manager Decision]]. **Myth 2: "You can't go back."** You can, and I've seen great PMs flip both ways more than once. The tracks are a fork in the road, not a one-way door.

## How leveling works across the fork

Both tracks are scored against the **same** [[The PM Competency Model|twelve competencies]] — what changes is the altitude and which competencies dominate. The full grid lives in [[The Master Competency Matrix]], and the per-rung cheat sheet is [[Competency Quick Reference by Level]]. Promotions on either track are gated the same way, through [[Score-Gated Leveling]] and [[Calibration and Promotion]].
