# Product Manager

The load-bearing rung of [[The Product Career Ladder]]. If [[Associate Product Manager|APM]] is the learning level, PM is the *doing* level — the first point where you own a product area and the safety net comes off. Most of the people executing real product work in any company sit here, and getting good at this rung is what the entire craft is built to produce.

> [!tip] My one-line definition
> A PM owns a product area **independently** and delivers outcomes on it. The defining shift from APM isn't skill — it's *autonomy*. You no longer need the problem handed to you fully framed; you can take an ambiguous-ish area, do the discovery, and drive it to a result.

## Scope, autonomy, complexity

This rung spans what the role architecture splits into PM II and PM III — "Applies Practical Knowledge" maturing toward "Applies Advanced Knowledge" on [[The Six Stages of Mastery]].

| Dial | At PM |
|---|---|
| **Complexity** | Defines requirements, manages the product lifecycle, leads development projects; prioritizes from feedback and analyzes performance. |
| **Autonomy** | Limited supervision early; by PM III, general direction but *sets own priorities*. Begins to impact multiple projects. |
| **Scope** | A whole product area, owned end to end. |
| **Ambiguity** | Can operate on ill-defined work — does the framing themselves rather than waiting for it. |

## The twelve competencies at PM

The headline phrase flips from APM's "*with support*" to "***independently***." A PM runs the full [[The PM Competency Model|model]] on their own area.

| Competency | What good looks like at PM |
|---|---|
| [[Feature Specification]] | Specs *major, complex* functionality independently; finds the right level of detail; uses comps and prototypes. |
| [[Product Delivery]] | Owns delivery for the area; runs the [[The 10-50-90 Execution Framework\|execution rhythm]] with minimal surprises. |
| [[Product Quality]] | Makes [[Sound Product Decision-Making\|sound quality calls]] across the relevant devices and use cases. |
| [[Fluency with Data]] | Drives the question, not just the answer; runs [[Experimentation and A-B Testing\|experiments]] and reads them honestly. |
| [[Voice of the Customer]] | Runs [[User Research and Discovery\|discovery]] and translates it into the roadmap. |
| [[User Experience Design]] | Partners with design as a peer; owns the usability/feasibility trade-offs for the area. |
| [[Business Outcome Ownership]] | Owns an [[Outcomes Over Outputs\|outcome]], not a feature list; accountable for moving a metric. |
| [[Strategic Impact]] | Connects their area to company strategy; contributes upward. |
| [[Product Vision and Roadmapping]] | Owns and defends a roadmap for the area. |
| [[Stakeholder Management]] | Manages the full [[Internal and External Stakeholders\|stakeholder]] set around the area independently. |
| **[[Team Leadership]]** | Begins to apply *informally* — mentoring an APM, influencing the team — even with no direct reports. |
| [[Managing Up]] | [[Managing Up\|Manages up]] proactively; brings problems with options, not just problems. |

> [!note] Where Team Leadership wakes up
> At APM, [[Team Leadership]] is *Not applicable*. At PM it switches on **informally** — the late-PM is the first level expected to influence the team and mentor more junior PMs, without yet owning anyone's career. This is the seed that, at the fork, grows into either [[Leading Without Authority\|influence]] (IC track) or formal management.

## How to be a great PM

- **Own the outcome, not the output.** The PMs who plateau here measure themselves by features shipped. The ones who get promoted measure themselves by the metric that moved. Internalize [[Outcomes Over Outputs]].
- **Get spiky on purpose.** This is where your [[The T-Shaped PM and Knowing Your Shape|shape]] starts to matter. Lean into your strong competencies; build a team around the rest.
- **Run a tight execution machine.** Reliable delivery ([[Product Delivery]], [[Agile Delivery and Team Cadence]]) is the floor that buys you the right to be heard on strategy.
- **Start influencing without authority.** Practice the muscle now ([[Leading Without Authority]]); it's load-bearing on *both* sides of the [[The IC and Manager Tracks|fork]] ahead.

> [!warning] The plateau
> The most common career stall I see is the "permanent PM" — reliable, well-liked, shipping steadily, but never escalating *scope* or *ambiguity*. They do one good year, repeatedly. Promotion to Senior requires reaching for *complexity you're not yet comfortable with*, not polishing what you've mastered.

## What promotion to Senior requires

You move up when you reliably handle *complex, interrelated, ambiguous* work and your influence visibly extends beyond your own area. That's the threshold where the [[The IC and Manager Tracks|track fork]] starts to come into view. Next rung: [[Senior Product Manager]]. Mechanics in [[Score-Gated Leveling]].
