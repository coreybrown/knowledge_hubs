# The IC vs Manager Decision

The single hardest career question in [[The Product Career Ladder]], and the one PMs most often get wrong — usually by not treating it as a *decision* at all, but as a default. After [[Senior Product Manager|Senior PM]] the ladder forks: stay a senior IC ([[Staff and Principal Product Manager]]) or become a manager ([[Group Product Manager]]). This note is how I coach people through that fork.

> [!warning] The myth that ruins this decision
> **Management is not a promotion. It is a change of profession.** Going from Senior PM to GPM is a *lateral* move into a different job, not a rung above the IC track ([[The IC and Manager Tracks]] are peers). Cagan puts it well: management is **coaching plus staffing.** If you take the manager fork to "get promoted," you'll end up doing a job you don't want — and doing it resentfully, which your team will feel. Pick the *work*, not the title.

## What each fork's job actually is

Strip away the titles and look at what you'd do all day:

| | **IC track** ([[Staff and Principal Product Manager\|Staff/Principal]]) | **Manager track** ([[Group Product Manager\|GPM]]+) |
|---|---|---|
| **Your output** | Your own work + the bar you raise across teams | *Your team's* work |
| **Your day** | Strategy, the hardest problems, the craft, cross-team influence | Coaching, [[Hiring and Building a Product Team\|hiring]], 1:1s, unblocking, calibration |
| **The dial you max** | Ambiguity + influence (no headcount) — see [[What Changes at Each Level]] | Influence *through people* |
| **You're judged on** | The quality and reach of *your* thinking | Whether your *people* and *org* succeed |
| **Core competency** | [[Strategic Impact]], [[Leading Without Authority]] | [[Team Leadership]], [[The Manager as Coach]] |

## The honest questions I ask

> [!tip] Where does your satisfaction actually come from?
> Not where you *think* it should come from — where it *does.* Two diagnostic questions cut through most of the fog:
> 1. **When a PM on your team ships something great that you coached but didn't build — is that a genuine high, or a hollow one?** If it's a real high, the manager track will feed you. If you feel a pang of "but I didn't make it," respect that signal.
> 2. **Would you rather be the person with the deepest answer in the room, or the person who grew the people who have the answers?** Neither is nobler. They're just different.

Other things I tell people to weigh honestly:

- **Management is mostly *not* product work.** Roughly half a manager's time goes to coaching alone ([[The Manager as Coach]]), plus hiring, performance, and politics. If what you love is the *craft* — discovery, [[Product Sense and Judgment|judgment]], shipping — the IC track keeps you in it.
- **The IC ceiling is real and high.** A Principal/Distinguished PM shapes company strategy with zero reports. You are *not* trading away impact by staying IC — you're trading *people-leverage* for *craft-and-influence-leverage*.
- **Aptitude isn't appetite.** I've seen brilliant ICs who'd be fine managers but would *hate* it, and average ICs who light up the moment they're coaching. Capability is necessary; *wanting it* is decisive.

## How to de-risk the choice

- **Try management before you commit.** Mentor formally, lead an intern, run a workstream with borrowed people. Practice [[Coaching and Feedback]] and [[Leading Without Authority]] and notice how it feels. A "tour of duty" into management is reversible.
- **You can change your mind — both ways.** Round-tripping the fork is *normal*, not failure. Better an honest IC than a resentful manager (and vice versa). The tracks are a fork, not a one-way door.
- **Decide on data, not drift.** Don't let the next open req or a flattering title make the call. Use the diagnostic questions, then choose deliberately.

> [!note] What this means for managers leveling their people
> If you manage PMs, do *not* push your best IC into management as the only reward for excellence. That's how you lose a great builder and gain a mediocre manager. Build a real senior IC track ([[Staff and Principal Product Manager]]) and reward both forks equally at [[Calibration and Promotion]] time. Talk about the fork *before* someone's at it — see [[The Manager as Coach]].
