# The Product Career Ladder

This is the map for a question every PM eventually brings me: what's next, and what does it actually take to get there? Most career-ladder docs I've read are a list of titles with vague adjectives bolted on. "Senior PMs are more senior," which tells you nothing. The ladder in this Atlas rests on one strong claim, and the whole domain hangs off it.

> [!tip] The one idea that matters
> **The same twelve competencies escalate in *altitude*, not in *kind*.** A Principal PM and an APM are doing the same job: [[Feature Specification]], [[Product Delivery]], [[Stakeholder Management]], all twelve from [[The PM Competency Model]]. What changes is the *scope* they apply it to, the *autonomy* they have, the *ambiguity* they absorb, and the *influence* they carry. You don't graduate out of the competencies. You operate them at a higher altitude. The four dials are detailed in [[What Changes at Each Level]].

That's why I won't treat a promotion as "learning new skills." Almost nobody fails at the next level because they lacked a *new* ability. They fail because they kept applying an old one at the wrong altitude. Think of a Senior PM still spec'ing single features instead of sequencing a roadmap, or a new GPM still doing the work instead of multiplying it through people.

## Two tracks, one fork

After [[Senior Product Manager|Senior PM]], the ladder forks. You stay an individual contributor and scale your *influence* without managing anyone, or you become a manager and scale your *impact through other people*. These are different jobs, and I want to be clear that they're peers, not a hierarchy. The senior IC track sits alongside the management track. It is not a consolation prize. The full side-by-side is in [[The IC and Manager Tracks]], and how to actually choose is in [[The IC vs Manager Decision]].

## The mastery spine underneath every rung

Titles are the surface. Underneath them I track progress on a six-stage progression of *mastery*, running from **Gaining Experience** to **Recognized Thought Leader**, and I apply it per competency rather than per person. Because you're spiky (see [[The T-Shaped PM and Knowing Your Shape]]), you'll sit at different stages on different competencies, and that's the point of the model, not a defect in you. The spine is the engine of the whole ladder: [[The Six Stages of Mastery]].

> [!warning] The myth I fight most
> **A title is not a level.** I've met "Senior PMs" operating like APMs and APMs operating like Staff. The title is a label HR applies. The *level* is the altitude you actually operate at, measured against the competencies. When the two drift apart, you get a promotion problem or a performance problem. Leveling has to be evidence-based, which is what [[Score-Gated Leveling]] and [[Calibration and Promotion]] are for.
