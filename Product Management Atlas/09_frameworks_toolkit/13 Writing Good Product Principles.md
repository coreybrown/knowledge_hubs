# Writing Good Product Principles

A product principle is a reusable decision rule — a sentence that makes the right call obvious when you're not in the room to make it. Good principles are the cheapest form of leverage a product leader has: write four of them well and you've delegated a thousand small decisions without delegating the judgment — they're a quiet engine of [[Leading Without Authority|influence without authority]]. Write them badly and you've made a poster. This note is the *how-to*; [[theScore Product Principles]] is my worked example throughout.

## The anatomy of a good principle

I hold every candidate principle against four tests. It must be all four, not three.

> [!note] The four tests
> **Specific and actionable.** You can *decide* with it. "Be customer-obsessed" decides nothing — everyone agrees and nothing changes. "Painkillers over vitamins" makes a prioritization call before you've seen the feature. If two reasonable people couldn't use it to settle a real argument, it's a value, not a principle.
> **Short.** It has to fit in your head and survive being quoted in a hallway. If you can't recite it from memory, it won't be there when you need it. A clause, not a paragraph.
> **A spiky point of view.** It must be something a thoughtful person could *disagree with* — which means it has to choose a side. "We win with fans" explicitly subordinates internal politics to the fan; someone could argue for an org-chart-driven roadmap instead. A principle nobody could oppose isn't guiding anything.
> **Motivational.** It should sound like a team you'd want to join. Principles are cultural artifacts as much as decision rules; the tone teaches what the team values. "Have a shooters mentality" gives permission to be bold — it's a rallying cry, not a policy.

| Test | Passes | Fails |
|---|---|---|
| Specific/actionable | "Painkillers over vitamins" | "Be customer-obsessed" |
| Short | A memorable clause | A paragraph of nuance |
| Spiky | Picks a side you could debate | A truism nobody opposes |
| Motivational | Sounds like a team you'd join | Sounds like a compliance memo |

## The mistake that kills most principle sets

> [!warning] Principles are NOT metrics
> This is the single most common and most damaging error, so I'll state it flatly: **a principle is not a KPI.** "We win with fans" is not "grow MAU 10% this quarter." A principle shapes *how you decide*; a metric tells you *whether the decision worked*. The two belong in different documents and serve different masters. Teams that "operationalize" their principles into measurable targets have quietly rebuilt their [[OKRs and Goal-Setting|OKRs]] and thrown away the thing that was guiding the *choices* the OKRs measure. Keep them separate: principles pick the direction, metrics check the result, and neither can do the other's job.

## How to write and keep them alive

A few rules I follow. **Keep it to a handful** — three to five. A list of twelve is a list of zero, because no one holds twelve rules in mind at a decision point. **Earn each one against a real past argument** — the test of a good principle is that it would have *resolved* a debate you actually had; if you can't name the argument it settles, cut it. **Write from conviction, not consensus** — principles sanded down until no one objects are by definition not spiky and therefore useless. And the survival test: **a principle is only real if someone cites it to change a decision.** If they live on a wiki page nobody opens, they're decoration. I'd rather have three principles people argue *with* than a dozen they nod *at*.

> [!tip] The hallway test
> Ask a teammate to state your principles from memory and then describe a recent decision one of them changed. If they can do both, the principles are working. If they can recite them but can't name a decision, you've written slogans. If they can't recite them at all, you've written a document.
