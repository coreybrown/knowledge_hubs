# The LNO Framework

Most prioritization tells you *which* tasks to do. **Shreyas Doshi's LNO framework** answers a different and badly neglected question: *how much effort does each task deserve?* It is the antidote to the perfectionist instinct that treats every task as worthy of your A-game. That instinct sounds admirable, and it is actually how PMs burn out while their highest-leverage work goes half-done.

Doshi's insight is that effort should be allocated by a task's *leverage*, not by its presence on your list or by how visible it is. He sorts tasks into three types:

> [!note] The three task types
> **Leverage (L):** Output value is *many times* the effort you put in. The product strategy memo, the hire, the framing of a hard problem, the customer insight that redirects the roadmap. Pour your best energy here, and *over*-invest, because the returns are super-linear.
> **Neutral (N):** Output value is *roughly proportional* to effort. Most routine PM work: a standard spec, a typical review. Do it competently and move on, because more polish does not pay.
> **Overhead (O):** Output value stays *low no matter how much* you put in. Expense reports, status formatting, scheduling, admin. The correct strategy is to **satisfice**: do the minimum acceptable, automate, or delegate. Effort here is nearly wasted.

| Type | Effort → value | The move |
|---|---|---|
| Leverage | Super-linear | Over-invest; bring your A-game |
| Neutral | Linear | Do it well, stop there |
| Overhead | Flat / low | Minimum viable; automate or delegate |

## Why this is the framework I correct people with most

> [!tip] Perfectionism is misallocated effort, not virtue
> The most common and most expensive mistake I see is applying *uniform* high effort across every task: gold-plating the Overhead and Neutral work while the one Leverage task gets your tired leftover hours. LNO reframes quality itself. Excellence is not doing everything excellently. It is matching effort to leverage. A B-minus on Overhead so you can deliver an A-plus on Leverage is not laziness, it is judgment, and internalizing that is one of the bigger force-multipliers in the job.

## How it fits with everything else

LNO operates on a different axis than the *what* frameworks, so it stacks cleanly with them. Once [[The Impact-Likelihood Matrix|Impact-Likelihood]] or [[RICE and Scoring Models|RICE]] has told you *what* to do, LNO tells you *how hard to grind* on each piece. It is the natural partner to [[The Eisenhower Matrix|Eisenhower]] too: Eisenhower decides whether a task survives at all, and LNO decides the effort it earns if it does. It also rhymes with Doshi's own [[The Three Levels of Product Work|three levels of product work]], where Leverage tasks are usually Impact-level work and a lot of Overhead is Optics you can satisfice without guilt.

> [!warning] The classification is the hard part, and it drifts
> Two cautions. First, classification is judgment, not a formula, and people systematically misclassify the *visible* as Leverage because it gets noticed. Visibility is not leverage. Second, classifications are not permanent: a task that is Overhead for a Staff PM may be Leverage for an APM still building the skill. Re-sort by *your* current leverage, honestly. The framework is only as good as the cold eye you bring to the sort.
