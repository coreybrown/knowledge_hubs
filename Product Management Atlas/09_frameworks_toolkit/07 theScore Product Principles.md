# theScore Product Principles

These are the product principles I authored and used to run product at theScore. I'm including them not because every team should adopt *these four* — they're specific to a sports-media product with a passionate fan base — but because they're a worked example of what good principles look like: short, spiky, and decision-shaping. The *how-to* of writing your own lives in [[Writing Good Product Principles]]; this note is the artifact itself.

A principle's job is to make the right call obvious when I'm not in the room. Good ones resolve arguments. Here are mine.

> [!quote] Have a shooters mentality
> Be bold. Don't be afraid to fail. The best products come from teams willing to take the shot — to ship the ambitious thing rather than the safe one. We'd rather miss on a bold bet than win small on a timid one. This is the principle that gives a junior PM permission to propose the audacious idea, and it's why I tolerate intelligent failures.

> [!quote] Balance game sense with data
> Use qualitative, quantitative, *and* intuition together — and beware analysis paralysis. Data informs the next iteration; it doesn't get a veto over judgment, and judgment doesn't get to ignore the numbers. Two corollaries I lean on: data should *inform the next iteration* (not just grade the last one), and **don't beat a dead horse** — when the signal is clear, stop measuring and move.

> [!quote] We win with fans
> The fan sits at the core of every decision. Build **painkillers, not vitamins** — solve real, felt problems over nice-to-haves. Unify roadmaps around the fan rather than around internal org charts. And remember that fans drive the bottom line: love precedes revenue, in that order.

> [!quote] Talk it up
> Communicate relentlessly. **Ship and share** — every launch comes with a story. **Teach, don't tell** — bring people along, don't just inform them. **Breadcrumbs, not loaves** — share progress in small, frequent pieces rather than one giant reveal. And the **first-15-seconds rule**: if you can't make someone care in fifteen seconds, you haven't found the point yet.

## Why these four, and how they're built

Each follows the anatomy I insist on in [[Writing Good Product Principles]]: **specific and actionable** (you can decide with them), **short** (they fit in your head), **spiky** (a real point of view someone could reasonably disagree with), and **motivational** (they sound like a team you'd want to join). "We win with fans" is spiky — it explicitly subordinates internal politics to the fan. "Painkillers over vitamins" makes a prioritization call before you've seen the feature.

> [!warning] Principles are not metrics
> The single most important thing about these: **they are not KPIs.** "We win with fans" is not "grow MAU 10%." A principle shapes *how you decide*; a metric tells you *whether it worked*. Teams that turn principles into measurable targets have built [[OKRs and Goal-Setting|OKRs]] and lost the point. Keep them separate — the principle picks the direction, the metric checks the result.

The other failure mode is the poster on the wall nobody uses. A principle earns its keep only when it *changes a decision* — when someone says "we're not doing that, it's a vitamin." If nobody ever cites them in an argument, they're decoration, and I'd rather have four real ones than twelve that sound nice.
