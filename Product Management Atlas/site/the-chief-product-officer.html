🪜The Career Ladder

The Chief Product Officer

3 min read·705 words
career-laddercpolevelexecutivemanager-track

The top of The Product Career Ladder, and product's seat in the C-suite. By the time someone reaches CPO, the question is no longer "are they a good PM?" The twelve competencies are assumed mastered; the job is now company leadership through the product lens. This is the rung where Recognized Thought Leader is the baseline, not the stretch.

✦My one-line definition

The CPO owns the product function company-wide and co-owns company strategy as a peer to the CEO, CTO, and CFO. They are accountable for whether product (as a discipline, an org, and a strategy) drives the business. Their "team" is every product person in the company, led through a layer of VPs.

#What the role actually is

A CPO sits at the intersection the whole craft is built on (customer, business, technology), but at company scale. The job has three faces:

FaceWhat it means
StrategyCo-author of company strategy; decides where the company plays and how product wins (The DHM Model, Business Acumen and Models).
OrganizationOwns the product operating model, the leveling/career system, and product culture across the whole org.
The executive teamA peer to the CEO/CTO/CFO; the voice of the customer and the product in every company-level decision.

#The competencies at C-level

All twelve are table stakes. What separates a CPO is the altitude, and three competencies are now nearly the entire job:

CompetencyAt CPO
Strategic ImpactSets company strategy through product; this is the dominant competency.
Team LeadershipLeads the whole function through VPs; sets product culture, the leveling system, the hiring bar; and is judged on the health of the org, not any product.
Managing Up / Executive Communication"Up" is now the board. Frames product strategy in company-financial terms to investors and the CEO.
Stakeholder ManagementAligns the entire executive team and external partners around the product strategy.
Product Vision and RoadmappingOwns and evangelizes the company product vision (Crafting a Product Vision).
ℹEMPOWERED, at company scale

Cagan's product-leadership model is the right lens for this rung: leadership is strategic context + coaching + staffing. A CPO provides the context (vision, strategy, principles) that lets empowered product teams across the company make good decisions, and builds the org and culture that produces strong product people. They don't make product decisions; they build the system that makes good product decisions reliably.

#How a great CPO operates

  • Lead through context, not control. A CPO who's still making feature calls has failed to scale. Set strategy and principles; let the org execute (OKRs and Goal-Setting).
  • Own the product culture deliberately. Whether the company runs empowered product teams or a feature factory is, ultimately, the CPO's choice, expressed through what they fund, reward, and tolerate.
  • Build and defend the leveling system. The entire ladder in this domain is, at scale, a CPO's responsibility: that levels mean something, that Calibration and Promotion is honest, that Score-Gated Leveling isn't title inflation.
  • Be the customer's credible voice in the C-suite. With Storytelling with Data and Executive Communication, translate user reality into terms a board will fund.
▲Two myths about the top

"CPO = best PM in the company." No. This is a general-management job with a product mandate. The best individual PM is often happier and more valuable on the senior IC track. "It's the only summit." Also no. A Distinguished PM (the IC apex) and a CPO are both ends of the ladder (The IC and Manager Tracks): one leads the company, the other advances the craft. Two different summits, not a higher one and a lower one.
