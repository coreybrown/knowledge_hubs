The Kano Model

3 min read·617 words
frameworkskanoprioritizationcustomer-insightsatisfaction

Most prioritization frameworks ask which feature to build. Kano asks a better question: what kind of value does this feature create, and how does that value decay over time? That second clause is the part people forget, and it's the whole point. I reach for Kano when a team is arguing about table stakes versus differentiators and talking past each other because they're scoring features on a single axis that doesn't exist.

Noriaki Kano developed the model in 1984 to break the lazy assumption that satisfaction is linear — that more feature equals more happy. It isn't. Kano plots two axes: how present a feature is (horizontal) against how satisfied the customer is (vertical). Different feature types trace different curves through that space.

#The categories

ℹThe three that matter

Basic (must-be): Expected. Their presence earns nothing; their absence is a dealbreaker. A login that works, a checkout that doesn't lose your cart. You can't win on these — you can only lose. Invest exactly enough to not lose. Performance (one-dimensional): Linear. More is better, less is worse, and customers can articulate the trade. Page speed, storage, price. This is where you compete head-to-head and where most roadmap debate lives. Delight (attractive): Unexpected. Absence costs nothing because nobody asked; presence creates disproportionate love. These are the features that get screenshotted. You can't get them from a survey asking "what do you want" — customers don't know to want them.

Two more round it out: Indifferent (nobody cares — kill these) and Reverse (the feature some users actively dislike, which is why one-size-fits-all settings are dangerous).

BasicNeutralAngryMeet the bar, no more
PerformanceHappyUnhappyCompete; pick your level
DelightDelightedNeutralWhere differentiation lives
IndifferentNeutralNeutralDon't build it

#The insight people miss: features decay

▲Today's delight is tomorrow's basic

Categories are not fixed. A front-facing camera was a delighter; now it's a basic — its absence would be unthinkable. This is the most actionable thing in Kano: a differentiator has a half-life. If your strategy depends on a delighter, assume competitors copy it and the market re-baselines it into a basic. The hard-to-copy question is exactly how long you get to enjoy the delight before it commoditizes.

#When it's useful, when it's theater

I use Kano as a conversation lens, not a scoring engine. The formal version — a survey with paired functional/dysfunctional questions, then a categorization table — is rigorous and occasionally worth it for a major investment decision. But most teams don't need the survey. They need the vocabulary: "that's a basic, stop gold-plating it" and "that's our one delighter, protect the resourcing." The theater version is running the full survey to justify a decision you'd already made, or treating the categories as permanent. Pair Kano with The Impact-Likelihood Matrix for the how risky axis it doesn't cover, and with RICE when you need to rank within a category.

A trap: chasing delighters while basics are broken. Delight on top of a broken basic is lipstick. Fix the login first.
