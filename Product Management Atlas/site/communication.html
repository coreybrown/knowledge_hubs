🤝Influencing People

Communication

3 min read·701 words
influencing-peoplecommunicationwritingverbalcore-skill

If I could keep only one skill for a PM, it would be this one. Not strategy, not product sense, not data. Communication. Everything a PM produces — a spec, a roadmap, a decision, a vision — is inert until it's transmitted into another person's head and changes what they do. A brilliant idea you can't communicate is functionally identical to no idea at all.

#My point of view: this is the #1 PM skill

I'm not alone here. Lenny Rachitsky, after studying what separates great PMs, ranks the top three skills in order: communication, then execution, then product sense. Communication sits above execution. His logic matches my experience: a PM's leverage is entirely indirect — you act through other people — so your effectiveness is capped by how clearly you can move information and conviction between minds.

Brandon Chu put it even more sharply: the PM is "the API" of the team — the interface every other function calls to coordinate. An API with a garbled contract breaks everything downstream. That's what a PM with poor communication is: a broken interface.

✦The test I apply

Good PM communication isn't measured by how much you said — it's measured by what the listener does next. If your update didn't change a priority, unblock a decision, or align a team, it didn't communicate. Output is words; outcome is behavior.

#Written vs verbal

These are different skills and most PMs are lopsided. Know which you're weaker at.

What it's forWhere PMs fail
WrittenAsync alignment at scale, durable decisions, the spec, the updateBurying the point; writing for themselves, not the reader; length as a proxy for rigor
VerbalReal-time alignment, persuasion, conflict, reading the roomTalking past the point; not listening; mistaking talking for communicating

Writing is the higher-leverage half, because it scales — one well-written doc aligns fifty people you'll never meet, and it persists after you leave the room. It's also the more honest half: muddled writing is a reliable tell for muddled thinking. The named behaviors in my model reflect this — clear, concise, empathetic communication across formats shows up under both Product Delivery and Stakeholder Management.

#Tailoring to the audience

The single highest-leverage habit: the same message, rebuilt for each audience. A roadmap change is not one communication — it's at least four.

  • To engineering — the why behind the trade-off, the constraint, the technical context.
  • To design — the user problem and the experience bar.
  • To executives — the business impact, in one line, up front (Executive Communication).
  • To the wider org — the narrative: what's changing, why it matters, what to do (Storytelling with Data).

A PM who sends the same generic update to everyone has confused broadcasting with communicating. The information went out; the alignment didn't happen.

▲Myth: communication means being a good talker

The charismatic PM who dominates meetings is often a worse communicator than the quiet one whose docs are crystalline. Communication includes active listening — and the loud ones are frequently the ones not listening. Don't confuse presence with effectiveness.

#How AI is changing it

This is the most interesting tension in the whole domain. AI is a phenomenal writing tool — it drafts the update, tightens the prose, restructures the doc. So one reading is that AI erodes the value of communication. I think the opposite. As the Reforge "higher ground" framing argues, when first drafts get cheap, the bottleneck moves to clarity of thought — knowing what you actually want to say and to whom. AI can fix your grammar; it can't decide your point. Lenny's framing is that PMs become "editors of super-intelligent suggestions" — and editing is a communication skill. The medium gets easier; the judgment underneath gets more valuable. (Competencies AI Commoditizes vs Elevates.)
