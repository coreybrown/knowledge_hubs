The Frameworks Toolkit

The frameworks I actually reach for, and when each one is just theater.

4 min read·804 words

This is my opinionated index of every framework in the Atlas, the tools I actually reach for, grouped by the job they do. I keep them in one place because the most common framework mistake is not picking the "wrong" one. It is not knowing which job you are doing, so you grab whatever is fashionable and start filling in its boxes as if the boxes were the answer.

▲A framework is a tool, never a box to fill in

Frameworks do not produce decisions. They produce structured arguments. They force your criteria into the open and make your reasoning auditable instead of political. The output of any of these is an input to judgment, not a substitute for it. The moment a team treats "we filled in the RICE sheet" or "we ran a SWOT" as having decided something, the tool has become theater. Use them to force the conversation, then bring taste. And pick a few you will use consistently, because framework-hopping every quarter destroys the one real benefit, which is comparability over time.

#How to read this index

Find the job to be done, then the tool. Many frameworks live in other domains (discovery tools in Customer Insight, strategy tools in Product Strategy), but they are all indexed here so this is the one map you need.

#Attribution

I built The Impact-Likelihood Matrix and theScore Product Principles, and The 10-50-90 Execution Framework is theScore's. The rest belong to the people who created them: Shreyas Doshi (LNO and the three levels), Petra Wille (The PM Wheel), Noriaki Kano (The Kano Model), Eric Ries (Lean Startup), Dai Clegg (MoSCoW), Eisenhower/Covey, Humphrey (SWOT) and Porter (Five Forces). I keep the credit for a practical reason and not just a polite one. Borrowed frameworks carry their authors' assumptions, and knowing the source is how you know the limits.
