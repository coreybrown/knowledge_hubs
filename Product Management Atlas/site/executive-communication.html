🤝Influencing People

Executive Communication

4 min read·756 words
influencing-peoplecommunicationexecutivesblufmanaging-up

A specialized, high-stakes branch of Communication and the core craft behind Managing Up. Talking to executives is not talking to your team with bigger words. It is a different game with different rules, and PMs who do not learn them get filtered out, not because their work is bad but because it never lands.

#The one thing to understand about executives

They are short on time and attention, and long on context you lack. They sit across many product areas, pattern-match against years of bets, and decide where the next dollar and headcount go. Every norm below falls out of that single fact. Write for someone who has ninety seconds, has seen this movie before, and is asking "so what should I do about it?"

#BLUF: bottom line up front

The most important habit, and the one PMs resist hardest. Lead with the conclusion. The ask, the recommendation, the headline number, all in the first sentence. Then the supporting detail, for those who want it.

PMs are trained to build an argument: context, then analysis, then conclusion. That structure is for people who will read to the end. Executives will not. They want the answer, and they will pull on the threads they care about. Burying your recommendation under three paragraphs of setup is the single most common way smart PMs lose an executive's attention.

◆Same content, two openings

Buries it: "Over the last quarter we ran three experiments on onboarding, analyzed retention cohorts, consulted with data science, and after reviewing the funnel we believe..." BLUF: "Recommendation: kill the new onboarding flow. It dropped activation 8%. Detail and the three experiments behind it below." The second one respects the reader. The first makes them dig.

#Business framing

Executives think in business outcomes, not features. Translate everything into their currency before you walk in:

  • Not "we shipped the referral feature" but "referrals drove 12% of new signups this month."
  • Not "engineering needs two more sprints" but "two more weeks de-risks a launch tied to our Q3 OKR."
  • Tie it to KPIs, ROI, and the metrics they are accountable for (Strategic Impact).

If you cannot state the business impact of your work in one sentence, you are not ready for the exec conversation, and that gap is usually a sign the work lacks a clear outcome, not just the framing.

#Brevity

Be ruthless. One slide where you had planned five. One sentence where you had written a paragraph. Brevity is not dumbing down. It is having done the synthesis for the reader rather than making them do it. A long memo to an executive often signals you have not found the point yourself.

#Anticipating questions

The behavior that separates senior communicators from junior ones. Before any exec interaction, I write down the three hardest questions they could ask, the skeptical ones, the "what about cost," the "why not the cheaper option," and I have crisp answers ready. There are two payoffs. I am never caught flat, and I often pre-empt the question entirely by addressing it in the appendix. Half of managing up is doing the executive's thinking for them.

✦Calm and solution-oriented

When something is on fire, the executive is reading you as much as the data. Lead with the situation, the plan, and what you need, not anxiety. "Here is what broke, here is what we are doing, here is the one decision I need from you" beats a panicked data dump every time. Composure under pressure is itself a credibility signal.

#How AI is changing it

Executive comms is mechanically AI-friendly. Drafting the board slide, compressing a quarter into three bullets, restructuring rambling notes into BLUF order, all of it is minutes of work now (Competencies AI Commoditizes vs Elevates). I use it as a first-pass editor routinely. What it cannot do is the judgment underneath: which metric this particular executive cares about, which risk is worth surfacing, and how much candor the moment calls for. The slide is the easy part. Knowing what belongs on it, and what the executive will actually do with it, stays a human read.
