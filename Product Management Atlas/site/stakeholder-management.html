🤝Influencing People

Stakeholder Management

4 min read·791 words
influencing-peoplecompetencystakeholder-managementalignment

The first competency in Influencing People, and the one most PMs underrate until it bites them. You can have the right strategy, a clean spec, and a shipping team — and still get blocked, blindsided, or overruled by someone you forgot to bring along.

#What it is

●The definition I use

Stakeholder Management — The ability to proactively identify stakeholders impacted by the PM's area and to work with them to factor their requirements into product decisions.

Two words in there carry the weight. Proactively — the failure mode is always reactive, finding out about a constraint after it's a crisis. And factor in — not obey. The job isn't to satisfy every stakeholder; it's to surface their needs early and make a defensible decision that accounts for them. Saying no to a stakeholder well is stakeholder management.

#The behaviors

I break this competency into three named behaviors.

#Internal Stakeholder Influence & Alignment

Building relationships with the people inside your company your work depends on or affects — through regular touchpoints, not just when you need something. The core moves: align on goals, priorities, and metrics before the conflict; communicate persuasively and adapt to the audience; and resolve conflict with empathy rather than escalation. The PMs who are good at this rarely seem to be fighting battles, because they've already had the conversation offline.

#External Stakeholder Management

Trusted relationships with the people outside the building — customers, partners, vendors, sometimes regulators. The work: translate external feedback into product direction (without letting the loudest customer set your roadmap), represent the company credibly, manage expectations transparently, and create mutual value. In my B2C mobile world this meant league partners, payment providers, and platform owners (Apple, Google) whose constraints I didn't control but had to design around.

#Org-Based Communications

Articulating vision, strategy, and priorities at every level of the org, adapting the message to each. The same roadmap update lands differently for an engineer, a marketer, and a VP. This behavior leans hard on Storytelling with Data: transparent updates that combine narrative and numbers so people stay aligned without you in the room.

#How it shows up by level

The escalation here is about scope of influence — from one feature to the whole company.

LevelWhat stakeholder management looks like
APMIdentifies the obvious stakeholders for one feature, with manager support; keeps them informed
PMIndependently maps and aligns stakeholders for complex work; runs the touchpoints; handles routine conflict
Senior PMOwns alignment across multiple teams; trusted to manage high-stakes external relationships; resolves real disputes
GPM / DirectorAligns stakeholders at the org level; sets the communication cadence others follow; manages executive-tier partners
VP+Builds the company-wide norms for how product communicates and aligns; the relationships are the strategy

#How to grow it

  • Make a stakeholder map and keep it current. Start with Internal and External Stakeholders — who's impacted, who decides, who just wants to be informed. Pair it with RACI and Decision Rights so "stakeholder" doesn't blur into "person who can veto me."
  • Front-load the relationship. Schedule the touchpoint before you need the favor. Influence runs on a trust balance you fund in advance.
  • Practice the persuasive update until people act on your data stories without a meeting.
  • Get good at the no. Acknowledge the need, explain the trade-off, offer the alternative. A respected no beats a resented yes.

#How AI is changing it

The mechanical layer — drafting the stakeholder update, summarizing the meeting, writing the status report — is exactly the work AI commoditizes first (a top example in Competencies AI Commoditizes vs Elevates). I now generate a first-draft update in seconds. What doesn't commoditize is the judgment about whose requirements to weight, the read on a room, and the trust you've banked. As Lenny Rachitsky puts it, the durable PM core is the "people/glue" work — and this is the heart of it. AI writes the memo; it can't have the hard hallway conversation for you.

▲Myth: stakeholder management means keeping everyone happy

No. That's how you get a watered-down, design-by-committee product. The goal is alignment, not universal approval — everyone heard, the trade-offs explicit, the decision owned. A PM optimizing for zero unhappy stakeholders is optimizing for the wrong thing.
