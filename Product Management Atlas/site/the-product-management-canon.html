📚Reference

The Product Management Canon

4 min read·907 words
referencereading-listcanonbooksresources

The reading list I actually recommend — not everything ever written about product, just the work that shaped how I think and that I'd hand to a PM at any level. I've ordered it roughly by where it lands in a career, and I say why each one matters. Read fewer of these more deeply than all of them once.

✦How to use a reading list

Don't binge. Pick one book that maps to your current gap (use Identifying Growth Areas), read it slowly, and apply one idea before moving on. A book you applied beats ten you skimmed.

#The foundations

Marty Cagan — INSPIRED (and EMPOWERED). INSPIRED is the closest thing the field has to a canonical text on how strong product teams work; EMPOWERED is its sequel on product leadership. If you read one PM book, read INSPIRED. It's the source of The Four Big Product Risks (value, usability, feasibility, viability), the product-vs-feature-team distinction, and the line I repeat most: we want competent people with character. EMPOWERED picks up where most ladders go thin — the craft of leading and coaching product people. Both are by Cagan and the SVPG partners.

Teresa Torres — Continuous Discovery Habits. The most practical book on discovery I know. Torres turns "talk to users" into a weekly habit owned by the product trio, and gives you the Opportunity Solution Tree to connect outcomes to solutions. If your problem is "we build things nobody wanted," this is the fix.

Melissa Perri — Escaping the Build Trap. Perri names the disease — shipping features instead of moving outcomes — and prescribes the cure. It's the book I give anyone whose org confuses being busy with making progress. The whole of Outcomes Over Outputs lives here.

#Strategy and judgment

Dan Olsen — The Lean Product Playbook. The clearest step-by-step on product-market fit and the MVP I've found. Olsen's Product-Market Fit Pyramid is a genuinely usable model when you're starting from zero. Tactical where Cagan is philosophical.

Gibson Biddle — DHM and the strategy talks. Biddle (ex-Netflix) isn't primarily a book, but his DHM model — strategy should Delight, be Hard to copy, and be Margin-enhancing — is the sharpest one-line test for a product strategy I use. His "How to Define Your Product Strategy" essays are required reading for anyone moving into strategy.

#Leveling, growth, and the PM as a person

Petra Wille — Strong Product People. The best book on the human side: coaching, growth, and self-awareness for PMs and their leaders. Wille's PMwheel is a self-assessment radar, and her thesis — great product leaders make strong product people — underwrites the entire Assessment and Coaching domain. This is the one most reading lists miss.

Ravi Mehta & Reforge — the competency model. The intellectual backbone of this Atlas. Mehta's "Peak Product Manager" work (with Reforge) is where the 4 areas × 12 competencies come from, and where the spiky-not-well-rounded idea is best argued. Reforge's broader courses are the most rigorous paid PM education going, if you can get access.

#The newsletters and the modern layer

Lenny Rachitsky — Lenny's Newsletter. The default starting point for the field today. Lenny's compiled career ladders from dozens of companies, ranks the core skills (communication → execution → product sense), and his guest essays are a rolling syllabus. If you subscribe to one thing, subscribe to this.

Shreyas Doshi. Not a book — threads and talks — but his frameworks punch above their weight: the three levels of product work (impact / execution / optics), the LNO effort model, and "most execution problems are strategy problems." Read him when you feel busy but not impactful.

John Cutler. Coined the "feature factory" anti-pattern and popularized the North Star Framework. Sharp, contrarian, and good for breaking lazy product habits.

ℹThe AI-PM voices (the new canon, forming now)

The AI shift is being written in real time on Substack and in courses, not books yet. The voices I track: Lenny Rachitsky and Reforge ("Moving to Higher Ground," the AI Evals course) on how AI reshapes the job; Aakash Gupta on AI product sense and evals; Claire Vo (ChatPRD, "Product Management Is Dead") on the generalist-specialist shift; Kevin Weil (OpenAI) on evals as a core PM skill; Cat Wu (Anthropic) on building on a moving model; and Ethan Mollick (Co-Intelligence) on the jagged frontier. This is the reading list for AI and the Modern PM.

#A note on the "CEO of the product" books

A lot of older PM writing leans on the "PM is the CEO of the product" framing. I think it's actively misleading and I argue against it in Myths and Misconceptions. Read those books for tactics if you like, but don't internalize the metaphor.
