🪜The Career Ladder

Director and VP of Product

4 min read·747 words
career-ladderdirectorvp-productlevelmanager-track

The product-leadership rungs of The Product Career Ladder, above GPM. The defining shift here is altitude of both people and strategy: you stop managing ICs and start leading through other managers, and you move from implementing strategy to setting it. This is where the job becomes mostly about organizational design and direction, and barely at all about shipping features yourself.

✦My one-line definition

A Director runs the product function for a domain — through managers — and translates company strategy into a coherent product strategy. A VP owns product strategy for a large slice (or all) of the business and leads through a management team. Both succeed or fail on whether the organization produces outcomes, not whether any one team does.

#Scope, autonomy, complexity

Master of Discipline on The Six Stages of Mastery — the level that defines how the discipline is practiced across the org.

RungComplexityAutonomy
DirectorImplements strategy; leads complex issues to focused solutions; runs collaborative cross-functional process across a department.Consultative direction across multiple teams; directs multilayered managers.
(Senior Director)Moves from implementing to setting functional strategy; impacts org objectives and long-term performance.Sets strategic direction across functions; begins to impact orgs outside their own; manages multilayered directors.
VPExecutes functional business plans; contributes to functional and business strategy; collaborates with exec leadership.Full autonomy; leads through a management team.

The throughline: leading through managers and setting (not just executing) strategy. A VP's hands are nowhere near a Jira board.

#The competencies at the leadership level

The same twelve, at the highest org altitude. Two clusters dominate.

CompetencyWhat it looks like here
Strategic ImpactSets functional/business strategy; anticipates industry risk and leverage points (Business Acumen and Models, The DHM Model).
Product Vision and RoadmappingOwns the vision for a large portfolio; reconciles and funds roadmaps across many teams.
Business Outcome OwnershipAccountable for business-line outcomes, not feature outcomes.
Team LeadershipProduct-wide leadership: builds the org structure, the hiring machine, the standards, the product culture; develops managers, not just PMs.
Stakeholder ManagementPeer to other functional VPs; manages the most senior stakeholders.
Managing Up / Executive CommunicationOperates as an executive; frames product in P&L terms to the CEO and board.
ℹThe work is now organizational design

At this altitude, "doing product" means deciding how product gets done: team topology (Product Teams vs Feature Teams), the operating model (The Product Organization Matrix), how strategy cascades (OKRs and Goal-Setting), and who you hire and promote (Calibration and Promotion). You're building the system that builds the product.

#How to be a great product leader

  • Set context, don't give answers. The EMPOWERED-team model is right: your job is to provide strategic context (vision, strategy, principles, objectives) and then let empowered teams solve problems. Hand teams problems to own, not solutions to build.
  • Build the bench. Your legacy is the managers and PMs you develop and the team you build — not your own roadmap. Promote your replacement.
  • Defend product-team culture. Hold the line against the feature-factory drift that pressure and scale invite. Culture is set by what leaders tolerate.
  • Communicate as an executive. Executive Communication and Storytelling with Data are now core craft; you sell strategy to peers and the board, not features to a team.
▲The myth at this altitude

"A VP is just a really senior PM." No. The competencies are the same, but the job is organizational leadership — most of your day is people, strategy, and politics, and almost none of it is product execution. PMs who get promoted on execution and then keep executing make terrible Directors. The skill that got you here is not the skill that keeps you here. (What Changes at Each Level.)

#What's above

The top product rung is The Chief Product Officer — product in the C-suite, owning the function company-wide and co-owning company strategy.
