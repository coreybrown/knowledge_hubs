📊Assessment & Coaching

The Manager as Coach

4 min read·851 words
assessmentcoachingmanagementleadershipcagan

If you take one thing from this domain, take this: coaching is the job. Not a part of the job, not the soft side of the job — the central work of managing PMs. Everything else (planning, reviews, hiring) exists to serve it.

#The Cagan claim I build on

Marty Cagan, in EMPOWERED, argues that coaching is a manager's number-one responsibility — and that, done right, coaching and staffing together take up the majority of a first-level manager's week (Cagan puts it near 80%). When I first read that I thought it was an exaggeration. Having run teams, I don't anymore — for anyone with junior reports, it is the job. Cagan splits the manager role into two halves: management (coaching + staffing) and leadership (strategic context, vision, strategy). The coaching half is the one new managers neglect, because it's invisible, slow, and doesn't show up on a roadmap.

●Why it's #1

Cagan's logic: your team's results are capped by the capability of the people on it. You can't out-strategize a team you haven't grown. The highest-leverage thing a manager does isn't make better decisions — it's create more people who make better decisions. (Marty Cagan, SVPG / EMPOWERED.)

This is also the moment Team Leadership stops being abstract. Its first behavior — Coaching & Performance — is this note. And the transition from doing the work to making the people who do the work better is the entire jump into management (The IC vs Manager Decision).

#How I coach

Coaching is not feedback (that's Giving and Receiving Feedback) and it's not the annual review (that's Running a Performance Review). It's the continuous, individualized work of growing a specific person.

  • The 1:1 is their meeting, not my status update. The fastest way to ruin coaching is to turn the 1:1 into a project check-in. Status has other channels. This time is for them — their growth, blockers, and the stuff they're avoiding.
  • Tailor to the person. The model is explicit that coaching is tailored to each person's strengths and goals (Coaching and Feedback). A confident senior PM needs me to be blunt and get out of the way; a shaky APM needs scaffolding and reps. Identical coaching for everyone means I'm really just talking to myself.
  • Coach to the gap, not the symptom. A slipping launch looks like a delivery problem; underneath it's often a prioritization or stakeholder gap. I trace the symptom to the competency (Identifying Growth Areas) and coach that.
  • Model the practice. The Coaching & Performance behavior includes modeling strong practices yourself. You cannot coach a spec standard you don't visibly hold. Hypocrisy is the fastest way to lose a team's trust.
  • Create a safe space to learn. People grow by trying things slightly beyond their reach and sometimes failing. If failure gets punished, they stop reaching, and coaching dies. My job is to make the stretch safe.
✦The assessment tools are coaching tools

The competency matrix and self-assessment aren't just for review season — they're the map a coaching conversation needs. Petra Wille makes this explicit: the PMwheel is built as a coaching and career-conversation tool, not a grading instrument. "Great product leaders make strong product people." Use the rubric to find the conversation, not to deliver a verdict.

▲Myth: coaching means having the answers

The worst coaching I've seen is a senior PM telling juniors exactly what to do. That builds dependence, not capability. Real coaching is mostly questions — "what are the risks here? what does the data say? what would you do if I were on vacation?" — that grow the person's own judgment. If your team can't function without you, you've been delegating tasks, not coaching people.

#What coaching is not

It's not therapy, it's not friendship, and it's not lowering the bar to be liked. The kindest coaching holds a high standard and gives people the support to reach it. Niceness that costs someone their growth isn't kind (Giving and Receiving Feedback).

#How AI changes it

Almost not at all — this is the competency AI touches least. AI can summarize a quarter of 1:1s or surface team-health signals (Competencies AI Commoditizes vs Elevates), but knowing what a person needs to hear, reading whether they're ready to hear it, and holding the relationship that makes hard truths land as care — none of that commoditizes. If anything, as mechanical PM work gets automated, growing the humans who do the irreducible work becomes a larger share of what a leader is for.
