🪜The Career Ladder

The Six Stages of Mastery

3 min read·695 words
career-laddermasteryprogressionframework

Titles are the surface of The Product Career Ladder. This is what runs underneath. When I level or coach a PM, I don't ask "are they a Senior?" I ask, for each competency, what stage of mastery they're operating at. That's a far more honest question, and it's the engine the whole ladder hangs off.

ℹLineage

The competencies these stages grade come from the Reforge / Ravi Mehta "Peak Product Manager" model (see The PM Competency Model). The six-stage mastery spine below, and the way I gate leveling on it, are my own layer on top.

#The spine

Six stages, applied per competency, not per person:

StageWhat it looks like in practice
1. Gaining ExperienceLearning the competency by doing it, with support. Needs the problem framed for them. The APM zone.
2. Applies Practical KnowledgeDoes it independently on well-defined work. The reliable PM.
3. Applies Advanced KnowledgeHandles complex, ambiguous, interrelated work; finds the right level of detail without being told. The Senior zone.
4. Subject Matter ExpertThe person others go to on this competency; coaches it; raises the bar around them. Staff / GPM.
5. Master of DisciplineDefines how the discipline is practiced across the org: process, standards, methodology. Director/VP.
6. Recognized Thought LeaderRecognized inside and outside the org as a definitional voice. CPO, Distinguished PM.
●The arc in one line

You move from being taught the work, to doing the work, to mastering the work, to teaching the work, to defining how the work is done, to advancing the field itself. Knows → Does → Masters → Teaches → Defines → Advances.

#Why "per competency" is the whole point

You do not sit at one stage, and this is the part I have to correct most often. Because PMs are spiky (The T-Shaped PM and Knowing Your Shape), a strong Senior PM might be a Subject Matter Expert in Product Delivery, Applies Advanced Knowledge in Strategic Impact, and still only Applies Practical Knowledge in Stakeholder Management. That isn't a problem to erase. It's a profile to manage, and the spine lets me describe a real human instead of forcing them onto a single number.

ℹHow a "level" is really computed

A level is a center of gravity across the twelve competencies, plus floor rules. You can't sit at stage 5 overall while a core competency languishes at stage 1. That floor logic is exactly how Score-Gated Leveling gates promotions, and it's why Calibration and Promotion exists: to align where someone actually operates with the title they carry.

#The stages map cleanly onto every scale I use

This spine isn't a separate system. It's the connective tissue between all the assessment tools in the Atlas, and the proficiency language lines up:

This spineProficiency / assessment scale
Gaining Experience"Knows" / Needs Focus
Applies Practical → Advanced Knowledge"Does" / On Track
Subject Matter Expert"Masters" / Outperform
Master of Discipline → Thought Leader"Teaches" / Leads

See The Proficiency Scale for the scoring mechanics and The Competency Self-Assessment for placing yourself.

▲Mastery is not tenure

The most common error I see is treating years-in-seat as a proxy for stage. Tenure correlates with mastery; it does not cause it. I've worked with ten-year PMs stuck at "Applies Practical Knowledge" because they did the same year ten times over. Stage is about the altitude of the work you reliably handle, not the date on your offer letter.
