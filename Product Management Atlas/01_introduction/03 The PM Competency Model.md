# The PM Competency Model

This is the spine of the whole Atlas. Over years of leveling and coaching PMs, I converged on a model with four competency **areas**, each holding three **competencies**, for twelve in total. It is adapted from the Reforge and Ravi Mehta "Peak Product Manager" model, which is the closest thing our field has to a complete definition of the job. What I have added is the part that makes it usable day to day: a verbatim set of **behaviors** under each competency, a **career ladder** mapped to it, and a **leveling system** that actually gates promotions ([[Score-Gated Leveling]]).

## The four areas and twelve competencies

| Area | What it means | The three competencies |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Product Execution]]** | Define, build, and launch exceptional products | [[Feature Specification]] · [[Product Delivery]] · [[Product Quality]] |
| **[[Customer Insight]]** | Understand and fulfill customer needs | [[Fluency with Data]] · [[Voice of the Customer]] · [[User Experience Design]] |
| **[[Product Strategy]]** | Deliver business impact via product innovation | [[Business Outcome Ownership]] · [[Strategic Impact]] · [[Product Vision and Roadmapping]] |
| **[[Influencing People]]** | Rally people around the team's work | [[Stakeholder Management]] · [[Team Leadership]] · [[Managing Up]] |

There is a rough arc here. Execution is where most careers start, and strategy and influence are where they mature. Treat that as a center of gravity, not a rule. Which brings me to the most important idea in the model.

## You are spiky, not well-rounded

> [!tip] Know your shape
> Almost nobody is strong across all twelve, and you do not need to be. The best PMs I have worked with are **spiky**. They are exceptional at three or four competencies and competent at the rest, and they win by knowing their shape and building teams that fill the gaps. Chasing a flat, uniformly "high" profile is a good way to end up mediocre at everything. More in [[The T-Shaped PM and Knowing Your Shape]].

## Levels: the same competencies, a higher bar

The twelve competencies do not change as you climb. The **altitude** does. I map progress on a six-stage mastery spine, running from *Gaining Experience* to *Recognized Thought Leader*, detailed in [[The Six Stages of Mastery]]. The same skill, say [[Feature Specification]], means "writes a clear spec for one feature with help" at the [[Associate Product Manager|APM]] level and "improves the spec process for every team" at the VP level. The full progression lives in [[The Product Career Ladder]], and the at-a-glance grid in [[The Master Competency Matrix]].

## Where this model is thin (and how I patch it)

I would rather be honest than dogmatic about this. A clean 4x12 grid under-weights a few things, so I treat these as first-class even though they do not sit neatly in one box: **discovery** as its own discipline ([[Continuous Discovery]], [[The Four Big Product Risks]]), **product sense** ([[Product Sense and Judgment]]), technical and **GTM** fluency, and, loudest of all right now, the way **AI is rewriting which competencies even matter** ([[Competencies AI Commoditizes vs Elevates]]).
