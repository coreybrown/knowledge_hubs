# What Is Product Management?

Here's my definition, and I'll defend it: **a product manager is accountable for whether a product succeeds.** Not for writing tickets, not for "owning the backlog," not for shipping a feature list on time. Those are activities. Success is an outcome, and the PM owns the outcome.

The job sits at the intersection of three forces — what **users** need, what the **business** needs, and what's **technically possible** — and the real work is judgment under uncertainty: choosing which problems are worth solving, and getting a team to solve them in a way customers love *and* the business can sustain. Everything in [[The PM Competency Model]] is downstream of that one sentence.

## What product management is *not*

> [!warning] The "mini-CEO" myth
> You'll hear that a PM is the "CEO of the product." It's a useful lie at best. You have all the accountability and almost none of the authority — you can't hire, fire, or order anyone to do anything. That's the whole game: you lead through influence, not control. See [[Leading Without Authority]].

Two more things it isn't. It is **not** the [[Product Manager vs Adjacent Roles|product owner]] role — running a backlog and writing stories is maybe 10% of the job, and the most automatable 10% (more in [[AI in Prototyping and Delivery]]). And it is **not** project management; a PM who only ships a predefined plan on a delivery track is, in my experience, a project manager with a fancier title. The difference is whether you're accountable for the *plan being right*, not just *executed*.

## The shape of the work

The clearest mental model is the overlap of disciplines — see [[The Product, Design, and Engineering Venn]]. The PM doesn't do design or engineering; the PM owns the **trade-offs** between them ([[Understanding Trade-offs]]) and is accountable for two of [[The Four Big Product Risks]]: is it **valuable** and is it **viable** for the business.

The other thing that separates good from great: an obsession with **outcomes over outputs** ([[Outcomes Over Outputs]]). Feature factories measure themselves by what they ship; real product teams measure themselves by what changes for the user and the business. Which kind of team you're on is the single best predictor of whether the product will work — see [[Product Teams vs Feature Teams]].

## Why it's hard to pin down

PM is inconsistently defined across companies, which is exactly why a clear competency model and career ladder matter. The role flexes with company stage ([[Product Management Across Company Stages]]) and specialization ([[Types of Product Managers]]) — an early-stage generalist and a platform PM at a 5,000-person company share a title and little else. The constant underneath all of it is [[Product Sense and Judgment]]: being right, more often than chance, about what to build.
