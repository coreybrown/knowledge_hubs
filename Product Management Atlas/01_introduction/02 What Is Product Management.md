# What Is Product Management?

Here is the definition I will defend: **a product manager is accountable for whether a product succeeds.** Not for writing tickets, not for "owning the backlog," not for shipping a feature list on time. Those are activities. Success is an outcome, and the PM owns the outcome.

The job sits at the intersection of three forces, what **users** need, what the **business** needs, and what is **technically possible**. The real work is judgment under uncertainty: choosing which problems are worth solving, then getting a team to solve them in a way customers love *and* the business can sustain. Everything in [[The PM Competency Model]] is downstream of that one sentence.

## What product management is *not*

> [!warning] The "mini-CEO" framing
> You will hear that a PM is the "CEO of the product," a line Ben Horowitz popularized. I think it is half-right and a little dangerous, and it is the framing I push back on most with new PMs. The half that is right: you carry the accountability. The half that gets people in trouble: you have almost none of the authority. You cannot hire, fire, or order anyone to do anything. That gap is the whole game, and you close it by leading through influence rather than control. See [[Leading Without Authority]].

Two more things the job is not. It is **not** the [[Product Manager vs Adjacent Roles|product owner]] role. Running a backlog and writing stories is maybe 10% of the work, and it is the most automatable 10% at that (more in [[AI in Prototyping and Delivery]]). It is also **not** project management. A PM who only ships a predefined plan on a delivery track is, in my experience, a project manager with a fancier title. The thing that separates the two is ownership of the *plan being right*, not just the plan getting *executed*.

## The shape of the work

The clearest mental model is the overlap of disciplines, which I lay out in [[The Product, Design, and Engineering Venn]]. The PM does not do the design or the engineering. What the PM owns is the **trade-offs** between them ([[Understanding Trade-offs]]), and accountability for two of [[The Four Big Product Risks]]: is it **valuable**, and is it **viable** for the business.

The other thing that separates good from great is an obsession with **outcomes over outputs** ([[Outcomes Over Outputs]]). Feature factories measure themselves by what they ship. Real product teams measure themselves by what changes for the user and the business. Which kind of team you are on tends to be the single best predictor of whether the product will work, and I unpack that in [[Product Teams vs Feature Teams]].

## Why it is hard to pin down

PM is defined inconsistently from company to company, which is exactly why a clear competency model and career ladder matter so much. The role flexes with company stage ([[Product Management Across Company Stages]]) and with specialization ([[Types of Product Managers]]). An early-stage generalist and a platform PM at a 5,000-person company share a title and not much else. The constant underneath all of it is [[Product Sense and Judgment]]: being right, more often than chance, about what to build.
