# Product Manager vs Adjacent Roles

Four roles share three letters or a word with "product manager" and get conflated with it constantly: Product Owner, Product Marketing Manager, Project Manager, and the engineering-driven "Technical Product Manager." Getting these wrong isn't pedantry. It's how PMs end up doing the wrong job well — grooming a backlog, writing release notes, or chasing a Gantt chart — while the actual product question goes unasked. Here's how I draw the lines.

## PM vs Product Owner

This is the one that matters most, so I'll be blunt and borrow from Marty Cagan, who's been blunt about it for years: **Product Owner is a role in a Scrum process, not a job.** Cagan's framing — that the PO responsibilities are roughly **10% of a real product manager's job** — is the cleanest correction I know. The PO half of the work is delivery hygiene: maintaining the backlog, clarifying stories for the sprint, accepting work. Necessary. But if that's *all* you do, you're a delivery clerk for someone else's decisions.

The other 90% is the product job: discovery, strategy, the [[The Four Big Product Risks|four risks]], the outcomes. Cagan's hard rule — **never split discovery from delivery** — exists because when you make "PO" a separate person from "PM," you sever the thinking from the doing, and you get a [[Product Teams vs Feature Teams|feature factory]] with extra ceremony.

> [!warning] The PO trap
> "Product Owner" as a permanent title is one of the surest signs of a company that has confused agile process with product strategy. It's also, as Cagan notes, the part of the role most exposed to AI — backlog grooming and ticket-writing are exactly what automation does well. Don't build a career there.

## PM vs Project Manager

A **project manager** is accountable for *delivering a defined scope on time and budget*. A **product manager** is accountable for *deciding what the scope should be and whether it's worth doing at all.* The project manager optimizes the plan; the PM owns whether there should be a plan. They're complements, not substitutes — on large programs you want both, and conflating them means either nobody owns the timeline or nobody owns the *why*. If your role is "make sure the thing ships on the date," you're doing valuable project management; just don't mistake it for product.

## PM vs Product Marketing

**Product Marketing (PMM)** owns positioning, messaging, go-to-market, and the narrative that reaches the market. The PM owns what gets built and why. The seam between them is [[Shipping and Launch|launch]], and it's where great products quietly die — built well, positioned poorly. I treat PMM as my closest cross-functional partner on the [[Competitive Landscape Analysis|market]] and commercialization side. The line: PM is accountable for the *product outcome*; PMM is accountable for the *market's understanding* of it.

## The role map

| Role | Accountable for | Time horizon | The trap |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Product Manager** | The outcome — right product, built, that works for users & business | Quarter to years | Doing the PO 10% and calling it the job |
| **Product Owner** | Backlog & sprint delivery (a Scrum role) | Sprint | Treating it as a standalone career |
| **Project Manager** | Defined scope, on time & budget | Project | Owning the *when* but not the *why* |
| **Product Marketing** | Positioning, messaging, GTM | Launch & campaign | Selling a product the PM mis-scoped |
| **Technical PM** | Product for technical users / platform | Quarter to years | Becoming an eng proxy, not a product owner — see [[Types of Product Managers]] |

> [!tip] The test
> If you can be fully evaluated on whether things shipped on time and to spec, you're holding an adjacent role. The PM is the only one on this list who can ship everything on time and still have failed — because the product didn't move the [[Outcomes Over Outputs|outcome]].
