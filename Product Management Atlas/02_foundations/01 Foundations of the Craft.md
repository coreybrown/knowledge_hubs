# Foundations of the Craft

Before you can grow a competency, you have to know what the job *is*. This domain is the bedrock the twelve competencies sit on — the shared definitions, distinctions, and mental models I assume you already hold by the time we talk about [[Feature Specification]] or [[Product Vision and Roadmapping]]. Skip these and the competency notes will feel like a checklist. Read these and they'll feel like a craft.

I built [[The PM Competency Model]] as the skeleton of this Atlas. But a skeleton needs ground to stand on. These thirteen notes are that ground: what a PM actually does, how the role differs from the ones it's confused with, the trade-offs at its core, the kinds of teams and orgs PMs work inside, and the judgment that separates a good PM from a great one.

## My one-paragraph thesis

Product management is the discipline of being **accountable for the outcome** of a product without owning the people or the code that produce it. You sit in the overlap of product, design, and engineering ([[The Product, Design, and Engineering Venn]]), and your core act is making and defending [[Understanding Trade-offs|trade-offs]] across the [[The Four Big Product Risks|four big risks]]. You do this through influence, not authority, which is why the whole back half of this Atlas is about [[Influencing People|people]]. Everything else is detail.

## How to read it

If you're new to the discipline, read top to bottom — the order is deliberate. If you're experienced, three notes are worth your time even if you skip the rest: [[The Four Big Product Risks]] (the cleanest model of what you're actually managing), [[The T-Shaped PM and Knowing Your Shape]] (the honest version of "what am I good at"), and [[Character, Competency, and Craft]] (why your best hire might be the one who interviews worst on frameworks).

> [!warning] The myth this domain kills
> That product management is a single, well-defined job you can master with a certification. It isn't. It's a *shape* of accountability that bends to the company, the stage, the team type, and the product. The foundations don't change; everything built on them does.
