# Foundations of the Craft

Before you can grow a competency, you have to know what the job *is*. This domain is the bedrock the twelve competencies sit on. It holds the shared definitions, distinctions, and mental models I assume you already carry by the time we talk about [[Feature Specification]] or [[Product Vision and Roadmapping]]. Skip these and the competency notes will read like a checklist. Read these and they read like a craft.

I built [[The PM Competency Model]] as the skeleton of this Atlas, and a skeleton needs ground to stand on. These thirteen notes are that ground: what a PM actually does, how the role differs from the ones it gets confused with, the trade-offs at its core, the kinds of teams and orgs PMs work inside, and the judgment that separates a good PM from a great one.

## My one-paragraph thesis

Product management is the discipline of being **accountable for the outcome** of a product without owning the people or the code that produce it. You sit in the overlap of product, design, and engineering ([[The Product, Design, and Engineering Venn]]), and your core act is making and defending [[Understanding Trade-offs|trade-offs]] across the [[The Four Big Product Risks|four big risks]]. You do all of this through influence rather than authority, which is why the whole back half of this Atlas is about [[Influencing People|people]]. Everything else is detail.

## How to read it

If you're new to the discipline, read top to bottom. The order is deliberate. If you're experienced, three notes earn your time even if you skip the rest: [[The Four Big Product Risks]] (the cleanest model of what you're actually managing), [[The T-Shaped PM and Knowing Your Shape]] (the honest version of "what am I good at"), and [[Character, Competency, and Craft]] (why your best hire might be the one who interviews worst on frameworks).

> [!warning] The myth this domain kills
> That product management is a single, well-defined job you can master with a certification. It isn't. It's a *shape* of accountability that bends to the company, the stage, the team type, and the product. The foundations hold steady. Everything built on them moves.
