# Product Sense and Judgment

Product sense is the competency nobody can fully define and everybody can recognize. It's why one PM looks at the same research as everyone else and proposes the thing that *works*, while another proposes something reasonable that lands flat. For a long time the field treated it as innate — you either have taste or you don't. I think that's both wrong and lazy. **Product sense is trainable**, it's one of the most valuable skills a PM can build, and the PMs who improve fastest are the ones who stop treating it as magic.

## What it actually is

The cleanest definition I've found comes from Lenny Rachitsky, who ranks it among the top three PM skills (alongside communication and execution): **product sense is the ability to consistently come up with product solutions that meet the needs of the people you're building for.** Two words in that sentence carry the weight — *consistently* and *needs*.

- **Consistently** is the operative word. Anyone can be right once; that's luck. Product sense is being right **more often than chance** — and being able to say *why* before the outcome proves you so.
- **Needs**, not wants. Real product sense reads the deep need under the stated want — which is why it's downstream of [[Jobs To Be Done]] and [[Voice of the Customer]], not a substitute for them.

I'd add a third element Lenny emphasizes: product sense is **empathy plus creativity**. Empathy to genuinely inhabit the user's situation, creativity to generate solutions that fit it. Neither alone is enough.

## Taste, and the thing beyond data

There's a part of product sense that data can't give you, and I won't pretend otherwise: **taste.** The judgment that *this* interaction feels right and *that* one feels cheap; that this is the elegant version and that's the over-engineered one. Taste is what's left to decide after the data has narrowed the field but before the answer is obvious — and in my experience that gap is where most product decisions actually live. theScore's principle *"balance game sense with data"* names this directly: quant and qual and intuition together, never analysis paralysis, never gut alone. Data tells you what *happened*; product sense tells you what to *do* about it.

> [!warning] The two failure modes
> - **Gut-only.** "I just know what's good." Sometimes true, usually ego, and unfalsifiable — which makes it uncoachable and dangerous at scale. If you can't articulate *why*, you don't have product sense; you have an opinion.
> - **Data-only.** Refuses to move without a number, so it only ever optimizes what already exists and never invents. Data can't tell you what to build next when there's no data on a thing that doesn't exist yet. That's a judgment call, and dodging it isn't rigor — it's abdication.
> Real product sense lives between these, using data to inform a judgment it's still willing to *own*.

## How to train it

Because it's trainable, here's how I actually build it — in myself and the PMs I coach:

> [!tip] Building product sense
> 1. **Make predictions and write them down.** Before a launch or experiment, predict the outcome *and your reasoning*; check yourself after. This is the fastest loop — it turns vague intuition into a calibrated, falsifiable skill. ([[Hypothesis-Driven Development]] is the formal version.)
> 2. **Do teardowns.** Take a product you admire and one you don't, and articulate *why* down to the decision. Borrowed taste becomes your own once you can name the reasons.
> 3. **Increase your at-bats with users.** Product sense is pattern recognition, and patterns come from volume. Lenny's advice is blunt: talk to more users, see more products, ship more things.
> 4. **Seek the disconfirming case.** Hunt for why you might be wrong. The best-judgment PMs are the *least* attached to being right and the most attached to being calibrated.

## A note on AI

Product sense becomes *more* valuable as AI commoditizes the mechanical parts of the job. When AI can draft the spec, run the analysis, and generate ten options, the scarce skill is knowing which option is *right*. Reforge's framing: "the premium on judgment and taste has never been higher." For probabilistic models specifically, product sense extends into a new dimension covered in [[The PM's AI Literacy]] and [[The AI Product Manager]] — knowing what a model can *reliably* do, which is its own kind of taste.
