# Character, Competency, and Craft

When I assess a PM — to hire, to coach, to promote — I sort everything they bring into a spectrum from **hard-to-teach to easy-to-teach.** It's the most practical mental model I have for developing people, because it tells you where to spend coaching energy and, more importantly, what to screen for in hiring that you'll *never* be able to install later. The headline: hire for the things you can't teach, train the things you can, and don't confuse the two.

## The spectrum

This is one of my own frameworks — Skill-Coaching × Effort — and it lays out five layers, hardest to teach on the left, easiest on the right:

| Layer | Examples | How teachable | Where you address it |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Character traits** | Humility, empathy, integrity, growth mindset, decisiveness, resilience, values | Barely — set early, change slowly | **Hire for it.** Screen hard. |
| **Competencies** | Leadership, communication, collaboration, execution, critical thinking, analysis, creativity, strategy | Slowly, with real effort over years | **Coach + experience** ([[The Six Stages of Mastery]]) |
| **Techniques** | User stories, backlog management, research methods, data viz, working with eng, SQL | Months, with practice | **Coach + reps** |
| **Processes** | Scrum, Kanban, OKRs, JTBD, Kano, Design Sprint | Weeks | **Train + a good doc** |
| **Tools** | Jira, Amplitude, Figma, Optimizely, Intercom | Days | **Train. Almost free.** |

Read it right and the strategic point jumps out: **the further left, the more it determines a PM's ceiling, and the less you can do about it after you hire.** Tools and processes are trivially teachable, so they should carry almost no weight in a hiring decision — yet job descriptions are stuffed with them ("must know Jira, Amplitude, Mixpanel"). Character is nearly unteachable and determines everything — yet it's the hardest to assess in an interview, so it gets underweighted. That inversion is most hiring mistakes in one sentence.

## "Competent people with character"

Marty Cagan compresses the whole left side into a phrase I've never improved on: he staffs teams with **"competent people of character."** Two words, both load-bearing.

> [!quote] Cagan's bar
> Competence is necessary — a PM who can't do the job is a liability no matter how lovely. But competence *without* character is worse, because a capable person who lacks integrity, humility, or empathy will use their capability to win arguments, hide problems, and protect themselves over the user. Character isn't a nice-to-have on top of skill. It's the thing that decides whether the skill points the right way.

In my experience the most dangerous hire isn't the under-skilled one — that's visible and fixable. It's the *highly* competent one with a character flaw: the brilliant PM who's quietly contemptuous of engineers, or can't say "I was wrong," or shades the data to look good. Their competence makes the damage bigger and harder to catch.

## What this means in practice

> [!tip] How I use the spectrum
> - **Hiring:** spend your hardest scrutiny on the left. Probe for humility ("tell me about a time you were wrong"), empathy (how they talk about past teams), and growth mindset (what they've learned recently). Assume you can teach Jira. See [[The PM Interview and Hiring Guide]].
> - **Coaching:** target competencies and techniques — the middle, where effort actually moves the needle. You can't coach someone into having character, but you can build their [[Communication]] or [[Fluency with Data]] over a year. That's where [[The Manager as Coach|coaching]] earns its keep.
> - **Don't fake it.** You cannot coach a character flaw away with feedback. If someone's core issue is integrity or humility, no amount of [[Coaching and Feedback]] fixes it — and pretending otherwise wastes everyone's time. That's a hiring miss to correct, not a development plan.

## The myth of the well-rounded résumé

The flip side: don't reject a candidate because they haven't used your stack. A PM of genuine character and strong competencies will learn your tools in a week. A PM with a perfect tool list and a brittle character will sink your team in a quarter. Hire left, train right. And note that "character + competency" sits *beneath* [[The T-Shaped PM and Knowing Your Shape|shape]] — shape tells you *which* competencies someone has; this spectrum tells you which of their gaps you can actually do something about.
