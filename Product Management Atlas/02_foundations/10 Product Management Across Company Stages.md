# Product Management Across Company Stages

The same competencies, the same title, the same person — and a completely different job depending on the size and stage of the company. I've watched brilliant startup PMs stall at an enterprise and seasoned enterprise PMs freeze at a seed-stage company. Neither lost their skill; they failed to re-read the room. Company stage changes *which competencies matter most, how much authority you hold, and what "good" even means.* If you're choosing a job or struggling in one, this is often the missing variable.

## The three stages

> [!note] What the job optimizes for
> - **Startup (seed–Series A, pre-PMF):** survival. Find [[Crafting a Product Vision|product-market fit]] before the money runs out. Speed and learning over process and polish.
> - **Scaleup (Series B–D, post-PMF):** growth and the transition from chaos to system. Make a thing that works work at 100x without breaking it. The hardest stage to PM in.
> - **Enterprise (public / late-stage):** leverage and protection. Move a huge base safely, defend the moat, coordinate across many teams and stakeholders.

## How the job changes, concretely

| Dimension | Startup | Scaleup | Enterprise |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Core risk** | Value (will anyone want it?) | Feasibility & viability at scale | Viability, brand, regulatory |
| **PM count** | 0–2; often the founder | Growing fast; first process | Many; clear ladder |
| **Authority** | High but informal — you just do it | Negotiated; roles forming | Lower per-person; spread across teams |
| **Process** | Almost none, and that's correct | Being invented (the painful part) | Heavy; you work *through* it |
| **Hottest competency** | [[User Research and Discovery\|Discovery]] + [[Product Sense and Judgment\|judgment]] | [[Product Strategy\|Strategy]] + [[Stakeholder Management\|stakeholders]] | [[Managing Up\|Managing up]] + [[Executive Communication\|exec comms]] |
| **Failure mode** | Building what nobody wants | Process that strangles speed, or none that allows scale | Politics; shipping nothing safely |

## The instincts that don't transfer

The danger isn't lack of skill; it's applying the *right* instinct in the *wrong* stage.

> [!warning] The two classic mismatches
> - **Enterprise PM at a startup.** Reaches for process, alignment meetings, and thorough documentation in a place that needs a shipped prototype by Friday. Mistakes rigor for value and dies of slowness. At pre-PMF, [[The 10-50-90 Execution Framework|heavy specs]] are usually premature precision.
> - **Startup PM at an enterprise.** "Just ship it" runs straight into legal, brand, a 40-million-user base, and six dependent teams. Mistakes speed for progress and gets a reputation for recklessness. The blast radius of a bad call is thousands of times larger.

Neither person is wrong about *how to PM*. They're wrong about *where they are*. The fix is humility about stage, not a new skill set.

## The stage shapes the team type too

Stage and [[The Product Organization Matrix|org structure]] interact. Startups are often a single [[Product Teams vs Feature Teams|empowered team]] by default — there's no one to hand down a roadmap, so the team owns the problem whether it wants to or not. Enterprises drift toward [[Product Teams vs Feature Teams|feature teams]] because coordination cost makes top-down roadmapping tempting — and that drift is exactly what the best enterprise product leaders fight. Knowing your stage tells you which gravity you're fighting.

## What this means for your career

If you're deciding where to go next, match the stage to your [[The T-Shaped PM and Knowing Your Shape|shape]]. Spiky on discovery and judgment with high ambiguity tolerance? Startups will love you. Spiky on stakeholder management and execution at scale? You'll thrive at a scaleup or enterprise where a startup would bore you. And know that the senior ladder ([[Director and VP of Product]], [[The Chief Product Officer]]) is a different animal at each stage — a VP at a 30-person startup and a VP at a public company share a title and little else.
