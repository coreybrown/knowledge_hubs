# The Product Organization Matrix

The [[Product Teams vs Feature Teams|product-vs-feature-team]] distinction tells you *how the team is run*. But it leaves out a second variable that changes the PM job just as much: **how much authority you actually have over the decision.** Stack those two axes and you get a 2x2 I keep coming back to — the Product Organization Matrix. It locates your real situation more precisely than any job title, and it explains why two people with "Senior PM" on their badge can be doing completely different jobs.

## The two axes

> [!note] The axes
> - **Team type (horizontal): Outcome ↔ Feature.** Is your team handed problems and outcomes (empowered) or handed a roadmap of features to deliver? This is Cagan's distinction.
> - **PM posture (vertical): Owner ↔ Facilitator.** Are you *accountable for the decision* (owner), or do you *bring the right people together so they decide* (facilitator)? Owners decide and answer for it; facilitators orchestrate and align.

## The four quadrants

| | **Outcome team** | **Feature team** |
|---|---|---|
| **PM as Owner** | **The empowered PM.** You own a problem and the call on how to solve it. The job in full — discovery, [[Understanding Trade-offs\|trade-offs]], outcomes. Cagan's ideal. | **The benevolent dictator.** You own decisions but the team executes a fixed roadmap. Common in [[Product Management Across Company Stages\|early-stage]] founder-led products. Fast, fragile, doesn't scale. |
| **PM as Facilitator** | **The coordinator.** Outcomes are the goal but you align rather than decide — typical when authority sits with a strong design or eng lead, or in a [[Cross-Functional Collaboration\|trio]] of equals. Healthy if the team is senior; a vacuum if it isn't. | **The order-taker.** Fixed roadmap, no decision authority. You're a [[Product Manager vs Adjacent Roles\|Product Owner / project manager]] with a product title. Avoid building a career here. |

The diagonal from bottom-right to top-left is the growth path: order-taker → toward owning either the decision or the outcome → toward owning both. The top-left is where the PM role is most fully itself.

## Owner vs Facilitator is a real choice, not a failing

I want to be careful here, because "facilitator" can sound like the loser quadrant and it isn't. **Facilitation is the right posture sometimes.** When your team is genuinely senior — a staff engineer and a principal designer who've each forgotten more than you know about their craft — the best thing you can do is facilitate: frame the problem, bring the right context, and let the experts decide, stepping in only to own [[The Four Big Product Risks|value and viability]]. Forcing yourself into "owner" of an interaction-design call you're unqualified to make is how PMs lose the room.

The failure mode isn't being a facilitator. It's being a facilitator *by default* — defaulting to consensus because you're avoiding the accountability of a decision. An empowered team with a facilitator who won't own value and viability has a hole at the center of the [[The Product, Design, and Engineering Venn|Venn]].

> [!tip] How to use the matrix
> Locate yourself honestly, then ask what move the situation needs. In *order-taker*, the move is to attach outcome metrics to your work until the team drifts left. In *coordinator*, the move is to make sure *someone* owns value/viability — ideally you. In *benevolent dictator*, the move is to start trusting the team with problems so it survives your absence. The matrix isn't a verdict; it's a compass.

## Why it echoes Cagan

The horizontal axis is Cagan's outcome-vs-feature distinction directly. The vertical axis adds the lived reality that even on an empowered team, *how much you personally decide* varies with your seniority, your team's seniority, and your org's culture. Together they explain a thing titles can't: why the job feels so different from company to company even at the same level. Your title is set by [[The Product Career Ladder|the ladder]]; your actual job is set by your quadrant.
