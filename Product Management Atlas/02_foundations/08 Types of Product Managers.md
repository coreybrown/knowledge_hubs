# Types of Product Managers

"Product manager" is one title covering jobs so different that practitioners often cannot do each other's work. A growth PM optimizing a checkout funnel and a platform PM designing an internal API share a competency model and almost nothing else day-to-day. I think the single biggest mistake early-career PMs make is assuming there is one PM job to master. There is not. There is a core craft, the [[The PM Competency Model|twelve competencies]], expressed through wildly different specializations. Knowing which one you are in, and which one a job posting is *actually* for, saves you years.

## The major specializations

> [!note] The types I'd name
> - **Core or feature PM.** Owns a product area in a customer-facing app, the default mental image of the job. Broadest competency demand.
> - **Growth PM.** Owns a metric (activation, retention, conversion) rather than a feature area. Lives in funnels, [[Experimentation and A-B Testing|experiments]], and [[Product Analytics and Metrics|analytics]]. Heaviest [[Fluency with Data|data]] demand of any type.
> - **Platform or technical PM.** Customers are *internal* teams or *external developers*, and deliverables are APIs, SDKs, and infrastructure. Heaviest feasibility and architecture fluency, lightest on consumer UX.
> - **0-to-1 PM.** Finds product-market fit from nothing. All discovery and judgment, little optimization. Tolerance for ambiguity is the whole job.
> - **Scaling or 1-to-n PM.** Takes a product that works and makes it work at 100x. Optimization, reliability, and [[The Product Organization Matrix|org]] design over invention.
> - **AI PM.** Builds products on probabilistic models. A genuinely different craft, and it gets its own treatment in [[The AI Product Manager]].

These are not mutually exclusive. A growth PM on an AI product at a scaleup is all three at once. They are lenses, not boxes.

## The B2C vs B2B split

Cutting across all of the above is the axis I know best, having spent my career in **B2C mobile** (sports, media, betting):

| | **B2C** | **B2B** |
|---|---|---|
| **User = buyer?** | Usually yes | Usually no; buyer, user, and admin differ |
| **Decision driver** | Emotion, habit, delight | ROI, workflow fit, procurement |
| **Volume** | Millions of low-touch users | Fewer, higher-value accounts |
| **Discovery** | [[Product Analytics and Metrics\|Analytics]] plus broad research | Deep [[User Research and Discovery\|interviews]], sales calls, advisory boards |
| **Killer competency** | [[User Experience Design\|UX]] and retention instinct | [[Stakeholder Management\|Stakeholder]] and viability management |

Here is why this matters. A B2C PM who is brilliant at funnel optimization can flounder in B2B, because the user cannot tell you what they need over an analytics dashboard. You have to go talk to them, and the person who pays is not the person who clicks. Neither is harder. They are different. Do not assume your instincts transfer.

## What stays constant

Underneath every type, the [[The Four Big Product Risks|four risks]] and the [[Understanding Trade-offs|trade-off]] act are invariant. Every PM, in every specialization, is managing value, usability, feasibility, and viability. Only the *weights* change. A platform PM weights feasibility heavily and usability lightly. A growth PM weights value, measured as conversion, above all. The competencies are the same alphabet. The specializations are different words.

> [!warning] The myth of the universal PM
> Job descriptions love "we need a strong generalist PM." Real teams need a *specific* shape for a *specific* problem (see [[The T-Shaped PM and Knowing Your Shape]]). When you interview, figure out which type the role actually is, because the title will not tell you. Ask what the one metric or outcome is that this PM owns. The answer reveals the type faster than any job description.
