# Types of Product Managers

"Product manager" is one title covering jobs so different that practitioners often can't do each other's work. A growth PM optimizing a checkout funnel and a platform PM designing an internal API share a competency model and almost nothing else day-to-day. I think the single biggest mistake early-career PMs make is assuming there's one PM job to master. There isn't. There's a core craft — the [[The PM Competency Model|twelve competencies]] — expressed through wildly different specializations. Knowing which one you're in (and which one a job posting is *actually* for) saves you years.

## The major specializations

> [!note] The types I'd name
> - **Core / feature PM** — owns a product area in a customer-facing app; the default mental image of the job. Broadest competency demand.
> - **Growth PM** — owns a metric (activation, retention, conversion) rather than a feature area. Lives in funnels, [[Experimentation and A-B Testing|experiments]], and [[Product Analytics and Metrics|analytics]]. Heaviest [[Fluency with Data|data]] demand of any type.
> - **Platform / technical PM** — customers are *internal* teams or *external developers*; deliverables are APIs, SDKs, infrastructure. Heaviest feasibility and architecture fluency; lightest on consumer UX.
> - **0-to-1 PM** — finds product-market fit from nothing. All discovery and judgment, little optimization. Tolerance for ambiguity is the whole job.
> - **Scaling / 1-to-n PM** — takes a product that works and makes it work at 100x. Optimization, reliability, [[The Product Organization Matrix|org]] design over invention.
> - **AI PM** — builds products on probabilistic models. A genuinely different craft; gets its own treatment in [[The AI Product Manager]].

These aren't mutually exclusive — a growth PM on an AI product at a scaleup is all three at once. They're lenses, not boxes.

## The B2C vs B2B split

Cutting across all of the above is the axis I know best, having spent my career in **B2C mobile** (sports, media, betting):

| | **B2C** | **B2B** |
|---|---|---|
| **User = buyer?** | Usually yes | Usually no — buyer, user, admin differ |
| **Decision driver** | Emotion, habit, delight | ROI, workflow fit, procurement |
| **Volume** | Millions of low-touch users | Fewer, higher-value accounts |
| **Discovery** | [[Product Analytics and Metrics\|Analytics]] + broad research | Deep [[User Research and Discovery\|interviews]], sales calls, advisory boards |
| **Killer competency** | [[User Experience Design\|UX]] & retention instinct | [[Stakeholder Management\|Stakeholder]] & viability management |

The reason this matters: a B2C PM who's brilliant at funnel optimization can flounder in B2B because the user can't tell you what they need over an analytics dashboard — you have to go talk to them, and the person who pays isn't the person who clicks. Neither is harder; they're different. Don't assume your instincts transfer.

## What stays constant

Underneath every type, the [[The Four Big Product Risks|four risks]] and the [[Understanding Trade-offs|trade-off]] act are invariant. Every PM, in every specialization, is managing value, usability, feasibility, and viability — the *weights* just change. A platform PM weights feasibility heavily and usability lightly; a growth PM weights value (measured as conversion) above all. The competencies are the same alphabet; the specializations are different words.

> [!warning] The myth of the universal PM
> Job descriptions love "we need a strong generalist PM." Real teams need a *specific* shape for a *specific* problem (see [[The T-Shaped PM and Knowing Your Shape]]). When you interview, figure out which type the role actually is — the title won't tell you. Ask: "What's the one metric or outcome this PM owns?" The answer reveals the type faster than any job description.
