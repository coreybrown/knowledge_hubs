# The T-Shaped PM and Knowing Your Shape

The most freeing idea in my career was learning that I did not have to be good at everything. The job description implies a superhuman: strategist, designer, data scientist, diplomat, engineer. Nobody is all of those. The great PMs I know are not well-rounded. They are **spiky**, exceptional at two or three things and merely adequate at the rest, and they are ruthlessly honest about which is which. Knowing your shape, and building a team that covers your flats, beats pretending to be uniformly excellent every time.

## T-shaped: the original idea

The **T-shaped** metaphor comes from IDEO and Tim Brown, with roots in a 1991 *Independent* article. The vertical bar is depth in one discipline, and the horizontal bar is breadth across many. A T-shaped PM has deep expertise in something, say [[Fluency with Data|growth analytics]] or [[User Experience Design|UX]], and enough literacy across engineering, design, strategy, and research to collaborate credibly with specialists. Depth earns you respect. Breadth lets you span the [[The Product, Design, and Engineering Venn|Venn]]. You need both bars. Pure breadth with no depth is the dreaded jack of all trades and master of none, where you can talk to everyone and impress no one.

## Spiky: the more honest version

Ravi Mehta sharpened this into what I think is the truer picture. Most PMs are not T-shaped. They are **spiky**, with multiple peaks and real valleys rather than one tidy vertical bar. His key insight is that great products are built by spiky people on well-rounded teams. You do not fix a valley by grinding it up to average. You fix it by pairing with someone whose spike *is* your valley.

> [!quote] Mehta, on shape
> The goal is not to become uniformly strong across the competency model. The goal is to be exceptional where it counts and to compose a *team* that is well-rounded, even though no individual on it is.

Mehta also names two shapes worth knowing. Product Builders are spiky toward execution and customer insight, and they make things. Product Architects are spiky toward strategy, and they shape direction. Most people lean one way. Neither is better. They are suited to different problems and different [[Types of Product Managers|types]] of PM role.

## How to find your shape

Self-perception is unreliable here, because we overrate our spikes and rationalize our valleys. So triangulate.

> [!tip] Three ways to map your shape
> 1. **Score yourself, then get scored.** Run [[The Competency Self-Assessment]] across the twelve competencies, then have a manager or peer score you blind. The gaps between the two are where your self-image is wrong, usually in both directions.
> 2. **Look at where work feels effortless versus grinding.** Your spikes are the things you do well *without* it feeling like effort. Genuine grind, even after practice, is a flat. Effort spent is a better signal than outcome achieved.
> 3. **Ask what people come to you for.** Your reputation is the market's read on your shape, and it is often more accurate than your own.

## Build the team around the shape

Once you know your shape, the implication is direct, and it changes how you hire ([[Hiring and Building a Product Team]]). Hire and partner for your valleys, not clones of your spikes. A strategy-spiky PM who hires another strategist has built a team that cannot ship. The strongest pods I have run paired a builder-shaped PM with an architect-shaped manager, or a data-spiky PM with a design-spiky lead. Complementarity beats redundancy.

> [!warning] The well-rounded myth
> "Develop into a well-rounded PM" is common career advice, and I think it is mostly wrong. Rounding off your spikes to raise your valleys makes you *less* valuable, not more, because you trade a rare strength for common adequacy. Invest in your spikes until they are undeniable. Invest in your valleys only to the threshold of "won't embarrass me in the room." The exception is a valley that is blocking your level. See [[Identifying Growth Areas]] for when a flat actually has to be raised.
