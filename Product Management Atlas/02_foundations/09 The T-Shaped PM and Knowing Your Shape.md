# The T-Shaped PM and Knowing Your Shape

The most freeing idea in my career was learning that I didn't have to be good at everything. The job description implies a superhuman — strategist, designer, data scientist, diplomat, engineer. Nobody is all of those. The great PMs I know aren't well-rounded; they're **spiky** — exceptional at two or three things and merely adequate at the rest — and they're ruthlessly honest about which is which. Knowing your shape, and building a team that covers your flats, beats pretending to be uniformly excellent every time.

## T-shaped: the original idea

The **T-shaped** metaphor comes from IDEO and Tim Brown (with roots in a 1991 *Independent* article): the vertical bar is **depth** in one discipline, the horizontal bar is **breadth** across many. A T-shaped PM has deep expertise in something — say, [[Fluency with Data|growth analytics]] or [[User Experience Design|UX]] — and enough literacy across engineering, design, strategy, and research to collaborate credibly with specialists. Depth earns you respect; breadth lets you span the [[The Product, Design, and Engineering Venn|Venn]]. You need both bars. Pure breadth with no depth is the dreaded "jack of all trades, master of none" — you can talk to everyone and impress no one.

## Spiky: the more honest version

Ravi Mehta sharpened this into what I think is the truer picture: most PMs aren't T-shaped, they're **spiky** — multiple peaks and real valleys, not one tidy vertical bar. And here's his key insight: **great products are built by spiky people on well-rounded teams.** You don't fix a valley by grinding it up to average. You fix it by pairing with someone whose spike *is* your valley.

> [!quote] Mehta, on shape
> The goal isn't to become uniformly strong across the competency model. The goal is to be exceptional where it counts and to compose a *team* that's well-rounded, even though no individual on it is.

Mehta also names two shapes worth knowing: **Product Builders** (spiky toward execution and customer insight — they make things) and **Product Architects** (spiky toward strategy — they shape direction). Most people lean one way. Neither is better; they're suited to different problems and different [[Types of Product Managers|types]] of PM role.

## How to find your shape

Self-perception is unreliable here — we overrate our spikes and rationalize our valleys. So triangulate.

> [!tip] Three ways to map your shape
> 1. **Score yourself, then get scored.** Run [[The Competency Self-Assessment]] across the twelve competencies, then have a manager or peer score you blind. The gaps between the two are where your self-image is wrong — usually in both directions.
> 2. **Look at where work feels effortless vs grinding.** Your spikes are the things you do well *without* it feeling like effort. Genuine grind, even after practice, is a flat. Effort spent is a better signal than outcome achieved.
> 3. **Ask what people come to you for.** Your reputation is the market's read on your shape, and it's often more accurate than your own.

## Build the team around the shape

Once you know your shape, the implication is direct and it changes how you hire ([[Hiring and Building a Product Team]]): **hire and partner for your valleys, not clones of your spikes.** A strategy-spiky PM who hires another strategist has built a team that can't ship. The strongest pods I've run paired a builder-shaped PM with an architect-shaped manager, or a data-spiky PM with a design-spiky lead. Complementarity beats redundancy.

> [!warning] The well-rounded myth
> "Develop into a well-rounded PM" is common career advice and I think it's mostly wrong. Rounding off your spikes to raise your valleys makes you *less* valuable, not more — you trade a rare strength for common adequacy. Invest in your spikes until they're undeniable; invest in your valleys only to the threshold of "won't embarrass me in the room." The exception is a valley that's blocking your level — see [[Identifying Growth Areas]] for when a flat actually has to be raised.
