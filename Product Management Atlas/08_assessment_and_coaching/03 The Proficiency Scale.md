# The Proficiency Scale

A competency is meaningless without a scale to measure it on. "Good at strategy" is a vibe; "On Track on Strategic Impact, with a clear gap on strategy *definition*" is a coaching plan. This note is the Rosetta Stone for the whole domain — the two scales I use, what each level actually means, and how they line up.

## My point of view: the scale is where most assessment quietly fails

People obsess over the competency *list* and then bolt on a sloppy scale — "1 to 5, you know, use your judgment." That's where calibration dies. If two managers don't mean the same thing by a "3," your numbers are noise dressed as data. I'd rather have a crude four-competency model with a razor-sharp scale than a beautiful twelve-competency one rated on vibes.

## Scale 1 — the 0–2 matrix rubric (the system of record)

[[The Master Competency Matrix]] uses a deliberately coarse three-tier scale. Coarse is a feature: it resists false precision and forces a real call.

| Score | Label | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| **0–0.5** | Needs Focus | Below the bar for the level. A real, named gap to coach against — not a verdict on the person. |
| **1–1.5** | On Track | Meeting the bar for the current level. The healthy default; most scores live here. |
| **2+** | Outperform | Exceeding the level — consistently operating like the level above. This is your promotion signal. |

The genius of three tiers is that "On Track" is the honest center of gravity. A team where everyone is Outperforming everything either has a once-in-a-decade roster or — far more likely — a [[Common Assessment Pitfalls|grading problem]].

## Scale 2 — the 1–5 mastery scale (the lighter tools)

The 9-skill [[The Competency Self-Assessment|Eval Sandbox]] and my [[Score-Gated Leveling|score-gated system]] use a finer five-point scale built on a *progression of independence*:

| Score | Label | The real test |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | Inadequate – Gap | Can't do it yet, even supervised. |
| **2** | Supervised – **Knows** | Does it correctly *with* oversight. |
| **3** | Unsupervised – **Does** | Does it reliably on their own. |
| **4** | Generates – **Masters** | Sets the standard; others learn from how they do it. |
| **5** | Leads – **Teaches** | Defines the practice and grows it in others. |

> [!tip] Knows → Does → Masters → Teaches is the spine
> That four-word ladder is the most portable idea in this domain. *Can you do it watched, alone, better than others, or teach it?* It maps almost one-to-one onto [[The Six Stages of Mastery]] (Gaining Experience → … → Recognized Thought Leader) and gives you a fast gut-check on any skill in seconds.

## How the scales line up

The two scales measure the same reality at different resolutions. Here's the crosswalk I keep in my head:

| 0–2 matrix | 1–5 mastery | Mastery stage |
|---|---|---|
| Needs Focus (0–0.5) | 1–2 / Knows | Gaining Experience |
| On Track (1–1.5) | 3 / Does | Applies Practical Knowledge |
| Outperform (2+) | 4–5 / Masters–Teaches | Advanced → SME and beyond |

> [!warning] Myth: a higher number is always the goal
> No. The target depends on the *level*. An [[Associate Product Manager\|APM]] who "Knows" a skill under supervision is exactly where they should be — pushing them to "Teaches" is malpractice, not ambition. Per-level targets (e.g. the Eval Sandbox totals APM 14 / PM 22 / SPM 30 / LPM 38 out of 45) exist precisely so "good" is defined *for your level*, not in the abstract. See [[Score-Gated Leveling]] and [[What Changes at Each Level]].

> [!note] Why I don't use a single universal scale
> The 0–2 rubric is right for a rich behavioral matrix where nuance lives in the *behaviors*, not the number. The 1–5 scale is right for a self-assessment radar or a summed leveling score where you need range. Forcing one scale onto both jobs is how you get either false precision or false coarseness.
