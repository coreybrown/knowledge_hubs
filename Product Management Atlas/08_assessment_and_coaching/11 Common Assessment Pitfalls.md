# Common Assessment Pitfalls

Every assessment instrument in this domain — the [[The Master Competency Matrix|matrix]], the [[The Competency Self-Assessment|self-assessment]], the [[Score-Gated Leveling|score]] — is only as good as the human running it. And humans are biased in predictable, well-studied ways. This note is the field guide to the traps I've fallen into and watched others fall into, with the countermeasure for each.

## My point of view: name the bias before you score

You don't beat these by being smart or well-intentioned — every bias here operates on people who *believe* they're being fair. You beat them with *process*: pre-committed criteria, first-hand evidence, and calibration. Goodwill is not a control. Structure is.

## The five that wreck PM assessment

> [!warning] 1. Recency bias
> You weight the last month and forget the other eleven. The PM who shipped a win in week 50 looks great; the one who carried Q1 and coasted lately looks weak. **Countermeasure:** keep a running evidence log all year, and at review time deliberately pull examples from *every* quarter ([[Running a Performance Review]]). If all your examples are recent, you haven't done the work.

> [!warning] 2. Halo / horns effect
> One vivid trait colors everything. The charismatic presenter gets rated up on [[Fluency with Data|data]] they're actually weak at (halo); the quiet PM who shipped flawlessly gets rated down across the board because they don't *perform* confidence (horns). **Countermeasure:** score each competency *independently*, against its own behaviors, with its own evidence. Force yourself to justify why a strong communicator is *also* strong at [[Product Quality|quality]] — usually you can't, and the halo dissolves.

> [!warning] 3. Grading to the org's median
> The pull to cluster everyone around "On Track" / "meets expectations" because the extremes feel risky. This destroys the instrument's whole purpose: if nobody is ever Needs Focus and nobody is ever Outperform, your scores carry zero information and your [[Calibration and Promotion|promotion]] signal is gone. **Countermeasure:** demand evidence for the *middle* too. "Why is this a solid On Track and not a Needs Focus?" is a question that breaks the herd toward the center. A flat distribution is a smell, not a sign of fairness.

> [!warning] 4. Confusing activity with impact
> The deadliest one for PMs specifically. You reward the person who shipped the most features, ran the most meetings, wrote the most tickets — and overlook the one who shipped *less* but moved the number that mattered. **Countermeasure:** score against [[Outcomes Over Outputs|outcomes, not outputs]]. The question is never "how busy were they" — it's "what changed in the business because they were here." A [[Outcomes Over Outputs|feature factory]] looks productive on every activity metric and accomplishes nothing.

> [!warning] 5. The self-assessment distortions
> They cut both ways. **Impostors under-rate**, discounting real wins as luck. **Overconfident PMs inflate the strategic competencies** — the ones with the least objective evidence and the most prestige. **Countermeasure:** anchor to evidence, and read the *diff* between self-score and manager-score as the signal, not either number alone ([[The Competency Self-Assessment]]).

## A few more worth naming

> [!note] Quieter traps
> - **Similarity bias** — rating people who work like you higher. Lethal for [[Hiring and Building a Product Team|building a diverse team]]; the [[The T-Shaped PM and Knowing Your Shape|spiky PM]] who covers *your* valley is exactly the one you'll under-rate.
> - **Idiosyncratic rater effect** — your ratings say as much about *you* as about them. The single strongest argument for [[Calibration and Promotion|calibration]].
> - **False precision** — treating a summed [[Score-Gated Leveling|score]] of 64 vs 66 as a real difference. The number is coarser than it looks ([[The Proficiency Scale]]).
> - **Potential-vs-performance conflation** — promoting on "they could be great" instead of "they are already operating at the next level." See [[Calibration and Promotion]].

> [!tip] The meta-countermeasure
> Every trap above is neutralized by the same three habits: **first-hand evidence** (no rumor), **independent per-competency scoring** (no halo), and **calibration** (no private scale). If you only remember one thing: *substantiate every score with a specific example, or don't give it.* That single discipline kills most of these on contact.
