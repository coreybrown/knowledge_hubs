# Assessment and Coaching

This is the domain where the rest of the Atlas stops being a reference and starts being useful. [[The PM Competency Model]] tells you what good looks like. This folder is about the harder question: *where are you against it, and how do you close the gap* — for yourself and, if you manage PMs, for your team.

## My point of view: a competency model that doesn't drive growth is wall art

I have built and run three different assessment instruments for real PM teams — a 12-competency matrix, a lighter 9-skill self-assessment, and a score-gated leveling system. Every one of them was worthless until it produced a *conversation* and a *plan*. A rubric that just ranks people is a performance-management weapon. A rubric that tells someone exactly which two behaviors to grow next quarter is a gift. Same instrument; the difference is entirely in how you wield it.

So this domain is built around a loop, not a scorecard:

> [!example] The assessment loop
> **Assess** (where am I?) → **Identify** (which gaps matter for my next level?) → **Plan** (3 areas, concrete tasks) → **Coach** (the manager's #1 job) → **Calibrate & promote** (was the bar actually met?) → reassess. Skip any step and the loop breaks.

## The instruments I use

I deliberately keep more than one tool, because different jobs need different precision.

| Instrument | Scale | When I reach for it |
|---|---|---|
| [[The Master Competency Matrix]] (12 competencies) | [[The Proficiency Scale\|0–2: Needs Focus / On Track / Outperform]] | Full annual leveling; the system of record |
| [[The Competency Self-Assessment]] (9-skill Eval Sandbox) | [[The Proficiency Scale\|1–5: Knows / Does / Masters / Teaches]] | A lighter, faster self-rating between cycles |
| [[Score-Gated Leveling]] (numeric bands) | 1–5 per skill, summed | When promotion needs a defensible, transparent floor |

> [!warning] Myth: more measurement equals more fairness
> Three instruments is not three times the rigor — it's three times the chance to hide behind a number. I run *one* as the system of record per team and treat the others as lenses. Precision theater is the most common way assessment goes wrong.
