# Building a Growth Plan

This is where assessment turns into action. You've found the two or three gaps that matter ([[Identifying Growth Areas]]); now you build the plan that closes them. I use one structure, it's deliberately small, and it has survived every team I've run it on.

## The structure: 3 growth areas × 3 small tasks

> [!example] The template I use
> Pick your **3 growth areas** (the load-bearing gaps). Under each, define **3 small, concrete tasks** — nine total. Each task is something you can *do* and *finish*, with a way to tell it happened.
>
> | | Task 1 | Task 2 | Task 3 |
> |---|---|---|---|
> | **Growth Area 1** | … | … | … |
> | **Growth Area 2** | … | … | … |
> | **Growth Area 3** | … | … | … |

Three by three is not arbitrary. Three areas is the most a person can hold attention on while doing their actual job. Three tasks each is enough to build a habit without being a second full-time role. Nine items feels small — and small is exactly why it gets *done*, where the ambitious twenty-item plan dies in week two.

## My rules for a task that actually works

The structure is easy; the discipline is in the tasks. A bad task is a vague intention. A good one is concrete, bounded, and *evidence-producing*.

- **It's a behavior, not an outcome you don't control.** "Get promoted" isn't a task. "Lead the spec review for the Q3 launch and get sign-off in one pass" is — and it *demonstrates* the [[Feature Specification|spec]] competency you're growing.
- **It's small enough to finish in weeks, not quarters.** If it's big, it's a project, not a task. Break it down until each piece is a single, schedulable action.
- **It produces evidence.** This is the rule I push hardest. Every task should leave a trace you can point to in a [[Running a Performance Review|review]] — a doc you wrote, a meeting you ran, a metric you moved. No evidence, no growth you can prove.
- **It's tied to your real work, not extra work.** The best growth tasks are reframings of things you were going to do anyway. You have to ship the feature regardless; the *task* is shipping it in the way that builds the gap skill.

> [!tip] Make it outcome-based, not activity-based
> "Read three books on strategy" is activity — and activity is the [[Common Assessment Pitfalls|favorite disguise of fake progress]]. "Write and defend a one-page strategy for my area, and have my manager poke holes in it" is outcome-based: it ends in a thing that either holds up or doesn't. Books are inputs; a defended artifact is evidence. Always prefer the artifact.

## Worked shape (not a real plan — a pattern)

Say your gap is [[Strategic Impact]]. The three tasks might be: (1) write a one-page narrative connecting your area to a company [[OKRs and Goal-Setting|objective]]; (2) present it to your skip-level and capture their pushback; (3) revise the [[Building and Managing a Roadmap|roadmap]] to reflect the strongest objection. Each is small, each leaves a trace, each is real work you'd do anyway — now aimed at the gap.

## Running the plan over time

A plan you write once and never revisit is theater. I review growth plans on the same cadence as 1:1s — light-touch, "where are we on the nine," not a re-litigation. The manager's job here is [[The Manager as Coach|coaching]]: unblock, give [[Giving and Receiving Feedback|feedback]] on the evidence as it lands, and adjust tasks that turned out wrong. When all nine are done, you reassess ([[The Competency Self-Assessment]]) and write the next plan. That loop — assess, plan, do, reassess — *is* deliberate growth. There is no shortcut around the reps.

> [!warning] The plan is the PM's, not the manager's
> If I write the plan *for* someone, they don't own it and it dies. My job is to coach them to write a good one and hold them to it. Ownership is the difference between a development plan and a to-do list someone resents.
