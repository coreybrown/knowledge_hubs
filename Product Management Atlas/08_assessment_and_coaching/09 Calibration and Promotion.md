# Calibration and Promotion

This is where assessment gets political, and where most orgs do it badly. A promotion is the highest-stakes assessment decision you make — it's expensive to reverse, it shapes the culture, and it's watched by everyone. Doing it with rigor instead of vibes is non-negotiable.

## My point of view: promote for the level you're observing, not the one you hope for

The bar is **"already operating at the next level,"** not "ready to try it" or "deserves a reward for tenure." This is the most common promotion mistake I see: promoting someone *into* a level as a bet, then watching them struggle in a role they hadn't yet grown into. A promotion should *recognize* a transition that has already happened, not gamble on one. If you're squinting, the answer is "not yet."

> [!tip] The Outperform signal
> On the [[The Proficiency Scale\|0–2 scale]], the promotion signal is **consistently scoring Outperform (2+) at the current level** in the competencies that define the next one — i.e. already doing the next job, just without the title. One brilliant quarter isn't it. *Consistently* is the word that matters.

## What calibration is, and why it's the antidote

Calibration is the meeting where managers compare ratings *across* their reports to enforce a shared bar. Left alone, every manager grades on their own private scale — generous ones inflate, demanding ones deflate, and a "PM" means something different on every team. Calibration drags those private scales into alignment.

> [!example] How a calibration session runs
> - Managers bring scored assessments ([[The Master Competency Matrix]]) **with evidence**, not adjectives.
> - Promotion candidates are discussed *against the level definition* ([[What Changes at Each Level]]), not against each other's personalities.
> - The room pressure-tests each case: *would this person clear the bar on a different team?* If the case only holds under this one manager, it's not a promotion case — it's a relationship.
> - Borderline cases get named honestly and deferred with a concrete gap, not waved through.

The goal isn't to ration promotions to a quota — that's [[Common Assessment Pitfalls|median-grading]], a real pitfall. The goal is that a level means the *same thing* everywhere.

## Building the case

A promotion case is an argument, and like any argument it needs evidence, not assertion.

- **Show next-level work already happening.** Concrete artifacts: the strategy they wrote, the cross-team initiative they led, the [[Outcomes Over Outputs|outcome]] they owned. "They're ready" is a claim; the artifact is proof.
- **Map it to the level's behaviors.** Walk the next level's column in [[The Master Competency Matrix]] and show evidence against each. Gaps that remain are honest reasons to wait.
- **Use the score as a floor, not the verdict.** In a [[Score-Gated Leveling|score-gated system]], the band and floor rules tell you whether someone is *eligible*. They don't tell you whether to promote — calibrated human judgment does. The number screens; it doesn't decide.
- **Pre-empt the obvious objection.** Every strong case has one weak spot. Name it before the room does, with a plan. This is the [[Giving and Receiving Feedback|prep self-check]] applied to promotion: can I substantiate it, and have I anticipated the pushback?

> [!note] The governance rhythm
> In my model, career-journey conversations *start as someone nears the top of their level* — not at the moment of promotion. Expectations are transparent year-round, the move happens at the yearly review when the bar is genuinely met, and increases are approved up the chain. A promotion should feel *earned and expected*, never like a surprise to the person or a fight in the room. If it's a surprise, the coaching failed somewhere upstream.

> [!warning] Myth: promotion is a reward for hard work
> It isn't. It's a recognition that someone is *already doing a bigger job*. Hard work that doesn't add up to next-level scope earns recognition, a raise, a thank-you — not a level. Conflating effort with level is how you end up with people stuck in roles that don't fit, resentful that loyalty didn't convert to advancement. Reward effort honestly; reserve levels for demonstrated scope.
