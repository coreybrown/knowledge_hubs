# Calibration and Promotion

This is where assessment gets political, and where most orgs do it badly. A promotion is the highest-stakes assessment decision you make. It is expensive to reverse, it shapes the culture, and it is watched by everyone. Doing it with rigor instead of vibes is non-negotiable.

## My point of view: promote for the level you're observing, not the one you hope for

The bar is "already operating at the next level," not "ready to try it" and not "deserves a reward for tenure." This is the most common promotion mistake I see: promoting someone into a level as a bet, then watching them struggle in a role they hadn't yet grown into. A promotion should recognize a transition that has already happened, not gamble on one. If you are squinting, the answer is "not yet."

> [!tip] The Outperform signal
> On the [[The Proficiency Scale\|0-2 scale]], the promotion signal is consistently scoring Outperform (2+) at the current level in the competencies that define the next one. In other words, they are already doing the next job, just without the title. One brilliant quarter isn't it. Consistently is the word that matters.

## What calibration is, and why it's the antidote

Calibration is the meeting where managers compare ratings across their reports to enforce a shared bar. Left alone, every manager grades on their own private scale. The generous ones inflate, the demanding ones deflate, and a "PM" ends up meaning something different on every team. Calibration drags those private scales into alignment.

> [!example] How a calibration session runs
> - Managers bring scored assessments ([[The Master Competency Matrix]]) with evidence, not adjectives.
> - Promotion candidates are discussed against the level definition ([[What Changes at Each Level]]), not against each other's personalities.
> - The room pressure-tests each case by asking whether this person would clear the bar on a different team. If the case only holds under this one manager, it is not a promotion case. It is a relationship.
> - Borderline cases get named honestly and deferred with a concrete gap, not waved through.

The goal is not to ration promotions to a quota. That is [[Common Assessment Pitfalls|median-grading]], a real pitfall. The goal is that a level means the same thing everywhere.

## Building the case

A promotion case is an argument, and like any argument it needs evidence, not assertion.

- **Show next-level work already happening.** Point to concrete artifacts: the strategy they wrote, the cross-team initiative they led, the [[Outcomes Over Outputs|outcome]] they owned. "They're ready" is a claim. The artifact is proof.
- **Map it to the level's behaviors.** Walk the next level's column in [[The Master Competency Matrix]] and show evidence against each one. Gaps that remain are honest reasons to wait.
- **Use the score as a floor, not the verdict.** In a [[Score-Gated Leveling|score-gated system]], the band and floor rules tell you whether someone is eligible. They don't tell you whether to promote. Calibrated human judgment does that. The number screens; it doesn't decide.
- **Pre-empt the obvious objection.** Every strong case has one weak spot. Name it before the room does, with a plan. This is the [[Giving and Receiving Feedback|prep self-check]] applied to promotion: can I substantiate it, and have I anticipated the pushback?

> [!note] The governance rhythm
> In my model, career-journey conversations start as someone nears the top of their level, not at the moment of promotion. Expectations are transparent year-round, the move happens at the yearly review when the bar is genuinely met, and increases are approved up the chain. A promotion should feel earned and expected, never like a surprise to the person or a fight in the room. If it is a surprise, the coaching failed somewhere upstream.

> [!warning] Myth: promotion is a reward for hard work
> It isn't. It is a recognition that someone is already doing a bigger job. Hard work that doesn't add up to next-level scope earns recognition, a raise, a thank-you, but not a level. Conflating effort with level is how you end up with people stuck in roles that don't fit, resentful that loyalty didn't convert to advancement. Reward effort honestly, and reserve levels for demonstrated scope.
