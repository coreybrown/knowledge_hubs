# Product Strategy

> [!quote] The definition I use
> **Product Strategy** — *Deliver business impact via product innovation.*

This is the third of the four competency areas in [[The PM Competency Model]], and it's the one most PMs think they're doing while actually doing something else. Strategy is where execution stops being enough. At the [[Associate Product Manager|APM]] and [[Product Manager|PM]] levels you're judged on whether you can ship; somewhere around [[Senior Product Manager|Senior PM]] the question quietly changes to *whether what you ship matters to the business.* That shift breaks a lot of careers, because the skills that got you here — specs, delivery, quality — are necessary but no longer sufficient.

## My point of view: strategy is deciding what NOT to do

If I could tattoo one sentence on every PM, it's this: **strategy is a set of choices about what you will not do.** A strategy that doesn't kill anything isn't a strategy — it's a wish list with quarters attached.

Most of what gets called "strategy" in our industry is a prioritized list of features. It has a theme slide, an OKR or two stapled on, and a roadmap underneath. It looks like strategy. It is not. A real strategy makes a bet: *we believe this market is moving here, our right to win is this, and therefore we are pouring resources into A and B and deliberately starving C, D, and E.* The features fall out of the bet. The bet does not fall out of the features. Richard Rumelt calls the feature-list version "bad strategy" and he's right — it's a way to avoid the discomfort of choosing.

> [!warning] The myth I fight most
> "We have a strategy" almost always means "we have a roadmap." A roadmap is the *output* of a strategy, not the strategy itself. If you can't state, in one breath, what you're choosing *not* to pursue and why, you have a backlog with ambitions — not a strategy. See [[Crafting a Product Vision]] and [[Building and Managing a Roadmap]] for how the three layers (vision → strategy → roadmap) actually differ.

The second thing I believe: strategy is only real if it survives contact with delivery. A beautiful deck that doesn't change what the team builds on Monday is theater. This is why my model fuses strategy and outcomes — you don't get credit for the idea, you get credit for the number that moved. As Shreyas Doshi puts it, most execution problems are actually strategy problems in disguise.

## Where strategy connects

Strategy doesn't start with a blank page. The raw material comes from [[Customer Insight]] — you can't bet on a market you don't understand, and the sharpest strategic gaps surface through [[Continuous Discovery]] and [[Competitive Landscape Analysis]]. Your bets only become real through [[Product Execution]], which is why [[Outcomes Over Outputs]] sits at the seam between the two domains. And no strategy ships without [[Influencing People]] — a strategy nobody's bought into is a private opinion, so [[Managing Up]] and [[Executive Communication]] are load-bearing here.
